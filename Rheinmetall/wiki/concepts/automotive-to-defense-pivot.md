# The Automotive-to-Defense Pivot

## Summary

Between 2017 and 2025, Rheinmetall transformed from a **dual-segment conglomerate** (defense + automotive components) into a **pure-play defense company**. This was the defining strategic arc of the decade, culminating in the classification of Power Systems (the civilian/automotive division) as discontinued operations under IFRS 5 on 17 December 2025. [Source: AR 2025, p.5]

## Timeline of the Pivot

### 2017-2019: Dual-Segment Status Quo
Rheinmetall operated two roughly equal segments:
- **Defence** -- vehicles, weapons, ammunition, electronics
- **Automotive** -- pistons, engine blocks, exhaust systems, mechatronics (branded as "KSPG" then "Automotive")

The automotive segment generated substantial revenue but lower margins than defense. Revenue mix was approximately 55-60% defense, 40-45% automotive. [Analysis]

### 2020: COVID as Catalyst
The pandemic brutally exposed the automotive segment's vulnerability. While defense revenue proved resilient (government contracts continued), automotive collapsed as global vehicle production halted. The impact on net income was devastating:

- Continuing operations (defense): +284 MioEUR
- Discontinued operations (automotive): **-283 MioEUR**
- Net income: **1 MioEUR**

[Source: AR 2022 p.2, AR 2025 p.3]

This near-zero net income year, despite decent defense performance, likely accelerated internal discussions about divesting the automotive business. The pandemic demonstrated that automotive was not just dilutive to margins -- it could consume the entire defense segment's profits.

### 2021-2022: Defense Surge, Automotive Renamed
The automotive segment was rebranded as **"Power Systems"** to reflect a broadening beyond pure automotive toward industrial and energy applications. However, this repositioning was overtaken by events.

The [Ukraine invasion](ukraine-war-impact.md) in February 2022 created an overwhelming strategic rationale to focus entirely on defense. With European governments committing hundreds of billions to rearmament, Rheinmetall's capacity constraint became the binding issue -- any management bandwidth or capital allocated to automotive was an opportunity cost.

### 2023-2024: Managed Separation
The annual reports from this period show Power Systems increasingly reported separately, with continuing/discontinued operations breakdowns becoming standard. The defense workforce grew while automotive shrank.

### 2025: Formal Divestiture
On 17 December 2025, Power Systems was formally classified as discontinued under IFRS 5. The 2025 annual report structure reflects this: all financial metrics are presented for "continuing activities" (defense only), with Power Systems as a below-the-line item.

The final write-down was -341 MioEUR in discontinued operations for FY2025. [Source: AR 2025 p.3]

## The Financial Case

| Metric | Defense (2025 continuing) | Power Systems (2025 discontinued) |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Revenue contribution | 9,935 MioEUR | Reported below the line |
| Operating profit | 1,841 MioEUR | - |
| Net impact | +1,176 MioEUR | -341 MioEUR |

The math was clear: Power Systems was destroying value while defense was generating extraordinary returns (33.5% ROCE in 2025).

## Strategic Logic

1. **Capital allocation:** Every euro invested in automotive earned lower returns than the same euro invested in defense. With a EUR 63.8bn backlog demanding massive capex, the opportunity cost was stark.

2. **Management focus:** Running two fundamentally different businesses (defense procurement cycles vs. automotive JIT manufacturing) splits executive attention. As defense complexity grew, this became unsustainable.

3. **Market valuation:** Pure-play defense companies trade at higher multiples than conglomerates. The divestiture likely unlocked significant shareholder value. [Analysis]

4. **Automotive structural headwinds:** The European automotive industry faces long-term challenges (EV transition, Chinese competition, emissions regulation). Rheinmetall's automotive products (pistons, engine components) were particularly exposed to ICE phase-out.

## Board Commentary

From the CEO statement in the 2025 annual report (translated):

> "The Board has decided to focus the company's activities on the military business, as the persistent general, structural and cyclical weakness of the automotive industry simultaneously burdens the relevant business of Rheinmetall under Power Systems."

[Source: AR 2025, p.23]
