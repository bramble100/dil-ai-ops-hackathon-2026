### Summary
Wikipedia article about the Cruquius Museum, which occupies a historic steam pumping station in Cruquius, Netherlands. Focuses on the history of draining the Haarlemmermeer.

### Key Facts

**The Museum**
- Occupies the old Cruquius steam pumping station in Cruquius, Netherlands
- Named after Nicolaas Kruik (1678-1754), Dutch land-surveyor (Latinized to "Cruquius")
- Kruik promoted plans to pump the Haarlemmermeer (Haarlem lake) dry
- Location coordinates: 52 deg 20'17"N, 4 deg 38'18"E

**Historical Context**
- The draining of Haarlemmermeer was international news in the 18th century
- Originally planned to use 150 windmills for 3 years
- Successful experiments with steam pumping at Groenendaal park in 1781
- Three steam-driven pumping stations were eventually established
- The Cruquius pumping station was one of three that drained the Haarlemmermeer
- Features the world's largest steam engine at the time of construction

**The Steam Engine**
- Made by Harvey & Co from Hayle, Cornwall, England
- One of the largest ever built
- Used to pump water from the Haarlemmermeer into the Spaarne river
- 9 meter drop in water level visible at the museum

**Heritage Status**
- Rijksmonument (national heritage site) since 1886
- Museum opened in 1933
- Part of the Haarlemmermeer Museum (haarlemmermeermuseum.nl)

### Summary
A comprehensive Wikipedia article covering the entire history of Amsterdam from prehistory to the 21st century. 24 pages with historical illustrations, maps, and paintings.

### Historical Periods Covered

**Origins and Prehistory**
- City origins around 1000 CE
- Inhabitants settled at mouth of Amstel river, began peatland reclamation
- After All Saints' Flood (1170), a dam was built in the Amstel (giving the city its name)
- 1275: Amsterdam granted toll privilege by Count Floris V
- Oldest city name: "Amestelledamme" (dam in the Amstel)
- Oldest documented reference: October 27, 1275

**Middle Ages (1000-1500)**
- First settlement at mouth of Amstel around 1000 CE
- Marshy area called Amestelle gradually reclaimed
- 1306: Gwijde van Henegouwen gave Amsterdam city rights
- 1323: Willem III established toll on Hamburg beer trade
- 1342: "Groot Privilege" awarded, strengthening city's position
- 1345: Miracle of Amsterdam - city became pilgrimage destination
- Great fires of 1421 and 1452 led to stone construction requirements
- Het Houten Huys (Wooden House) at Begijnhof survives from this period

**Religious Strife and Revolt (16th century)**
- Protestant Reform and Mennonite community in Amsterdam
- 1566 rebellion against Spanish Hapsburg rule
- 1578: Alteratie - Amsterdam switched to Protestant/Calvinist side
- Religious tolerance emerged: Catholics worshipped in hidden churches
- Jews from Spain, Portugal, and Huguenots from France found refuge

**The Golden Age (1585-1672)**
- 17th century: Amsterdam's Golden Age
- Dutch East India Company (VOC) founded 1602 - first multinational corporation
- Amsterdam became most important trading hub and financial center
- Amsterdam Stock Exchange founded 1611 - oldest "modern" securities market
- 1613: Expanded around Canals, became largest port in the world
- 625 foreign ports, third largest city in Europe
- Tulip Mania (1630-1637) - first speculative bubble
- Government by regents (Regenten) - closed oligarchy

**Golden Age - Notable Families**
- Regent-patrician dynasties: Boelens Loen, De Graeff, Bicker, Pauw, Hooft
- Cornelis and Andries de Graeff - state-minded (republican) faction leaders
- Johan de Witt - leader of republican faction

**Immigration**
- Fall of Antwerp (1585) brought many merchants to Amsterdam
- By 17th-18th century, immigrants were majority of population
- Germans, Flemish, French Huguenots, Ashkenazi/Sephardic Jews
- Armenian community - significant traders in silk, spices, diamonds
- Sephardic Jews from Portugal welcomed; Portuguese Synagogue opened 1675
- Yiddish influenced Amsterdam dialect of Dutch

**Plague**
- Bubonic plague outbreak 1663-1666
- Jan Hinlopen and wife (Rembrandt's patron) fell victim
- 1664: 24,148 buried in Amsterdam (10%+ of population)
- Tobacco smoke used as prophylactic

**Decline and Modernization (18th-19th century)**
- 18th-19th century decline due to competition from England, France, Prussia
- Franco-Dutch War and Anglo-Dutch Wars had devastating effect
- 1795: Netherlands conquered by France; Batavian Republic (1795-1806)
- 1806: Napoleon installed brother Louis as King of Holland
- 1810: Amsterdam declared third imperial capital (after Paris and Rome)
- Royal Palace of Amsterdam became an imperial palace
- 1813: Liberation from French
- 1815: United Kingdom of the Netherlands established
- 1863: North Sea Canal approved; Amsterdam-Rhine Canal in 1952
- Industrial revolution: diamond industry, shipbuilding, engine factories
- Palingoproer (eel riots) of 1886
- Second Golden Age: Centraal Station (1889), Concertgebouw (1886), Rijksmuseum, Stedelijk Museum built

**20th Century**
- WWI: Netherlands neutral but suffered food scarcity
- 1920: Amsterdam Expansion Act - city grew 4x larger
- 1928 Summer Olympics hosted in Amsterdam
- 1932: Afsluitdijk completed, separating Zuiderzee from North Sea
- WWII: Nazi occupation 1940-1945
  - February Strike of 1941 - unprecedented protest against Jewish persecution
  - Over 100,000 Dutch Jews deported; three-quarters perished in Holocaust
  - Anne Frank and sister Margot among those hiding in the Jewish Quarter
  - Hunger Winter of 1944 - 20,000 died
  - Liberation: May 5, 1945; Canadian forces
- Post-war immigration from Morocco, Suriname, Turkey
- 1992: El Al cargo plane crash in Bijlmermeer - 43 dead

**Social Movements**
- 1960s-70s: Provo movement - anarchist/counterculture
- Amsterdam became "magisch centrum" (magical center) of Europe
- Soft drugs tolerated; city became hippie destination
- Squatter movements in 1970s-80s
- Amsterdam coronation riots (1980)

**21st Century**
- Amsterdam is constitutional capital; The Hague has government
- Tourism boom: 3,000 new hotel rooms 2012-2015, Airbnb added 11,000 accommodations
- Overtourism concerns: real estate surged, comparisons with Venice
- Metro Line 52 (North/Southline) opened July 21, 2018
- Urban renewal: Houthaven, Overhoeks (former Shell area), NDSM, IJburg
- Population: 931,298 in January 2024
- Amsterdam 2040 Structural Vision initiative

**Culture**
- First theater 1638; French theater popular in 18th century
- Opera from 1677; metronome invented here 1812 by Dietrich Nikolaus Winkel
- Rijksmuseum and Stedelijk Museum built end of 19th century
- Concertgebouw (1888) - world-renowned concert hall
- First official same-sex marriage in the world held in Amsterdam
- De Wallen: famous red-light district around Oudezijds Achterburgwal
- Amsterdam is popular setting for Dutch and foreign films

**Geographic History**
- 165 canals with combined length of 75 km (47 mi)
- More canals than Venice and Paris
- Torensluis (built 1648) - oldest bridge in original state
- Grachtengordel (canal ring) took 400 years to build - UNESCO World Heritage
- City built on soft ground; buildings have deep foundations with long poles
- Centraal Station built on 8,687 poles; 11 million poles in the whole city by 2022

**Municipality History**
- Annexed municipalities: Sloten, Buiksloot, Nieuwendam, Ransdorp, Watergraafsmeer (1921)
- Weesperkarspel (Bijlmermeer) in 1966
- Weesp in 2022

### Key Practical Tips Aggregated from All Sources

**Best Time to Visit**
- March-May for tulips (Keukenhof open ~mid-March to mid-May)
- April 27: King's Day (biggest national celebration)
- Summer (June-August) for general sightseeing and outdoor activities
- Year-round for museums

**Transport**
- OV-chipkaart for all public transit
- I Amsterdam Card for museums + transit (1-4 day options, 67-130 EUR)
- Cycling is the primary local transport; rent bikes everywhere
- Trains connect all major Dutch cities efficiently
- Schiphol Airport is the main international gateway

**Money-Saving Tips**
- Book Anne Frank House and Van Gogh Museum tickets well in advance (sell out)
- Free rooftop terrace at NEMO
- Free entry to Vondelpark, Begijnhof, and many churches
- Albert Cuyp Market for cheap local food
- Night buses require separate tickets unless you have a multi-day pass

**Food and Drink Must-Try**
- Stroopwafels (caramel waffles)
- Raw herring (haring) with onions
- Gouda and Edam cheese
- Bitterballen (fried meat balls)
- Poffertjes (mini pancakes)
- Erwtensoep (pea soup)
- Kroket (croquettes)
- Jenever (Dutch gin)
- Dutch pancakes (pannenkoeken)

**Cultural Notes**
- Holland vs. Netherlands: "Holland" technically refers to two provinces (North and South Holland); the country is the Netherlands
- Amsterdam is the constitutional capital; The Hague is the seat of government
- Dutch are extremely direct in communication
- Cycling rules must be respected - stay out of bike lanes
- Tipping is not mandatory but 5-10% is appreciated
- Most places accept cards/contactless payment

**Accommodation Areas**
- Central Station area: convenient for transit
- Museumplein: close to major museums
- Jordaan: charming, quieter neighborhood
- De Pijp: lively, multicultural, near Albert Cuyp Market
