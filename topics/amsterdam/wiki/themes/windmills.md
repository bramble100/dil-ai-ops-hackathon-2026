# Dutch Windmills

## Overview

Windmills are one of the most iconic symbols of the Netherlands. Originally built for practical purposes -- draining water, grinding grain, sawing wood, making paint -- they became cultural icons representing Dutch ingenuity and the centuries-long battle against water. **[Multi-source]**

## Where to See Windmills

### Kinderdijk (UNESCO World Heritage)

[Kinderdijk](../places/kinderdijk.md) has 19 historic windmills -- the largest concentration in the Netherlands. Declared UNESCO World Heritage for their role in water management. Two windmill-museums let you see the mechanics inside. Located south of Rotterdam, about 90 km from Amsterdam. **[Source: viajeros-callejeros]**

### Zaanse Schans

[Zaanse Schans](../places/zaanse-schans.md) is an open-air village with working windmills you can enter. Just 20 km north of Amsterdam (15 min by train). The mills demonstrate various industrial purposes: sawing wood, making paint, pressing oil. Combined with cheese and clog-making demonstrations. **[Multi-source]**

## Windmills and Water

The connection between windmills and water management is central to Dutch identity. Before steam power, windmills were the primary technology for pumping water out of low-lying areas (polders). The [Cruquius Museum](../places/cruquius-museum.md) tells the story of how steam engines eventually replaced windmills for draining the Haarlemmermeer. **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**

## Fun Facts

- At their peak, the Netherlands had over 10,000 windmills **[Unverified]**
- About 1,000 historic windmills survive today **[Unverified]**
- Many are still operational and maintained by volunteer millers
- National Mill Day (Nationale Molendag) is held annually in May, when windmills across the country open to visitors **[Unverified]**
