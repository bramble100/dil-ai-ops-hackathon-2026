# Dutch Water Management

## Overview

The Netherlands' relationship with water defines its national identity. With roughly one-third of the country below sea level, the Dutch have been fighting, managing, and engineering water for centuries. The famous saying goes: "God created the world, but the Dutch created the Netherlands." **[Multi-source]**

## The Haarlemmermeer Story

The draining of the Haarlemmermeer (Haarlem Lake) is one of the great feats of Dutch water engineering:

- **The problem**: A large inland lake that kept growing, threatening Amsterdam and surrounding towns
- **Early plans**: As early as 1678, Nicolaas Kruik (Latinized: Cruquius) surveyed the lake and promoted drainage plans **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**
- **International attention**: By 1751, the Virginia Gazette was reporting on Dutch plans to drain the lake with 150 mills **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**
- **Steam solution**: After successful steam pumping experiments at Groenendaal park (1781), three steam-driven pumping stations were built: Leeghwater, Lynden, and Cruquius **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**
- **The draining**: Dike built in 1840s. Pumping began 1850. Lake drained in just 3 years -- exactly as predicted a century earlier **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**
- **The result**: The Haarlemmermeer polder is now home to Schiphol Airport, one of Europe's busiest

## Cruquius Museum

The [Cruquius Museum](../places/cruquius-museum.md) preserves the historic Cruquius pumping station. Its engine, built by Harvey & Co of Hayle, Cornwall, is thought to be the **largest steam engine ever built** -- certainly the largest beam engine. The compound engine has an inner HP cylinder of 84 inches (2.1m) and an outer LP annular cylinder of 144 inches (3.7m). **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**

The museum operated as a pumping station until 1933, then became a museum. In December 2025, a new film room opened with 7 short films on the water history of Haarlemmermeer. As of March 31, 2026, the museum is fully accessible again after 2+ years of bridge construction work. **[Source: cruquius-nieuws]**

## The Stelling van Amsterdam

The area around [Cruquius](../places/cruquius-museum.md) is part of the Stelling van Amsterdam (Defence Line of Amsterdam), a ring of fortifications and water management infrastructure with UNESCO World Heritage status. Nearby Fort Vijfhuizen and Fort Cruquius are both part of this network. **[Source: cruquius-wikipedia]**

## Key Concepts

- **Polder**: Low-lying land reclaimed from water and protected by dikes
- **Dike (dijk)**: Embankment built to hold back water
- **Ringvaart**: Ring canal surrounding a polder
- **Gemaal**: Pumping station used to drain water from polders
- **Waterschappen**: Regional water boards -- some of the oldest democratic institutions in the world
