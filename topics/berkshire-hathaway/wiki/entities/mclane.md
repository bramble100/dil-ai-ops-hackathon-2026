## Overview

McLane Company is one of the largest wholesale distributors in the United States with $23B in annual revenues, acquired by Berkshire from Wal-Mart in May 2003. The company distributes groceries and nonfood items to convenience stores, mass merchandisers, quick-service restaurants, and movie theaters.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** May 23, 2003
- **Parent (prior):** Wal-Mart Stores, Inc.
- **Revenue:** $23B annually
- **Pre-tax margins:** ~1% (razor-thin but high capital velocity)
- **Business:** Wholesale distribution — grocery, nonfood items, cigarettes
- **Key Customers:** Convenience stores, drug stores, fast food chains, movie theaters, Wal-Mart competitors
- **Deal Mechanics:** Single 2-hour meeting with Wal-Mart CFO Tom Schoewe, handshake, money wired in 29 days, no due diligence. "We knew everything would be exactly as Wal-Mart said it would be — and it was"
- **Goldman Sachs:** Byron Trott was instrumental — now involved in three Berkshire acquisitions (also Larson-Juhl, Justin Industries)

## Significance

McLane demonstrates the Berkshire acquisition advantage at its purest: a $23B revenue business changed hands in 29 days with no lawyers, no due diligence teams, and no post-closing adjustments. Wal-Mart sold because McLane's wholesale business (selling to Wal-Mart competitors like Target) created conflicts, not because of any performance issues.

The 1% margin business is characteristic of Berkshire's willingness to own high-volume, low-margin operations that require scale advantages and operational excellence — similar to GEICO's insurance model. McLane's distribution capabilities create value through volume and efficiency rather than pricing power.
