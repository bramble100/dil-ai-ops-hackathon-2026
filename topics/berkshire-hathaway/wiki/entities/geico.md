## Overview

Government Employees Insurance Company — a low-cost auto insurer with a structural cost advantage over competitors whose marketing structures prevent adaptation. Berkshire's largest non-controlled holding throughout 1977-1981, and the single most important investment in demonstrating the "wonderful business at a fair price" philosophy.

## Key Facts

- **Business model:** Direct-to-consumer auto insurance, bypassing the independent agent system
- **Moat:** Structural cost advantage from direct marketing; competitors' agency systems make imitation nearly impossible
- **Near-death (1976):** Came to the edge of bankruptcy due to operational and financial mismanagement — but the underlying franchise was intact
- **Turnaround CEO:** Jack Byrne, who provided "managerial brilliance in abundance" [1980 Letter]
- **Berkshire ownership:** ~33% equity interest (7.2M shares); voting rights held by independent party per DC and NY insurance regulators
- **Cost basis:** $47M ($28M invested ~1976, remainder ~1980)
- **Earning power (1980):** ~$20M Berkshire's share; only ~$3M reported as dividends
- **Market value (1981):** $199.8M

## Significance

GEICO is the cornerstone case study for multiple Berkshire principles:

- **Not a true turnaround:** "Extraordinary business franchise with a localized excisable cancer" — analogous to American Express after the 1964 salad oil scandal. The economic engine was always excellent; only the management and financial structure needed repair[^1].
- **Owner earnings in action:** $47M cost buys $20M annual earning power, but GAAP reports only ~$3M (dividends). To acquire equivalent earning power outright would cost $200M minimum[^2].
- **Share buybacks:** GEICO reduced share equivalents from 34.2M to 21.6M in two years, dramatically enhancing per-share value.
- **Minority ownership preferred:** Buffett couldn't vote the shares and didn't control management — and was perfectly content, because Jack Byrne was better at running GEICO than Buffett would have been.
