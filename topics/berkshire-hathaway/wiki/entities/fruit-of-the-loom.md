## Overview

Fruit of the Loom is America's dominant basic apparel brand, acquired by Berkshire out of bankruptcy in 2002. The company commands extraordinary market share in mass-market underwear and has been revitalized under CEO John Holland with manufacturing largely relocated offshore to maintain cost competitiveness.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** 2002 (agreed 2001; acquisition from bankruptcy completed April 2002)
- **CEO:** John Holland (former Warnaco/Fruit CEO)
- **Purchase Price:** ~$835M (cash, out of Chapter 11)
- **Market Share (2003):** 42.3% of men's and boys' underwear at mass marketers; women's and girls' share grew from 11.3% to 13.9%
- **Operations:** Majority of manufacturing moved offshore; domestic employment reduced, but productivity gains and brand dominance maintained
- **Prior History:** Founded 1851; went bankrupt under excessive debt and mismanagement by prior owners

## Significance

Fruit of the Loom validates Buffett's distinction between a bad business and a good business badly managed. The brand itself — commanding 42% market share in a category where consumers buy by brand name — is exactly the kind of pricing power Buffett values. The bankruptcy was caused by financial leverage and management failures, not by any erosion of the brand's consumer franchise.

John Holland's turnaround (he had previously run the company under its former owners) demonstrates that operational excellence can be restored when the capital structure is fixed. Berkshire's acquisition out of bankruptcy at ~$835M for a business that generates significant cash flow illustrates the value of being a buyer-of-last-resort when financial distress creates motivated sellers.
