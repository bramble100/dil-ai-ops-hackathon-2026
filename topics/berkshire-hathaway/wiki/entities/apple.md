## Overview

Apple Inc. is Berkshire Hathaway's largest equity holding and one of the "Four Giants" that define the company's post-2017 identity. Originally identified by Todd Combs and Ted Weschler, the position grew to become the most valuable single investment in Berkshire's history — demonstrating how patience and buyback compounding can transform a partial ownership stake into an enormous asset.

## Key Facts

- **Initial purchase:** ~2016-2017 (position disclosed in 2017 letter at **$28.2B**)
- **Cost basis:** ~$31B (as of 2025)
- **Market value:** ~$62B (2025), having been reduced through sales
- **Ownership:** Rose from ~5.2% to ~5.55% through Apple's own buybacks (costless to Berkshire)
- **Dividends:** Part of four-stock US core yielding $1.7B combined annually
- **Role:** Replaced [IBM](ibm.md) as largest equity holding in 2017

## Significance

**[2017 Letter]** Apple at **$28.2B** replaces IBM as Berkshire's largest equity position. The shift from IBM to Apple represents a quiet evolution in Buffett's technology thesis — from IBM's declining services business to Apple's consumer brand moat and ecosystem lock-in[^1].

**[2018-2019 Letters]** Named one of the "five groves" framework's key assets. The buyback compounding dynamic becomes central: Apple's aggressive repurchase program increases Berkshire's ownership percentage at zero cost — from 5.2% to 5.4%. Combined with Berkshire's own buybacks, shareholders own **10% more Apple** than in July 2018. Mae West: "Too much of a good thing can be... wonderful"[^2][^3].

**[2020 Letter]** Apple named one of the **"four family jewels"** alongside P/C insurance, BNSF, and BHE. Berkshire's Apple stake is now one of the "four businesses that account for a large part of Berkshire's value." Apple's continued buybacks push ownership from 5.4% to higher levels[^4].

**[2021 Letter]** Elevated to the **"Four Giants"** framework: (1) insurance float, (2) **Apple (5.55%)**, (3) BNSF, (4) BHE. Apple is the only non-controlled business in the top four — a testament to Buffett's conviction that a non-controlling position in a wonderful business can be more profitable than full ownership of a good one[^5].

**[2022 Letter]** The "Secret Sauce" comparison: Apple joins [Coca-Cola](coca-cola.md) and [American Express](american-express.md) in demonstrating that businesses with truly durable moats compound for decades. Berkshire is the **largest owner of 8 S&P 500 companies** — Apple chief among them[^6].

**[2024-2025 Letters]** Part of the four-stock US equity core: Apple ($62B), AmEx ($56B), Coke ($28B), Moody's ($12.6B) — totaling **$158.6B on $9.1B cost** basis. "Household names" that Berkshire has "no intent to sell." Marketable equities as filter application: these companies "earn very high returns on the net tangible equity required for their operations" and were accumulated when they "very occasionally sell at bargain prices"[^7][^8].
