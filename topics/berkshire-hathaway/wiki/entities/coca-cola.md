## Overview

The world's most ubiquitous consumer product company. Berkshire's largest common stock investment beginning in 1988 — and destined to become its most famous. The purchase marked the definitive shift from Graham-era statistical bargains to Munger-influenced franchise investing.

## Key Facts

- **Initial purchase:** Summer 1988; 14,172,500 shares at $592.5M cost
- **1989 increase:** Holdings grew to 23,350,000 shares at $1,024M total cost
- **Market value (yearend 1989):** $1,804M — 76% gain on cost
- **CEO:** Roberto Goizueta (since 1981); Don Keough (President, former Buffett neighbor in Omaha)
- **Moat:** "The world's most ubiquitous product" with explosive overseas growth under Goizueta/Keough

## The 52-Year Delay

**[1989 Letter]** Buffett's self-deprecating investment narrative:

> "Of a certainty, it was in 1936 that I started buying Cokes at the rate of six for 25 cents from Buffett & Son, the family grocery store, to sell around the neighborhood for 5 cents each. In this excursion into high-margin retailing, I duly observed the extraordinary consumer attractiveness and commercial possibilities of the product."

He continued observing these qualities for 52 years "as Coke blanketed the world" — while allocating capital to "street railway companies, windmill manufacturers, anthracite producers, textile businesses, trading-stamp issuers, and the like." Only in the summer of 1988 did his "brain finally establish contact with his eyes."

## Significance

### Why Buffett Bought

**[1989 Letter]** Two catalysts converged: (1) Roberto Goizueta's 1981 transformation — refocusing the company and energizing international growth; (2) the price finally became reasonable relative to the business quality. Goizueta possessed a "truly rare blend of marketing and financial skills" — maximizing both product growth and shareholder returns. "Normally, the CEO of a consumer products company will cause either marketing or finance to dominate at the expense of the other discipline."

### The Franchise in Action

Coca-Cola exemplifies the [business quality](../principles/business-quality.md) archetype: a consumer franchise with global pricing power, minimal capital requirements relative to earnings, and a product whose competitive position strengthens with scale. The brand moat is self-reinforcing — ubiquity breeds familiarity, which breeds preference.

### Favorite Holding Period

**[1988 Letter]** Coca-Cola and Freddie Mac were purchased with the expectation of permanent ownership: "When we own portions of outstanding businesses with outstanding managements, our favorite holding period is forever."

## 1990-1992 Performance

**[1990 Letter]** Market value: $2,171.6M (cost $1,023.9M) — a 112% gain in two years. Holdings remain 46,700,000 shares. Look-through earnings contribution: $58M from Berkshire's 7.0% share of undistributed Coke earnings.

**[1991 Letter]** The defining moment in the Coke investment: P/E expansion drove Coke's stock far faster than its earnings in 1991. Coke alone contributed nearly $1B to Berkshire's $2.1B net worth increase — "a phenomenon not apt to be repeated." Market value: $3,747.7M. Look-through contribution: $69M. Buffett notes ruefully that by 1991, "our Coke stock alone is worth more than Berkshire's entire net worth was when we bought it" ($3.4B at purchase vs. $3.75B at yearend 1991). "Coca-Cola and Gillette are two of the best companies in the world and we expect their earnings to grow at hefty rates in the years ahead."[^3]

**[1992 Letter]** Market value: $3,911.1M (cost $1,023.9M) — 282% gain on cost. Holdings now 93,400,000 shares (post-split). Look-through earnings contribution: $82M on 7.1% ownership. Total look-through from Coke alone has now exceeded the original cost basis on an annual basis. Coke and Gillette together account for roughly $1.3B in market value appreciation since purchase[^4].
