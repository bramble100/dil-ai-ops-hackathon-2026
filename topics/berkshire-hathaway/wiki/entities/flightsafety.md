## Overview

The world's leading provider of professional aviation training, acquired by Berkshire in December 1996 for approximately $1.5B (cash plus fractional Berkshire shares). Founded in 1951 by Al Ueltschi, who was still running the company at age 79 when the deal closed.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** December 1996 for ~$1.5B (cash + fractional BRK shares)
- **Founder/CEO:** Al Ueltschi (age 79 at acquisition); started the company with $10,000
- **Operations:** 41 learning centers, 175 simulators, $700M annual revenues
- **Market position:** Dominant in professional flight simulation training; trains pilots for corporate aviation, airlines, and military
- **Deal origin:** Buffett cold-called Ueltschi after an NYSE Annual Dinner seatmate mentioned the company; deal closed within weeks

## Significance

**[1996 Letter]** FlightSafety represents the "phone call" acquisition advantage — Berkshire's reputation draws founders who want permanent homes for their businesses. Ueltschi, like the Blumkins, Heldmans, and Bill Child, sought a buyer who would preserve the culture and autonomy he had built over 45 years.

The acquisition demonstrates three capital allocation principles: (1) reputation as deal-flow generator — Ueltschi came to Buffett, not the reverse; (2) cash-heavy structure minimizes dilution; (3) Buffett does not need technical expertise in flight simulation — the four filters are satisfied by the economics (high returns, captive customer base, recurring revenue) and management (founder still actively running at 79).

> "Al Ueltschi had a simple conviction: if you want to become a good pilot, you should train in a simulator rather than risk your neck in an airplane." — 1996 Letter (paraphrase)
