## Overview

General Re Corporation, acquired by Berkshire Hathaway on December 21, 1998, was the largest US property-casualty reinsurer. The acquisition — Berkshire's largest ever at the time — was paid entirely in Berkshire stock, inflating the reported 1998 book value gain. General Re also included 82% ownership of Cologne Re, Germany's largest reinsurer. The integration proved difficult: 1999 underwriting losses of $1.4B were among the most painful events in Berkshire's history.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** December 21, 1998
- **Consideration:** ~$22B in Berkshire stock (entire consideration in stock — Buffett acknowledges this diluted per-share intrinsic value despite the headline 48.3% book value gain)
- **CEO:** Ron Ferguson (remained post-acquisition)
- **Business:** Largest US P&C reinsurer; ~$7B annual revenues; global operations
- **Cologne Re:** General Re owned 82% of Cologne Re, Germany's largest reinsurer
- **Float contribution:** $15.3B (General Re's share of Berkshire's $22.7B total float at yearend 1998)

## Structural Rationale

Buffett's rationale for acquiring General Re rests on a competitive asymmetry:

> "Reinsurers face Wall Street pressure to smooth earnings and limit catastrophe exposure. Their public-company status forces them to turn away the most volatile policies — exactly the policies Berkshire is positioned to write."

Berkshire's willingness to absorb earnings volatility allows it to accept business that competitors structurally cannot. The acquisition was also attractive because General Re was the established market leader — not a turnaround.

## 1999 Crisis

[1999 Letter] The integration revealed serious problems: General Re's reserves from prior soft-market years were inadequate, and pricing discipline had eroded. 1999 pre-tax underwriting loss: **$1.4B**. Float cost jumped to ~5.8%, far above the T-bond yield.

Buffett was explicit in the 1999 letter: _"I have not kept the promise I made at the time of the acquisition, and I apologize."_ Ron Ferguson remained; Berkshire began restructuring reserve adequacy and repricing discipline.

## 2000: Repricing Underway

**[2000 Letter]** Float reaches $15.5B (General Re's share of Berkshire's $27.9B total). Berkshire's overall float cost was 6% in 2000 (underwriting loss $1.6B), largely driven by General Re. Ron Ferguson, Joe Brandon, and Tad Montross "took many actions during 2000 to bring that company's profitability back to past standards." Pricing not yet fully corrected but significantly improved. "If there's no mega-catastrophe in 2001, General Re's float cost should fall materially."

The retroactive reinsurance line complicates comparisons: Berkshire's overall underwriting loss in 2000 includes $482M from "pain-today, gain-tomorrow" policies (versus $400M in 1999) — these represent first-year losses on policies with no ongoing cost, whose overall economics are favorable.

## 2001: Full Reckoning

**[2001 Letter]** General Re's pre-tax underwriting loss in 2001: **$4.3B** (vs. $1.4B in 1999 and $1.4B in 2000). The vast majority is attributable to two causes:

1. **September 11**: Berkshire absorbed $2B-$2.5B in 9/11 losses, concentrated in General Re
2. **Loss development (honest mistake)**: $800M of underwriting losses from prior years that were not recognized when they occurred — belatedly charged to 2001 earnings

"The mistake was an honest one, I can assure you of that. Nevertheless, for several years, this underreserving caused us to believe that our costs were much lower than they truly were, an error that contributed to woefully inadequate pricing."

General Re broke all three underwriting principles: (1) wrong circle of competence (terrorism risk was excluded from pricing models); (2) aggregation failure (did not search for correlation in terrorism exposure); (3) incorrect reserving.

**Leadership change:** Joe Brandon appointed CEO in September 2001. Buffett: "Joe has many of Jack [Welch]'s characteristics: smart, energetic, hands-on, expects much of both himself and his organization."

Float: $19.3B (General Re's share of Berkshire's $35.5B total). "General Re should be a huge asset for Berkshire. I predict that Joe and Tad will make it so."
