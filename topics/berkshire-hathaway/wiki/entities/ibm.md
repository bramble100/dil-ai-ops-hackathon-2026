## Overview

International Business Machines (IBM) became one of Berkshire's Big Four equity holdings in 2011, when Buffett invested $10.9B for 63.9 million shares (5.5% ownership). This was a dramatic departure from Buffett's historical avoidance of technology companies, justified by IBM's transformation into a services and software business with predictable recurring revenue and aggressive share repurchase programs.

## Key Facts

- **Initial investment:** $10.9B for 63.9M shares (2011)
- **Ownership 2011:** 5.5%
- **Ownership 2012:** 6.0%
- **Big Four position:** Joins American Express, Coca-Cola, and Wells Fargo as a permanent pillar

## Significance

### The Repurchase Math Essay

IBM is the vehicle for Buffett's most detailed mathematical analysis of share repurchases from the **shareholder's perspective**. The counterintuitive insight: existing shareholders should hope for a _lower_ stock price during repurchase periods, because more shares are retired per dollar spent.

Buffett constructs a five-year scenario: if IBM earnings grow 20% and shares outstanding decline 20% through repurchases, Berkshire's per-share earnings claim rises dramatically — but only if the stock stays low during the buyback period. "In the end, the math is simple: If the business does well over time, so will the stock." This extends the Mr. Market framework into the era of massive corporate buybacks[^1].

### Big Four Look-Through

**[2011 Letter]** The Big Four (AmEx 13.0%, Coke 8.8%, IBM 5.5%, Wells Fargo 7.6%) generate $862M in dividends but $3.3B in Berkshire's share of retained earnings — $2.4B annually in unreported value creation.

**[2012 Letter]** IBM ownership rises to 6.0%. Big Four earnings share: $3.9B total; dividends received: $1.1B; $26.7B unrealized gains across all four.

**DIRECTV** — the first $1B+ holding in the Todd Combs/Ted Weschler portfolios — appears in the 2012 equity list, signaling the investment successors' growing mandate[^2].
