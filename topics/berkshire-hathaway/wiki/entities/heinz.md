## Overview

H.J. Heinz Company is a global food manufacturer acquired in June 2013 through a partnership between Berkshire Hathaway and 3G Capital, led by Jorge Paulo Lemann. The deal introduced a new "partnership template" for Berkshire — a co-investment structure where Berkshire provides financing and 3G manages operations.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** June 2013
- **Total deal value:** ~$23B (including debt assumption)
- **Berkshire investment:** $8B in preferred stock (9% coupon, estimated ~12% annual return with other features) + $4.25B for half the common equity
- **3G Capital investment:** $4.25B for the other half of common equity
- **Key people:** Jorge Paulo Lemann (3G founder, Buffett friend), Bernardo Hees (new CEO), Alex Behring (Chairman)
- **Business:** Iconic food brands; ketchup, sauces, meals, snacks, infant nutrition
- **Employees added:** Heinz contributed to the 42,283 employee increase, bringing Berkshire total to 330,745

## Significance

### The 3G Partnership Template

Heinz represents a structural innovation in Berkshire's acquisition approach. Rather than operating the business directly (Berkshire's traditional model), Berkshire serves as the **financing partner** while 3G's management team runs operations. The partnership has several distinctive features:

- **Permanent ownership:** "Berkshire never intends to sell a share of the company" — unlike private equity, there is no exit timeline
- **Scalable model:** Buffett explicitly states this "partnership template... may be used by Berkshire in future acquisitions of size"
- **Preferred + common structure:** The $8B preferred at 9% (with features yielding ~12%) provides downside protection and current income; the common provides upside participation

### Earnings Impact

Only minor 2013 earnings reflected at Berkshire due to $1.3B in one-time purchase and restructuring charges. Buffett expects substantial 2014 earnings. Early operating results described as "encouraging."

### Fortune 500 Count

With Heinz, Berkshire owns 8½ companies that would individually qualify for the Fortune 500. "Only 491½ to go."

### 2025: "Disappointing... Well Short of Adequate Return"

**[2025 Letter]** Abel delivers a blunt assessment: Kraft Heinz has been **"disappointing"** and delivered results **"well short of an adequate return"** on Berkshire's investment, even including the preferred stock's income. This is the most negative language used about any Berkshire holding in the 2025 letter.

The candid admission contrasts sharply with the 2013 letter's optimism ("will be prospering a century from now") and underscores a recurring Buffett/Abel theme: the willingness to publicly acknowledge investment mistakes. KHC joins the ranks of PCC, Dexter Shoe, and Energy Future Holdings as major investments that fell short of expectations[^2].
