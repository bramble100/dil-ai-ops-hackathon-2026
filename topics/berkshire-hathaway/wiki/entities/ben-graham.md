## Overview

Benjamin Graham (1894-1976) is the intellectual foundation upon which Buffett built his entire investment philosophy — and, by extension, Berkshire Hathaway. Author of _The Intelligent Investor_ (1949) and _Security Analysis_ (1934), and Buffett's professor at Columbia Business School, Graham created the frameworks of Mr. Market, margin of safety, and intrinsic value that permeate every page of the shareholder letters.

## Key Facts

- **Relationship:** Buffett's professor at Columbia; employer at Graham-Newman Corp
- **Key works:** _The Intelligent Investor_ (1949), _Security Analysis_ (1934)
- **Core concepts:** Mr. Market, margin of safety, intrinsic value, "voting machine vs. weighing machine"
- **Direct Berkshire impact:** Graham's unnamed "bonanza investment" in _The Intelligent Investor_ was GEICO — leading to Buffett's 1951 Saturday visit and the 45-year arc that culminated in full ownership

## Significance

**[1987 Letter]** The **Mr. Market parable** — Graham's allegorical business partner who offers a daily price driven by emotion, not value — receives its fullest treatment in any Buffett letter. Written months after Black Monday, the timing gives Graham's insight the force of lived experience. "In the short run, the market is a voting machine but in the long run it is a weighing machine"[^1].

**[1988 Letter]** Graham-Newman's documented track record (20%+ annually over decades) forms the backbone of Buffett's most rigorous attack on Efficient Market Theory. The cumulative proof: $1,000 compounded at Graham's returns would have grown to ~$97M vs. ~$405K at market rates[^2].

**[1992 Letter]** The growth-value synthesis: "In our opinion, the two approaches are joined at the hip: Growth is always a component in the calculation of value." Buffett credits Graham with establishing DCF as the universal valuation method — whether applied to growth stocks, net-nets, or bonds. "Value investing is a redundancy"[^3].

**[1993-1994 Letters]** Graham's five real risk factors replace academic beta: (1) certainty of long-term economics, (2) certainty of management competence, (3) management integrity, (4) purchase price, (5) taxation/inflation. Thirty years of macro shocks "made not the slightest dent in Ben Graham's investment principles"[^4].

**[1997 Letter]** The margin of safety warning — Buffett's most serious overvaluation signal — directly invokes Graham: "Today's price levels... have materially eroded the 'margin of safety' that Ben Graham identified as the cornerstone of intelligent investing"[^5].

**[2013 Letter]** Buffett's definitive Graham tribute: "My financial life changed" when he purchased _The Intelligent Investor_ in 1949. Two connections to Berkshire traced:

1. Graham's unnamed bonanza investment in the book was **[GEICO](geico.md)** — "If Ben had not recognized the special qualities of GEICO when it was still in its infancy, my future and Berkshire's would have been far different"
2. Graham recommended **Northern Pacific** at $17/share — the predecessor to [BNSF](bnsf.md), which now earns $40M every four days

A single sentence in a book → GEICO → BNSF → ~$250B of Berkshire value[^6].

**[2014 Letter]** Graham's Shakespeare quote applied to market psychology: "The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars, but in ourselves." Volatility ≠ risk proven across 50 years of Berkshire data — Graham's original insight validated by the longest track record in investing history[^7].

**[2016 Letter]** The **Graham weighing machine** vindicated by The Bet: Protegé Partners' hedge-fund-of-funds trails the unmanaged Vanguard S&P 500 index fund 85.4% vs. 22.0% over nine years. Graham's insight that fees destroy returns for investors as a group, proven at scale[^8].
