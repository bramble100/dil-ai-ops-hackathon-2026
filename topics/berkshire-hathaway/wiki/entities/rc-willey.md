## Overview

Utah's dominant home furnishings retailer, acquired by Berkshire in 1995 via stock exchange. Bill Child grew the business from $250K in annual sales (1954) to $257M (1995), capturing over 50% of the Utah market — while remaining closed on Sundays.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** 1995 via Berkshire stock exchange
- **CEO:** Bill Child (took over from father-in-law R.C. Willey in 1954)
- **Sales:** $257M (1995); >50% Utah market share in home furnishings
- **Deal origin:** Irv Blumkin (Nebraska Furniture Mart) introduced Child to Buffett
- **Distinctive feature:** Closed on Sundays (Child's religious conviction); competitors open 7 days yet cannot match R.C. Willey's results

## Significance

**[1995 Letter]** R.C. Willey demonstrates that exceptional management can overcome structural disadvantages — closing one day per week in retail would normally be suicidal, yet Child achieved dominant market share through superior execution. The Irv Blumkin introduction illustrates the network effect of Berkshire's existing manager relationships: satisfied owner-operators refer others.

The >50% market share in Utah, achieved while handicapped by Sunday closures, is Buffett's proof that management quality can create what amounts to a local franchise even in the commodity retailing business.

## Boise and Las Vegas Expansion (2001)

**[2001 Letter]** Bill Child expanded R.C. Willey beyond Utah with the same closed-on-Sunday policy:

- **Boise, Idaho**: Bill Child built the store himself for $11M and offered to sell it to Berkshire at cost if it succeeded. Buffett was skeptical. The store "immediately became a huge success."
- **Henderson, Nevada** (adjacent to Las Vegas): Buffett was "even more skeptical." The result: "This store outsells all others in the R.C. Willey chain, doing a volume of business that far exceeds the volume of any competitor and that is twice what I had anticipated."

At the Las Vegas ribbon-cutting, Buffett tried to claim the idea was his. "It didn't work. Today, when I pontificate about retailing, Berkshire people just say, 'What does Bill think?'"

R.C. Willey's Utah sales: grown from $250,000 (when Bill Child took over 48 years earlier) to over $300M by 2001.
