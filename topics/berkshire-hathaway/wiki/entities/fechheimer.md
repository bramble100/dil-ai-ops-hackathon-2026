## Overview

A uniform manufacturing and distribution business, founded in 1842 in Cincinnati. Acquired by Berkshire in June 1986 after Chairman Bob Heldman wrote a letter responding to Berkshire's annual-report "ad" for acquisitions.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** June 3, 1986; Berkshire purchased ~84% at a ~$55M total business valuation
- **Prior ownership:** Sold to venture capitalists in a 1981 LBO; management retained equity; debt paid down substantially by 1986
- **Management:** Three-generation Heldman family — Bob (Chairman), George (President), Gary, Roger, Fred
- **1987 pre-tax earnings:** $13.3M (up substantially from 1986's $8.4M)
- **Berkshire visited neither Cincinnati headquarters nor any facilities** — evaluation based purely on economic characteristics and management quality

## Significance

Fechheimer established the Berkshire "ad" acquisition pipeline. The pattern: family business with excellent economics, multi-generational management, sellers wanting a permanent home. Identical in structure to the Nebraska Furniture Mart acquisition — most shares held by people wishing to redeploy capital; operating family wanted to continue managing.

**[1986 Letter]** "Its economic record is superb; its managers are talented, high-grade, and love what they do; and the Heldman family wanted to continue its financial interest in partnership with us."

**[1987 Letter]** "Three generations of Heldmans have for decades consistently built the sales and profits" — earnings increased substantially in 1987.

One limitation: size. Utilizing only ~2% of Berkshire's net worth, Fechheimer was too small to materially affect overall returns.

### 1988-1989 Performance

**[1988 Letter]** A 1988 acquisition is integrated into Fechheimer — consistent with the Heldman family's operational capability. Five Heldmans now manage the combined enterprise. Buffett notes there's "a lot of heredity in this business"[^2].

**[1989 Letter]** Part of the "Sainted Seven Plus One." The Heldman family continues building the uniform business. Fechheimer validates the Berkshire acquisition template: family management, no corporate interference, compounding returns within the subsidiary[^3].

**[1990 Letter]** George Heldman (69) retired, with Bob, Fred, Gary and Roger Heldman carrying on. "I tried to talk him out of it but he had one irrefutable argument: with four other Heldmans to carry on, he was leaving us with an abundance of managerial talent." Operating performance improved considerably as integration problems from the 1988 acquisition moderated. Several unusual items kept reported earnings flat despite improved operations. Retail operation now has 42 stores in 22 states[^4].

**[1991 Letter]** Pre-tax earnings: $12.9M (vs. $12.5M in 1990). Steady performance.

**[1992 Letter]** Pre-tax earnings: $13.7M — another record. The four-Heldman management team continues to perform well, validating George's retirement as a clean succession rather than a disruption[^5].
