## Overview

Executive Jet Aviation (EJA), operating under the NetJets brand, is the fractional-aircraft ownership company acquired by Berkshire Hathaway on August 7, 1998 for approximately $725M in cash and stock. Rich Santulli invented the fractional-ownership model in 1986; by acquisition EJA was by far the largest operator in the industry. Buffett and Munger were both EJA customers who independently concluded it was the best aviation value for non-commercial travelers.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** August 7, 1998
- **Consideration:** ~$725M in cash and stock
- **Founder/CEO:** Rich Santulli (inventor of fractional aircraft ownership, 1986)
- **Scale at acquisition:** 163 aircraft; 1,000+ customers; largest fractional operator by far
- **Model:** Customers buy a fraction of a specific aircraft type (e.g., 1/16 share) and receive guaranteed availability with 4–24 hours' notice, proportional flight hours, and no ownership/maintenance complexity
- **Competitive position:** Dominant; first-mover scale advantages in scheduling, fleet standardization, and pilot training create a moat that smaller competitors cannot match

## Growth (1999)

[1999 Letter] EJA was supply-constrained in 1999 — demand exceeded fleet capacity. Recurring revenues grew 46% year-over-year. FlightSafety (Berkshire's other aviation holding) became EJA's exclusive pilot training provider, creating an intra-Berkshire operational link.

## Investment Thesis

EJA represents a capital-intensive but economically attractive business: customers pay a large upfront fractional purchase price plus a monthly management fee plus a per-flight-hour variable charge. The model generates recurring, locked-in revenues from high-net-worth individuals and corporations with strong switching costs (crew familiarity, fleet standardization, guaranteed availability). Buffett's personal familiarity with the product gave him direct confidence in the value proposition.
