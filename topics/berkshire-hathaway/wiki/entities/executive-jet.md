## Overview

Executive Jet Aviation (EJA), operating under the NetJets brand, is the fractional-aircraft ownership company acquired by Berkshire Hathaway on August 7, 1998 for approximately $725M in cash and stock. Rich Santulli invented the fractional-ownership model in 1986; by acquisition EJA was by far the largest operator in the industry. Buffett and Munger were both EJA customers who independently concluded it was the best aviation value for non-commercial travelers.

## Key Facts

- **Acquired:** August 7, 1998
- **Consideration:** ~$725M in cash and stock
- **Founder/CEO:** Rich Santulli (inventor of fractional aircraft ownership, 1986)
- **Scale at acquisition:** 163 aircraft; 1,000+ customers; largest fractional operator by far
- **Model:** Customers buy a fraction of a specific aircraft type (e.g., 1/16 share) and receive guaranteed availability with 4–24 hours' notice, proportional flight hours, and no ownership/maintenance complexity
- **Competitive position:** Dominant; first-mover scale advantages in scheduling, fleet standardization, and pilot training create a moat that smaller competitors cannot match

## Growth (1999)

[1999 Letter] EJA was supply-constrained in 1999 — demand exceeded fleet capacity. Recurring revenues grew 46% year-over-year. FlightSafety (Berkshire's other aviation holding) became EJA's exclusive pilot training provider, creating an intra-Berkshire operational link.

## Investment Thesis

EJA represents a capital-intensive but economically attractive business: customers pay a large upfront fractional purchase price plus a monthly management fee plus a per-flight-hour variable charge. The model generates recurring, locked-in revenues from high-net-worth individuals and corporations with strong switching costs (crew familiarity, fleet standardization, guaranteed availability). Buffett's personal familiarity with the product gave him direct confidence in the value proposition.

## 2000-2001 Performance

**[2000 Letter]** EJA's recurring revenue from monthly management fees and hourly usage grew 49% in 2000 (following 46% in 1999). Business remains low-margin, with significant European expansion costs. "You can be sure that EJA's spending on safety will be whatever is needed." Buffett notes that he, his wife, children, sisters, 94-year-old aunt, all but one director, and at least nine Berkshire managers regularly fly NetJets. Pilots average 23 training days per year. [^3]

**[2001 Letter]** NetJets sold a record number of planes and grew service income 21.9% — but operated at a small loss (vs. small profit in 2000). Profitable in the US; European losses exceeded US profits. NetJets accounts for about half of fractional-ownership industry by customer plane value. After September 11, there was a brief spike in new orders; pace later returned to normal. Scale advantages are real: 300+ planes constantly moving in the US reduces positioning costs and response time. Margins will be modest under current competitive conditions, but the business's scale advantage over competitors is compounding. [^4]

### 2002-2003 Performance

**[2002 Letter]** NetJets maintains ~75% industry share (by value of planes under management). Operations span 132.7 million nautical miles across 130 countries. European operations continue to lose money. Rich Santulli praised as a visionary operator.

**[2003 Letter]** Pre-tax loss **$41M** plus $32M aircraft inventory writedown. Industry share slips to 70% (by net sales value) but still dominant. European losses continue — the primary drag on profitability. Mayo Clinic Executive Travel Response program launched. Despite the losses, Buffett praises Santulli and remains committed: the competitive moat is widening even as short-term losses persist. [^5]

### 2004: European Momentum and Marquis Jet Partnership

**[2004 Letter]** NetJets again captures **~70% of net new business** (by dollar value) in the four-company fractional industry. Key metrics:

- **US contracts:** 3,877 → **4,967** (including Marquis Jet Partners customers; vs. ~1,200 when Berkshire bought NetJets in 1998)
- **European contracts:** 364 → **693** (including 25-hour cards marketed directly in Europe)
- **Marquis Jet Partners:** Not owned by NetJets but a customer that repackages NetJets capacity into 25-hour cards, leveraging Berkshire's reputation
- **Financials:** Modest US profit largely offset by European losses. "Europe has been expensive for NetJets — far more expensive than I anticipated — but it is essential to building a flight operation that will forever be in a class by itself"
- **Cross-Atlantic usage:** US owners made 2,003 flights in Europe (+22% YoY, +137% from 2000); European owners made 1,067 flights in US (+65% YoY, +239% from 2000)
- Rich Santulli remains CEO; Buffett views provider selection "as akin to picking a brain surgeon: you simply want the best" [^6]

[^6]: [2004 Letter](../sources/buffett-letter-2004.md) — 70% net new business; US contracts 4,967 (from 3,877); European contracts 693 (from 364); Marquis Jet Partners; US profit offset by European losses; cross-Atlantic usage growing rapidly
