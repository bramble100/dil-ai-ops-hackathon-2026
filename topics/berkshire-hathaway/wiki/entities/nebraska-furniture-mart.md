## Overview

America's largest home furnishings store, generating over $100M in annual sales from a single 200,000 sq ft location in Omaha. Founded in 1937 by Rose Blumkin ("Mrs. B") with $500, acquired by Berkshire (90%) in 1983. The business embodies Buffett's ideal: exceptional value to customers translated into exceptional economics for owners.

## Key Facts

- **Founded:** 1937 by Rose Blumkin with $500
- **Acquired by Berkshire:** 1983 (90% ownership; 10% retained by family management)
- **Revenue:** $100M+ annually from a single store
- **Competitive position:** Sells more furniture, carpets, and appliances than all Omaha competitors combined
- **Key people:** Rose Blumkin ("Mrs. B"), Chairman, age 90, on sales floor seven days a week; Louie Blumkin, President, "the shrewdest buyer of furniture and appliances in the country"; three Blumkin grandsons in management
- **Cost advantage:** Expense ratios "competitors don't even dream about"

## The Mrs. B Story

Rose Blumkin emigrated from Russia at age 23, talking her way past a border guard with no formal education and no English. She learned the language from her daughter, who taught her each evening the words learned at school that day.

After years selling used clothing, she saved $500 and opened Nebraska Furniture Mart in 1937 — named after the American Furniture Mart in Chicago. When her tiny resources ran out, she sold furniture and appliances from her own home to pay creditors. When Omaha retailers pressured manufacturers not to sell to her, she found alternative supply channels and cut prices. She was hauled into court for violating Fair Trade laws — won every case, and sold the judge $1,400 worth of carpet after one hearing.

## Significance

### The "Grizzly" Test

> "One question I always ask myself in appraising a business is how I would like, assuming I had ample capital and skilled personnel, to compete with it. I'd rather wrestle grizzlies than compete with Mrs. B and her progeny." — 1983 Letter

This "competitive dread" test becomes a recurring Buffett heuristic for evaluating moats.

### Exceptional Value → Exceptional Economics

NFM's formula: buy brilliantly, operate at minimal expense ratios, pass savings to customers. The resulting volume dominance creates a self-reinforcing cycle — the ideal business model in Buffett's framework.

### Character-Based Management

Buffett emphasizes the Blumkin family's "business ability, work ethic, and, most important, character." The acquisition required no detailed due diligence — Buffett's admiration was based on decades of observation. This reflects the "honest and competent management" filter from the [Four Filters](../principles/four-filters.md).
