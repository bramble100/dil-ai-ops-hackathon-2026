## Overview

A major U.S. defense contractor. Berkshire initially bought General Dynamics shares as an arbitrage play on its Dutch tender offer in 1992, then converted to a long-term investor after studying CEO Bill Anders's strategy — a textbook example of how conviction can emerge from process.

## Key Facts

- **Initial entry:** Summer 1992; arbitrage on Dutch tender repurchasing ~30% of shares
- **Stake built:** 14% of remaining shares in one month (tender volume facilitated buying)
- **Cost:** $312.4M (14.1% stake at yearend 1992)
- **Market value at yearend 1992:** $450.8M
- **CEO:** Bill Anders — "clearly articulated and rational strategy"; "focused and imbued with a sense of urgency"

## Significance

The General Dynamics story illustrates how arbitrage and long-term investing can overlap. Buffett started buying purely for a small short-term spread on the tender, then studied the company and saw something exceptional in Bill Anders's execution. He immediately abandoned the arbitrage intent and built a large position[^1].

This case adds nuance to the four-filter framework: conviction can build in stages. The arbitrage rationale gave Buffett a reason to study the company deeply; the analysis revealed that filter (3) — management quality — was unusually strong. Opportunistic entry through a process that required deep reading of the situation is itself a form of price discipline[^1].
