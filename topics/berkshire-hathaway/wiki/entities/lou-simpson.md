## Overview

Lou Simpson is GEICO's Vice Chairman and Chief Investment Officer, managing the company's equity portfolio independently from Buffett. Over a 25-year span (1980–2004), Simpson compiled a **20.3% compound annual return** vs. 13.5% for the S&P 500 — one of the most impressive long-duration track records in institutional investing.

## Key Facts

- **Role:** Vice Chairman / CIO, GEICO
- **Portfolio:** ~$2.5B in equities (as of 2004)
- **25-year track record (1980–2004):** 20.3% CAGR vs. S&P 13.5%
- **Independence:** Buffett learns of Lou's trades only 10 days after month-end; "he's usually right"
- **Investment style:** Concentrated, long-term equity positions — philosophically aligned with Buffett/Munger

## Significance

**[2004 Letter]** Buffett devotes a full table and paragraph to Simpson's track record — an unusual spotlight for someone who operates quietly and independently. The 25-year CAGR of 20.3% compounding ~$2.5B of institutional capital is remarkable because: (1) it spans two recessions, the dot-com bubble, and its aftermath; (2) it's achieved with a concentrated portfolio, not indexing; and (3) the magnitude of capital managed makes the outperformance harder to sustain.

Buffett's framing implies Simpson as a potential successor for Berkshire's investment management — one of only a handful of people Buffett publicly praises for investment acumen over multi-decade horizons. The autonomy Buffett grants Simpson (learning of trades only after the fact) is itself a testament to trust and alignment[^1].
