## Definition

Economic Goodwill is the capitalized value of a business's ability to earn returns on net tangible assets well above market rates. It arises from durable competitive advantages — consumer franchises, low-cost production positions, governmental franchises — and is entirely separate from accounting Goodwill (the excess of purchase price over fair value of identifiable assets). Economic Goodwill can increase even as accounting Goodwill is amortized to zero.

## Buffett's Teaching

### Economic vs. Accounting Goodwill

**[1983 Letter — Appendix]** When Blue Chip bought See's in 1972 for $25M, See's had ~$8M in net tangible assets and earned ~$2M after tax (25% ROE on tangibles). The $17M excess was recorded as accounting Goodwill and amortized over 40 years. But by 1983, See's earned $13M after tax on $20M of tangible assets — the economic Goodwill had _grown enormously_ while accounting Goodwill had been written down to $12.5M.

> "While accounting Goodwill regularly decreased from the moment of purchase, economic Goodwill increased in irregular but very substantial fashion." — 1983 Appendix

### Sources of Economic Goodwill

Three primary sources identified:

1. **Consumer franchise** — "a pervasive favorable reputation with consumers based upon countless pleasant experiences they have had with both product and personnel" (See's Candies)
2. **Low-cost producer position** — sustainable structural cost advantage (GEICO's direct-marketing model)
3. **Governmental franchise** — not subject to profit regulation (television stations)

> "Consumer franchises are a prime source of economic Goodwill... the value of the product to the purchaser, rather than its production cost, [becomes] the major determinant of selling price." — 1983 Appendix

### Goodwill as the Ultimate Inflation Hedge

The key insight: businesses with high economic Goodwill require minimal tangible assets, so when inflation doubles the price level, they need far less additional capital to maintain operations.

**The See's vs. Mundane Business comparison:**

| Metric              | See's (1972) | Mundane Business |
| ------------------- | ------------ | ---------------- |
| Earnings            | $2M          | $2M              |
| Net tangible assets | $8M          | $18M             |
| ROE on tangibles    | 25%          | 11%              |
| Purchase price      | $25M         | $18M (at book)   |

After a doubling of the price level:

| Metric                    | See's | Mundane Business |
| ------------------------- | ----- | ---------------- |
| New earnings              | $4M   | $4M              |
| Additional capital needed | $8M   | $18M             |
| Nominal value gain        | $25M  | $18M             |
| Gain per dollar invested  | $3.12 | $1.00            |

> "Asset-heavy businesses generally earn low rates of return — rates that often barely provide enough capital to fund the inflationary needs of the existing business, with nothing left over for real growth, for distribution to owners, or for acquisition of new businesses." — 1983 Appendix

> "During inflation, Goodwill is the gift that keeps giving." — 1983 Appendix

This overturns the traditional wisdom that tangible assets protect against inflation: "It doesn't work that way."

### Buffett's Intellectual Evolution

> "My own thinking has changed drastically from 35 years ago when I was taught to favor tangible assets and to shun businesses whose value depended largely upon economic Goodwill. This bias caused me to make many important business mistakes of omission, although relatively few of commission." — 1983 Letter

Quoting Keynes: "The difficulty lies not in the new ideas but in escaping from the old ones."

### How to Evaluate Goodwill

Two perspectives for managers and investors:

1. **Evaluating operating results:** Ignore amortization of Goodwill. "What a business can be expected to earn on unleveraged net tangible assets, excluding any charges against earnings for amortization of Goodwill, is the best guide to the economic attractiveness of the operation."
2. **Evaluating acquisitions:** Also ignore amortization, but carry purchased Goodwill at full cost forever. Define cost as "full intrinsic business value" of all consideration given, not just accounting value.

A good business (perspective 1) is not always a good purchase (perspective 2) — "although it's a good place to look for one."[^1]

### The Textile Auction as Negative Example (1985)

**[1985 Letter]** The devastating counterpoint: textile equipment with $13M original cost (including $2M spent 1980-84) and $866K book value sold at auction for $163,122 gross — net of costs, less than zero. Looms bought for $5,000 apiece in 1981 sold for $26 each as scrap. Meanwhile: "the economic goodwill attributable to two paper routes in Buffalo — or a single See's candy store — considerably exceeds the proceeds we received from this massive collection of tangible assets."

NFM, See's, and the Buffalo News demonstrate the positive case: $72M pre-tax earnings (vs. $8M fifteen years prior) with only $40M additional capital. The average American business requires ~$5 of additional capital per $1 of additional earnings[^2].

### The Scott Fetzer Purchase-Price Demonstration (1986)

**[1986 Letter — Appendix]** Berkshire paid $315M for Scott Fetzer's $172.4M book value — a $142.6M premium allocated to inventory write-ups ($37.3M), fixed asset adjustments ($68M net of deferred tax changes), and residual Goodwill ($24.3M). The resulting balance sheet showed $315M shareholders' equity vs. the old company's $172.4M — same assets, different accounting.

The annual charges flowing from these write-ups ($11.6M) reduced reported GAAP earnings from $40.2M to $28.6M — but none were tax-deductible and none represented real economic costs. Owner earnings were identical under both presentations[^3].

### Unrecorded Goodwill (1986-1987)

**[1986 Letter]** Key managers improved earnings "dramatically while, except in the case of insurance, utilizing little additional capital. This accomplishment builds economic value, or 'Goodwill,' that does not show up in the net worth figure on our balance sheet."

**[1987 Letter]** Seven non-financial units earned ~57% after-tax ROE on $175M of equity — carried on Berkshire's books at ~$222M above underlying assets. But the units should be judged on their returns on underlying (pre-premium) equity, not on what Berkshire paid.

### Sainted Seven Plus One (1988-1989)

**[1988 Letter]** The seven non-financial units raise their return to "about 67% on average equity capital" — again without meaningful leverage. The gap between book value and economic goodwill continues widening.

**[1989 Letter]** Borsheim's joins the group, making it the "Sainted Seven Plus One." The combined economics: 57% return on average equity with no net leverage — cash equivalents matched funded debt. Buffett notes that unrecorded goodwill continues growing as these managers improve earnings "while, except in the case of insurance, utilizing little additional capital"[^4].

## Practical Application

- **Evaluate ROE on net tangible assets** — a business earning high returns on tangible assets possesses economic Goodwill, regardless of what the balance sheet shows
- **Ignore Goodwill amortization** in evaluating operating results; carry it at full cost when evaluating acquisitions
- **Prefer asset-light businesses** with pricing power during inflationary periods — they need minimal incremental capital to maintain purchasing power
- **Distinguish economic from accounting Goodwill** — a rising franchise value can coexist with a declining balance sheet figure
