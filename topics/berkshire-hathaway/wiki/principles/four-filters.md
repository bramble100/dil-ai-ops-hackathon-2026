## Definition

Buffett's four criteria for selecting equity investments — applied identically whether buying an entire company or a small stock position:

1. **Understandable business** — within the buyer's circle of competence
2. **Favorable long-term prospects** — durable competitive advantage
3. **Honest and competent management** — integrity and skill
4. **Very attractive price** — significant margin of safety

Criteria (1)-(3) can usually be satisfied by a "small number of potential investments." Filter (4) — price — is the binding constraint that "often prevents action."

## Buffett's Teaching

> "We select our marketable equity securities in much the same way we would evaluate a business for acquisition in its entirety. We want the business to be (1) one that we can understand, (2) with favorable long-term prospects, (3) operated by honest and competent people, and (4) available at a very attractive price." — 1977 Letter

> "We ordinarily make no attempt to buy equities for anticipated favorable stock price behavior in the short term. In fact, if their business experience continues to satisfy us, we welcome lower market prices of stocks we own as an opportunity to acquire even more of a good thing at a better price." — 1977 Letter

**Concentration follows conviction:** Once all four filters are passed, Buffett commits heavily. "We try to avoid buying a little of this or that when we are only lukewarm about the business or its price. When we are convinced as to attractiveness, we believe in buying worthwhile amounts."[^1]

**Same framework for stocks and acquisitions:** This is a key Berkshire insight — there is no separate "stock" framework and "acquisition" framework. The same four tests apply. The only difference is that the stock market sometimes offers better prices than negotiated deals[^2].

## Evolution

Stated explicitly in the 1977 letter and restated with slight variation in 1978. The 1982 letter formalizes the acquisition-specific version as the six criteria (see [Capital Allocation](capital-allocation.md)), which become a recurring feature of every subsequent letter. The 1983 letter adds the "grizzly" test as a moat evaluation heuristic: "One question I always ask myself in appraising a business is how I would like, assuming I had ample capital and skilled personnel, to compete with it."[^3] In later letters, each filter deepens into its own principle:

- Filter (1) → [circle of competence] (to be developed)
- Filter (2) → [Business Quality](business-quality.md) (moats, tailwinds)
- Filter (3) → management quality assessment (to be developed)
- Filter (4) → intrinsic value and margin of safety (to be developed)
