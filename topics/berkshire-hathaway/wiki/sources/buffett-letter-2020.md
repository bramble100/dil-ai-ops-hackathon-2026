## Summary

The 2020 letter contains Buffett's most thorough defense of the conglomerate form, his PCC overpayment confession ($11B writedown), and a masterclass on share repurchase compounding using Apple as the case study. Float reaches $138B. BNSF and BHE earn a combined $8.3B despite COVID-19's impact; Katie Farmer becomes BNSF's first female CEO. Buffett retells Berkshire's origin stories — GEICO, National Indemnity, Nebraska Furniture Mart, Clayton Homes, and Pilot Flying J — weaving them into the "never bet against America" theme. Operating earnings fell 9% to $21.9B, but $24.7B in repurchases (5.2% of shares) increased per-share intrinsic value.

## Key Claims

- GAAP: $42.5B ($21.9B operating, $4.9B realized, $26.7B unrealized, -$11B writedown)
- **PCC mistake confession**: "I paid too much for the company" — $11B writedown; aerospace downturn exposed overestimate of normalized profit potential; "a big one" among errors
- **Conglomerate defense**: traditional conglomerates failed because (a) great businesses wouldn't sell, forcing purchases of so-so companies, and (b) overvalued stock used as acquisition currency ("I'll pay you $10,000 for your dog by giving you two of my $5,000 cats"); Berkshire's "two strings to our bow" — controlled + non-controlled businesses
- **Four "family jewels"**: (1) P/C insurance (Ajit Jain, 53 years), (2) BNSF 100%, (3) Apple 5.4%, (4) BHE 91%
- **$24.7B share repurchases** = 80,998 A-share equivalents = 5.2% reduction; "American CEOs have an embarrassing record of devoting more company funds to repurchases when prices have risen than when they have tanked. Our approach is exactly the reverse."
- Apple repurchase compounding: started at 5.2% (mid-2018), sold some, now own 5.4% due to Apple buybacks; combined with Berkshire buybacks, shareholders own 10% more Apple indirectly than in July 2018
- Float $138B; costless on cumulative basis; "we like our odds"
- Bond market bleak: 10-year Treasury 0.93% at yearend (down 94% from 15.8% in September 1981)
- BNSF: 15% of non-local US ton-miles; 390 antecedent railroads; $41B invested since purchase; $41.8B dividends paid to Berkshire; 23,000 miles, 28 states; Katie Farmer CEO (Carl Ice retired); 7% volume decline but +2.9pp profit margin
- BHE: $18B western grid project (2006-2030); never paid a dividend; $154B US PP&E (Berkshire #1, AT&T #2 at $127B); BHE+BNSF combined $8.3B 2020 ($4.2B in 2011)
- See's Candies 100th year: Mary See's first small outlet in LA → hundreds of shops; "Don't mess with my candy"
- GEICO founding retold: Leo & Lillian Goodwin, $100K, 1936; $238K first year → $35B in 2020
- National Indemnity: Jack Ringwalt, $125K, 1940, Omaha Central High; odd-ball risks; "largest P/C company in the world as measured by net worth"
- Clayton + Pilot: Knoxville brothers; both >$1B pre-tax; 47,000 combined employees; Big Jim Haslam age 90
- NFM: Mrs. B ($2,500, 1936); $72,264 net worth / $50 cash (1946); Louie Blumkin Normandy/Purple Heart; three largest US furniture stores; COVID record sales despite six-week closure; "God Bless America"
- **"Never bet against America"** — Berkshire's framework for prosperity required America's framework
- **Berkshire Partnership** essay: BPL 1956-1969 history; five ownership buckets (founder, index, active institutional, active individual, long-term partners); Phil Fisher's restaurant analogy; Stan Truhlsen turns 100; Emdee, Ltd.
- Top holdings: Apple $120.4B (5.4%), BofA $31.3B, Coca-Cola $21.9B, AmEx $18.3B; BYD $5.9B; Japanese trading houses begin; Wells Fargo dropped from top 15

## Relevance

The 2020 letter is simultaneously retrospective and forward-looking. The conglomerate defense explains why Berkshire's structure works when most conglomerates failed. The PCC confession adds to the "mistakes" canon alongside Dexter and USAir. The Apple/repurchase compounding section is the most mathematically precise demonstration of how buybacks create shareholder value. The origin stories — retold during COVID — reinforce the "American entrepreneurship" theme that runs through 49 years of letters.
