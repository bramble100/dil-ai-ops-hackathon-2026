## Summary

The 2005 letter reports Berkshire's 41st year under present management, with book value rising 6.4% to $59,377/share (21.5% CAGR from 1964). Five acquisitions were completed or announced — all for cash — and the letter introduces two of Buffett's most enduring parables: the **Gotrocks family** (frictional costs destroy investor returns) and the **Fred Futile option math** (fixed-price options reward CEOs who retain earnings regardless of performance). Hurricane Katrina inflicted $2.5B in losses ($3.4B total with Rita/Wilma), yet float remained costless at $49.3B thanks to GEICO's stellar year. The management succession question is addressed publicly for the first time.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share: $59,377 (+6.4%); 41-year CAGR 21.5%; per-share investments $74,129 (28.0% CAGR); per-share pre-tax operating earnings $2,441 (17.2% CAGR)
- Five acquisitions completed or announced, all for cash: MedPro (medical malpractice), Forest River (RVs, $1.6B sales), Business Wire (press releases, 25,000 clients, 150 countries), Applied Underwriters (81%, workers' comp), PacifiCorp via MidAmerican ($3.4B equity + $1.7B debt, 1.6M electric customers)
- Hurricane losses: Katrina $2.5B, Rita + Wilma $0.9B = $3.4B total; float $49.3B still costless overall
- GEICO: market share 5.6% → 6.1%, policies +12.1%, productivity up 32% (employment −4%), ad spend $502M (from $31M in 1996), $1,221M underwriting profit
- Gen Re Securities derivatives wind-down: 2,890 → 741 contracts; $104M loss in 2005; $404M cumulative; 100-year contract discovered; "canary in the coal mine"
- Clayton Homes: servicing portfolio $5.4B → $17B; $416M pre-tax earnings; Karsten acquired; industry at 40-year lows
- MidAmerican: PUHCA repealed Aug 8, 2005; Berkshire converts to 83.4% voting common; PacifiCorp adds 1.6M customers; HomeServices $64B in transactions; four-party partnership (Buffett/Scott/Sokol/Abel) unchanged
- Manufacturing/service/retail: 22.2% tangible ROE on $16.8B equity; Fruit of the Loom 48.7% men's underwear share (from 44.2%), 24.7% women's (from 13.7%); Shaw $550M bolt-on acquisitions; FlightSafety Farnborough $100M+ facility; NetJets US deep in red despite European growth (+37% contracts)
- Portfolio: $46.7B market value on $15.9B cost; Gillette merged into P&G ($5B pre-tax non-economic gain); new positions in Anheuser-Busch and Wal-Mart; Wells Fargo increased; realistic 10-year expectation: 6-8% earnings growth, stock prices to match
- **Gotrocks parable:** frictional costs (brokers → managers → consultants → hyper-Helpers/hedge funds) now consume ~20% of corporate earnings before reaching equity investors; Newton's Fourth Law: "For investors as a whole, returns decrease as motion increases"
- **Fred Futile/Stagnant Inc.:** fixed-price options on 1% of a company that earns 10% ROE and retains all earnings yield $158M for the CEO over ten years even if the business improves not at all — CEO earns $100M+ even if earnings _decline_ 20%; options give management free capital
- **Executive compensation critique:** "Ratchet, Ratchet and Bingo"; comp committees as slaves to comparative data; Jim Kilts at Gillette praised as rare CEO worth every penny; "nothing succeeds like failure" for fired executives
- Trade deficit: $955M pre-tax forex loss (2005); $2B cumulative gain; shifting from direct currency to foreign equities for positive carry
- Debt philosophy: three acceptable uses — (1) repos for short-term strategies, (2) receivables financing (Clayton), (3) MidAmerican utility debt (ringfenced); "a long string of impressive numbers multiplied by a single zero always equals zero"
- **Management succession:** first public discussion — three candidates identified, board unanimous on current choice, separate investment manager role planned; decay scenario (not just death) addressed candidly; "I feel terrific"
- See's Candies: Chuck Huggins retires after 34 years (CEO since 1972); Brad Kinstler succeeds
- "Widening the moat": daily competitive improvement essay — short-term earnings targets that sacrifice brand/cost/customer position are "irreversible damage"; auto/airline industries as cautionary examples

## Relevance

The 2005 letter is the richest on **executive compensation** in the entire corpus — the Fred Futile math and Gotrocks parable are signature Buffett teaching tools. It also contains the first explicit discussion of **management succession** (a topic that will dominate later letters). The derivatives wind-down continues to illustrate the 2002 "WMD" warning. The PacifiCorp acquisition marks MidAmerican's emergence as a major Berkshire platform, and the PUHCA repeal removes a structural governance constraint. GEICO's productivity gains (32% in two years) demonstrate the "widening the moat" principle in action.
