## Summary

The 1995 letter announces Berkshire's most transformative year: three acquisitions that doubled revenues (Helzberg's Diamond Shops, R.C. Willey, and — just after yearend — 100% of GEICO for $2.3B), the proposed Class A/B recapitalization to preempt expense-laden unit trusts, and the convertible preferred retrospective revealing Gillette as the massive winner and USAir as the persistent heartburn. Book value reached $14,426/share (43.1% YoY, 23.6% CAGR over 31 years), though Buffett warns that market price has outpaced intrinsic value and that "at a price at which Charlie and I would not consider buying it."

## Key Claims

- Net worth gained $5.3B (45.0%); per-share book value grew 43.1% due to share issuance for Helzberg's and R.C. Willey; 31-year CAGR 23.6%
- "Rising tide lifts all yachts" — disclaims credit for the strong year
- GEICO purchase of remaining ~50% for $2.3B completed January 2, 1996; Buffett tells the 45-year origin story (Lorimer Davidson, January 1951, cold Saturday, Washington DC)
- GEICO moat: operating costs at 23.6% of premiums (down ~1pt YoY); "In business, I look for economic castles protected by unbreachable 'moats'"
- Tony Nicely (52, 34-year GEICO veteran) runs insurance; Lou Simpson runs investments (22.8% annual equity returns vs. 15.7% S&P, 1980-1995); Lou as Buffett/Munger succession backstop
- Float reaches $3,607.2M at negative cost (underwriting profit); 20.7% annual compounded growth since 1967
- Float-as-equity analogy: if $3.4B of float were replaced with equity, same assets but more shares and lower earnings — float "wonderfully benefits a business"
- Helzberg's Diamond Shops acquired via stock exchange (May 1994 sidewalk encounter at 58th & Fifth Avenue); 134 stores, $282M sales; Jeff Comment CEO
- R.C. Willey Home Furnishings acquired via stock (Irv Blumkin referral); Bill Child grew from $250K (1954) to $257M (1995); >50% Utah market share
- "Have-to-be-smart-every-day" retailing vs. "have-to-be-smart-once" network TV station; "in retailing, to coast is to fail"
- No strategic plan as acquisition advantage: "Thus we feel no need to proceed in an ordained direction... can instead simply decide what makes sense for our owners"
- Acquisition standard: mentally compare every move against "dozens of other opportunities open to us, including the purchase of small pieces of the best businesses in the world via the stock market"
- Convertible preferred retrospective: Gillette best ($600M → $2,502M common); Champion sold at cost+19% after-tax; Salomon $140M first partial redemption; First Empire preferred being called; USAir at 60% of par, paying penalty dividends (5% over prime)
- The Gillette error: should have bought $600M of common (~60M shares at ~$10) instead of preferred (48M conversion shares) — $625M opportunity cost minus $70M extra dividends
- Disney/Cap Cities merger: July 14, 1995 Sun Valley encounter with Michael Eisner; Berkshire sought Disney stock; previously sold Cap Cities at $63 (1993 Mistake Du Jour) then $635M more sold vs. $1.27B merger value
- Disney 1966 anecdote: market cap <$90M, $21M pre-tax, "selling at only five times rides!"; bought at 31¢/share split-adjusted, sold at 48¢ in 1967
- Class B recapitalization to counter expense-laden Berkshire clone trusts; B shares = 1/30th economic interest, 1/200th vote; public offering of $100M+ new B shares
- Candid warning: "at $36,000... Charlie and I do not believe it undervalued"
- Berkshire overhead: <2 basis points of net worth; headquarters staff grew from 11 to 12
- Super-cat: fourth year of good fortune; Ajit Jain's versatility — insured Mike Tyson's life, Lloyd's against 225+ "names" dying, two Chinese satellite launches
- "A bad reinsurance contract is like hell: easy to enter and impossible to exit"
- Scale drag: "the difficulty of finding [good big ideas] grows in direct proportion to our financial success"
- No key manager has left Berkshire for another employer in 30 years (1965-1995)
- Mrs. B (102) honored at The Rose theater restoration; Susan Jacques (36) CEO of Borsheim's

## Relevance

This letter marks the culmination of the GEICO story — from a cold Saturday in 1951 to 100% ownership at $2.3B. The full GEICO narrative (45 years, $47M → $2.3B for the final half) is the ultimate case study for patient, franchise-based investing. The "have-to-be-smart-once" vs. "-every-day" framework adds a new dimension to business quality analysis. The Class B recapitalization demonstrates Buffett's commitment to attracting the right shareholders. The convertible preferred retrospective provides the definitive scorecard on a capital allocation experiment spanning 1987-1995.
