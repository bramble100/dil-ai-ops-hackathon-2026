## Summary

The 2004 letter marks Berkshire's 40th anniversary under Buffett's management, with book value reaching $55,824/share (+10.5%, slightly trailing the S&P's +10.9%). The letter's intellectual centerpiece is an extended essay on the US trade deficit and its "Sharecropper's Society" consequences. Operationally, it features a "Portrait of a Disciplined Underwriter" — a 25-year NICO premium table demonstrating the ebb-and-flow model — and a detailed retelling of GEICO's 69-year history from Leo Goodwin's $200,000 founding in 1936 to an expected 6% market share in 2005. Float reaches $46.1B with $1.5B underwriting profit. Buffett confesses to sitting on $43B in cash after "striking out" on acquisitions, and acknowledges he "talked when I should have walked" during the Bubble.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share: $55,824 (+10.5%); 40-year CAGR 21.9% (from $19 in 1964). S&P returned +10.9% — first near-parity year since Berkshire's equity holdings fell to <50% of net worth
- Float: $46.1B (up $1.9B), with $1.5B underwriting profit — better than free for ~half of the 38 years in insurance
- $43B in cash equivalents — unable to find multi-billion acquisitions or attractive securities; "not a happy position"
- NICO "Portrait of a Disciplined Underwriter": 25-year table showing premium from $79.6M (1980) through boom-bust-boom cycle to $605.6M (2004); Don Wurster has $950M float at near-certain negative cost
- GEICO history retold: Leo Goodwin founded 1936 with $200,000; State Farm/Allstate comparison; Jack Byrne rescue 1976; Tony Nicely era from 1993; expected ~6% market share in 2005; New Jersey re-entry (140,000 policyholders since August 2004); customers saved ~$1B; profit-sharing $191M (24.3% of salary)
- General Re: Joe Brandon "restored a long-admired culture of underwriting discipline" but current-year gains offset by adverse prior-year developments; $3M underwriting profit; float $23.1B
- Four Florida hurricanes: $1.25B Berkshire loss; mega-catastrophe preparedness detailed ($100B scenario — Berkshire share 3-5%)
- Manufacturing/Service/Retail: $44.1B revenues; 21.7% return on average tangible net worth. Shaw $466M pre-tax despite $300M fiber cost surge (25.6% tangible ROE). Fruit of the Loom +14% unit sales. MiTek: 100% steel cost increase; Gene Toombs building "a mini-Berkshire"
- Clayton Homes: Oakwood acquisition completed (now industry's largest producer/retailer); 11,837 employees (from 7,136); receivables ~$9B; Berkshire financing synergy ($7.35B at cost-plus-one)
- FlightSafety: rebound to 15.1% return on tangible equity (from 8.4%); 283 simulators, $1.2B cost; Al Ueltschi hands CEO to Bruce Whitman
- NetJets: ~70% of net new business; US contracts 3,877→4,967; European contracts 364→693; modest US profit offset by European losses; Marquis Jet Partners card program
- Big Four portfolio (AmEx/Coke/Gillette/Wells Fargo): $3.83B cost → $1.2B annual earnings share (31.3% of cost); $434M cash distributions (11.3% of cost). "I talked when I should have walked" during the Bubble
- Lou Simpson: 25-year GEICO equity track record presented (20.3% CAGR vs. S&P 13.5%); manages ~$2.5B independently
- Foreign exchange: $21.4B across 12 currencies; extended trade deficit analysis; $618B current account deficit; "Sharecropper's Society" warning; Adam Smith critique; Keynes on failing conventionally
- Corporate governance: stock-option expensing mandatory June 15, 2005; Coke board independence controversy (Matthew 6:21 "treasure" test); whistleblower line value; directors-without-CEO meetings
- Simple propositions: zinc recovery project failure ($579M write-off); 10 variables at 90% each = 35% probability; "mono-linked chains"
- Three investor enemies: (1) high costs/excessive trading, (2) tips and fads over quantified evaluation, (3) untimely entries and exits
- "Run your business as if it were the only asset your family will own over the next hundred years"
- "Extraordinary business ability is largely innate" — only one of NICO's four CEOs since 1940 graduated from college
- Tom Murphy's $3M hiring decision: a $20,000/year employee is really a $3M lifetime commitment

## Relevance

The 2004 letter is transitional — Berkshire's equity-heavy era has given way to a diversified conglomerate where stock market movements affect less than 50% of net worth. The $43B cash problem foreshadows the "elephant gun" challenge that dominates later letters. The NICO portrait and GEICO history provide the most comprehensive insurance narratives in the corpus. The trade deficit essay is Buffett's longest macro-economic argument, extending the 2003 foreign currency positions into a full thesis. The "talked when I should have walked" Bubble confession is the most candid self-criticism since the 1989 "Mistakes" retrospective.
