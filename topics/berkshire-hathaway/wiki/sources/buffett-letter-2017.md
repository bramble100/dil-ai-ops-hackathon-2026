# Buffett's 2017 Shareholder Letter

## Summary

The 2017 letter records a $65.3B net worth gain (+23.0%, book value $211,750), but Buffett immediately flags that $29B came from the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act — not from operations. The letter's three intellectual landmarks are: (1) the new GAAP rule requiring unrealized equity gains/losses in net income, which Buffett warns will produce "truly wild and capricious swings" that make bottom-line figures "useless"; (2) the conclusion of "The Bet" (S&P 125.8% vs. best fund-of-funds 87.7%) with a bonus lesson about bonds-to-Berkshire arbitrage; and (3) the acquisition-mania critique (CEO "can-do" self-selection, cheap debt fueling overpayment, Berkshire's all-equity evaluation discipline). Operationally, the $10.2B AIG retroactive reinsurance deal is the world-record single premium, pushing float to $114.5B — but three hurricanes (Harvey/Irma/Maria) end the 14-year underwriting profit streak with a $3.2B pre-tax loss. Ajit Jain and Greg Abel are named Vice Chairmen in early 2018, crystallizing succession.

## Key Claims

- Book value $211,750/share (+23.0%); 53-year CAGR 19.1%; overall gain 1,088,029%
- $65.3B net worth gain — $36B from operations, $29B from Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (December 2017)
- New GAAP rule: net change in unrealized equity gains/losses must be included in net income; $170B marketable stock portfolio can swing $10B+ in a quarter; GAAP bottom line "useless" for analysis
- "At Berkshire what counts most are increases in our normalized per-share earning power"
- Pilot Flying J: 38.6% partnership interest; $20B annual volume; ~750 locations; Haslam family; contractual to increase to 80% in 2023; Kevin Clayton's endorsement helped cement the deal
- Acquisition-mania critique: "CEO job self-selects for 'can-do' types"; "don't ask the barber whether you need a haircut"; subordinates cheer enlarged domains; investment bankers smell fees; "spreadsheets never disappoint"; cheap debt boosts EPS mechanically
- Berkshire evaluates acquisitions on an all-equity basis; "insane to risk what you have and need in order to obtain what you don't need"
- "The less the prudence with which others conduct their affairs, the greater the prudence with which we must conduct our own"
- Clayton Homes: 49% of US manufactured-home market (3x nearest competitor); 19,168 retail + 26,706 wholesale; Oakwood Homes + Harris Doyle bolt-ons doubled site-built presence
- Shaw Industries: $5.7B sales; 22,000 employees; USF acquisition (luxury vinyl tile, +40% sales in 2017)
- HomeServices: Long & Foster + Houlihan Lawrence + Gloria Nilson acquired; 40,950 agents; $127B in "sides"; close to #1 US by volume
- AIG retroactive reinsurance: $10.2B premium for up to $20B long-tail losses — world-record single premium
- Float $114,500M; 2017 underwriting loss $3.2B pre-tax — ended 14 consecutive years of underwriting profit
- Three hurricanes (Harvey, Irma, Maria): ~$3B Berkshire loss (~$2B after tax); industry-wide ~$100B; Berkshire's 3% share expected going forward
- $400B mega-cat probability: ~2% annually; Berkshire's share ~$12B, "far below annual earnings from non-insurance activities"
- Non-insurance pre-tax income $20B (+$950M); BNSF+BHE 44% of operating earnings
- Next five by earnings (Clayton, IMC, Lubrizol, Marmon, PCC): $5.5B
- Next five (Forest River, Johns Manville, MiTek, Shaw, TTI): $2.1B (up from $1.7B)
- Cash: $116B at yearend (up from $86.4B); capex $11.5B vs. depreciation $7.6B; amortization $1.3B (largely non-economic)
- Investments: Apple $21.0B cost/$28.2B market (largest holding by value); BofA warrant exercised → 700M common shares ($5B cost, $20.7B market); IBM mostly sold; Kraft Heinz $9.8B cost/$25.3B market (equity method)
- Ben Graham: "In the short run, the market is a voting machine; in the long run, however, it becomes a weighing machine"
- Four major Berkshire price declines: −59.1% (1973-75), −37.1% (10/87), −48.9% (1998-2000), −50.7% (2008-09) — "strongest argument against ever using borrowed money to own stocks"
- Kipling's If quoted for investor temperament
- "The Bet" concluded: S&P 500 index fund +125.8% vs. fund-of-funds average +36%; Girls Inc. received $2,222,279 (not $1M) thanks to bonds-to-Berkshire switch in 2012; "Performance comes, performance goes. Fees never falter"
- Bonds-to-Berkshire switch: zero-coupon bonds at 95.7% face value (.88% yield) sold in Nov 2012 → 11,200 BRK-B shares; "kindergarten-like analysis"
- Risk redefined: "the possibility that consumption today is foregone... and this objective won't be attained"; long-term bonds in 2012 were "far riskier" than equities by this standard
- Ajit Jain and Greg Abel elected directors and Vice Chairmen (early 2018); "Ajit responsible for insurance operations, Greg oversees the rest"; "Charlie and I will focus on investments and capital allocation"
- PCC bolt-on: Wilhelm Schulz GmbH (Germany); "Betting on people can sometimes be more certain than betting on physical assets"
- Todd Combs and Ted Weschler: >$12B each; $8B+ pension trust; independent decision-making

## Relevance

This letter marks the formal succession announcement (Jain/Abel as Vice Chairmen), the conclusion of the decade-long hedge-fund bet, the GAAP unrealized-gains revolution, and a new float high ($114.5B) despite the first underwriting loss in 15 years. The acquisition-mania critique, with its "barber/haircut" and "can-do CEO" formulations, joins Buffett's canon on institutional imperative.
