## Summary

The 1996 letter celebrates Berkshire's largest-ever acquisition (FlightSafety International, ~$1.5B) and GEICO's first full year as a subsidiary (10% voluntary auto policy growth, 16.9% profit-sharing contribution), while introducing "The Inevitables" as a formal investment category and delivering Buffett's first explicit index fund endorsement. Float reaches $6,702M at negative cost (22.3% CAGR since 1967), super-cat writes the California Earthquake Authority (~$1B exposure), and book value hits $19,011/share (31.8% YoY, 23.8% CAGR over 32 years). The letter also marks Berkshire's web debut (berkshirehathaway.com) and the USAir recovery after years of distress.

## Key Claims

- Net worth gained $6.2B (36.1%); per-share book value grew 31.8% to $19,011; 32-year CAGR 23.8%
- "The Inevitables": Coke and Gillette as businesses whose worldwide dominance is virtually certain to endure; "What could be more advantageous in an intellectual contest... than to have opponents who have been taught that thinking is a waste of energy?"
- FlightSafety International acquired December 1996 for ~$1.5B (cash + fractional BRK shares); Al Ueltschi founded 1951 (age 79 at deal); 41 learning centers, 175 simulators, $700M revenues; Buffett cold-called after NYSE seatmate conversation
- Kansas Bankers Surety acquired 1996 for ~$75M; Don Towle president; met at Berkshire's birthday party for Mrs. B
- GEICO first full subsidiary year: voluntary auto policies +10%; estimated market share gains ~1 full percentage point; $2.9B float; Buffett's 16.9% contribution to profit-sharing pool; Tony Nicely at 2x industry average policyholder growth
- GEICO incentive comp: twin goals of (1) growth in voluntary auto policies and (2) profitability of seasoned business; pays out at percentages of base salary (from 0% to 28%+); metrics tied to what employees can control daily
- Circle of competence: "Investors who confine themselves to what they know, as combative as that might be, will do fine." "What counts most is how well you understand what you know and that you know its boundaries... vital"
- Index fund recommendation: "Most investors, both institutional and individual, will find that the best way to own common stocks is through an index fund that charges minimal fees"
- "We make more money when snoring than when active" — "inactivity strikes us as intelligent behavior"
- Float reaches $6,702.0M at negative cost; 22.3% CAGR since 1967 ($17.3M → $6,702M)
- Super-cat: no mega-catastrophe in 1996 (fourth consecutive lucky year); major contracts include Allstate Florida and California Earthquake Authority (~$1B exposure); 10-15% probability of $600M single-event loss
- Ajit Jain: 10-year record, built $3B float business from scratch; "If Charlie, I and Ajit are ever in a sinking boat — and you can only save one of us — swim to Ajit"
- Incentive compensation principles: (1) goals tailored to economics of specific operating unit, (2) simple, (3) tied to things employee can influence in daily activities; Scott Fetzer incentive plan as ideal
- USAir recovery: $47.9M in dividend arrears paid; common at $40 (was $4); Buffett still expects to sell
- Salomon exchangeable notes ($500M): Travelers merger pending; Berkshire exchanged into Travelers common; "experience — negative overall — considerably increased my appreciation for the difficulty of being both combative and fair"
- Look-through earnings: $1,522M (1996); formula = reported operating earnings + undistributed investee earnings − hypothetical 14% capital gains tax on retention
- Disney/Cap Cities merger closed February 1996: Berkshire received Disney stock; $10,525M market value on $345M cost (Coca-Cola: $10,525M on $1,299M cost)
- Two-column intrinsic value framework: (1) per-share investments and (2) per-share pre-tax earnings from operations excluding investment income; investments/share $28,500, operating earnings/share $421
- "If you aren't willing to own a stock for ten years, don't even think about owning it for ten minutes"
- "Selling a wonderful business to buy another" — Bulls-trade-Michael-Jordan analogy on selling Coke for USAir
- Richard Branson airline joke: "How do you become a millionaire? Make a billion dollars and then buy an airline"
- "Lethargy bordering on sloth remains the cornerstone of our investment style"
- Berkshire.com launched: berkshirehathaway.com introduced for annual meeting logistics and proxy distribution
- Star Furniture acquired (January 1997, just after yearend); Melvyn Wolff CEO; $200M+ sales; #1 Houston market share
- Dairy Queen system-wide revenues $5B mentioned in connection to forthcoming 1997 acquisition

## Relevance

This letter crystallizes Buffett's mature investment philosophy: "The Inevitables" as the highest category of business quality, the circle of competence as the discipline that makes stock selection possible, and the index fund as the honest recommendation for those outside their circle. The FlightSafety acquisition demonstrates the "phone call" deal-making advantage of Berkshire's reputation. GEICO's first subsidiary year proves the acquisition thesis — operating leverage at 10% growth with incentive-driven management. The two-column intrinsic value framework provides the clearest window into how Buffett himself measures Berkshire's worth.
