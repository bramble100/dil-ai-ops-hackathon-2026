## Overview

The 1999 letter is Buffett's harshest self-assessment. Book value gained only 0.5% while the S&P rose 21% — "the worst absolute performance and worst relative performance in our history." Capital allocation earned a D. General Re's integration was rough: $1.4B pre-tax underwriting loss in 1999 wiped out years of float-cost advantage. Yet GEICO delivered perhaps its most spectacular year ever (market share 2.7%→4.1%, $242M marketing, $113.3M profit-sharing to associates). Two acquisitions anchor the year: Jordan's Furniture (highest sales/sqft in major furniture retail, Nov 1999) and MidAmerican Energy (~$2B, the first major regulated utility acquisition). The letter also contains the most explicit articulation of Buffett's tech-stock circle-of-competence refusal and a sweeping warning about equity market overvaluation.

## Key Claims

- Book value gained 0.5% to $37,987/share; 35-year CAGR 24.0%. "Worst absolute and relative performance in our history." Capital allocation grade: D. "We did many things right but also made one particularly egregious mistake." (General Re integration; Dexter Shoe deterioration implied.)
- General Re 1999 underwriting: pre-tax loss $1.4B. Reason: redundant reserves from prior years, inadequate pricing discipline during soft market years, and reserve adjustments. Float cost jumped to ~5.8% vs. T-bond rate. Buffett is explicit: "I have not kept the promise I made at the time of the acquisition, and I apologize." Ron Ferguson remains; Berkshire is restructuring reserve adequacy.
- GEICO spectacular year: market share 2.7%→4.1% (1.4-point gain in one year, fastest in company history); $242M marketing spend; 28.4% profit-sharing ($113.3M to 8,874 associates, avg ~$12,800/associate); Tony Nicely runs the best automobile insurer in America. Lorimer Davidson, the man who taught Buffett insurance in 1951, died age 97.
- Float: $25.3B total across the group. GEICO alone ~$3.4B; NICO and affiliates (Ajit Jain) $6.3B — Ajit has built a $6.3B float operation from scratch. General Re remains in reserve rehabilitation.
- Jordan's Furniture acquired Nov 13, 1999: family business since 1927, Barry and Eliot Tatelman. Sales-per-square-foot highest among major US furniture retailers. Pioneered "shoppertainment" — entertainment experiences integrated into showroom floor. Tatelmans paid $9M out of their own pockets in special bonuses to all employees at closing.
- MidAmerican Energy ~$2B acquired 1999. PUHCA 1935 (Public Utility Holding Company Act) constrains Berkshire's voting power to <10% of utility even though economic interest ~76%. Walter Scott Jr. (longtime Buffett friend) facilitated. David Sokol CEO. First regulated-utility investment for Berkshire; chosen for predictable cash flows and large capital deployment capacity.
- See's Candies: record year — 24% pre-tax margin; $857M cumulative pre-tax profits vs. $25M 1972 purchase price. 34x cumulative return.
- Look-through earnings: $1,926M (nearly flat from $1,930M in 1997; 1998 was suspended). Formula: operating earnings $1,085M + investee retained $921M − 14% hypothetical tax. Investee data includes General Re first full year.
- Operating earnings: $1,085M vs. $1,899M in 1998. Drop driven by General Re underwriting loss ($1.4B charge). Berkshire insurance operations ex-General-Re remained profitable.
- FlightSafety Boeing venture: FlightSafety partnered with Boeing to create FlightSafety Boeing, 50%/50%, for airliner simulation. 222 simulators; $215M capex investment.
- Dexter Shoe: competitive position deteriorating — imported shoes intensifying pressure on US manufacturers. Buffett bought Dexter in 1993 for 25,203 BRK shares; "a mistake — we traded a fine business (Berkshire shares) for a deteriorating one."
- Share repurchase extended framework: if Berkshire stock trades at a significant discount to intrinsic value, Buffett would repurchase shares rather than sit on cash — but the hurdle is steep (must trade at $X% below value AND have no better use for the capital). Not a programmatic buyback policy.
- Tech-stock circle of competence: "We don't own tech stocks, not because of past success or failure, but because we can't assess the durability of competitive advantage with the certainty we need. I may well be wrong about tech — intelligent people disagree. But I would rather be wrong, and lose the opportunity, than invest without the required understanding." Market loves growth; Berkshire can't predict which growth will be lasting.
- Market valuation warning (1999 context, Dow ~11,497): "Equity investors are wildly optimistic about future returns." For stocks to achieve historical real returns from current valuations, corporate profits must reach an impossibly high fraction of GDP, or valuations must contract. "The math doesn't work." Buffett estimates realistic 10-year equity returns at 4–6% annually (after inflation and transaction costs).
- "Enchanted market" framing: "Investors now expect the stock market to produce miracles. Many have abandoned all other standards. When the market falls, they'll be stunned." Driven by a decade of compounding gains and the tech-stock extrapolation fallacy.
- FASB goodwill debate: if purchase accounting is required (as FASB proposed), goodwill amortization will penalize honest acquisitions economically. Buffett opposes mandatory pooling elimination but also opposes the 40-year straight-line amortization that follows purchase accounting. He advocates an "impairment-only" approach to goodwill — only charge it when value actually declines. "Economic goodwill generally rises over time; the FASB rule destroys the very metric it intends to track."
