## Summary

The 2023 letter opens with a tribute to Charlie Munger, who died November 28, 2023, at 99. Buffett names Munger "the architect" of modern Berkshire — the one who redirected Buffett from cigar butts to wonderful businesses at fair prices. The letter introduces "Bertie" (Buffett's sister) as the intended reader, emphasizing plain-language communication. Operating earnings reach a record $37.4B, while GAAP figures ($96B) remain "worse-than-useless." BNSF faces capital-intensive reality ($22B in over-depreciation spending in 14 years) and margin erosion. BHE encounters an existential regulatory crisis: wildfire losses, zero-profitability threats in western states, and the specter of the public-power model. Insurance sets records across the board ($83B premiums, $5.4B underwriting profit). Occidental Petroleum (27.8%) and five Japanese trading companies (9% each, ¥1.3T yen-financed) represent significant new positions. Berkshire's $561B GAAP net worth is the largest in American history.

## Key Claims

- Charlie Munger died November 28, 2023, 33 days before his 100th birthday; Buffett names him "the architect" of Berkshire, himself as "the general contractor"
- Munger's 1965 advice: "Forget about ever buying another company like Berkshire. Add wonderful businesses at fair prices. Abandon everything you learned from Ben Graham. It works but only when practiced at small scale"
- Operating earnings record $37.4B; GAAP: $96B (2023), −$23B (2022), $90B (2021) — "worse-than-useless"
- BNSF: $5.1B earnings; 23,759 miles of track; $70B on balance sheet; $500B+ replacement cost; $22B over-depreciation in 14 years ($1.5B/year); profit margins slipped vs. all five peers; rail suicides ~once/day in North America
- BHE: $2.3B (down from $4.3B); wildfire losses of unknown final magnitude; "confiscatory resolution" risk; public-power model may spread; "I made a costly mistake in not" anticipating regulatory erosion
- Insurance: record $83B premiums; $5.4B underwriting profit; $9.6B investment income; 57 years in business; 5,000-fold volume increase; "Cooperstown honorees" of P/C insurance
- Occidental Petroleum: 27.8% common + warrants; Vicki Hollub praised; US energy independence (5M→13M BOEPD); carbon capture; "doing the right things for both its country and its owners"
- Five Japanese trading companies: ~9% each of Itochu, Marubeni, Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo; ¥1.6T cost/¥2.9T market value; ¥1.3T yen bonds; $8B unrealized gain; shareholder-friendly policies (less aggressive comp, 1/3 earnings to dividends)
- $561B GAAP net worth — largest of any American company; ~6% of S&P 500 total net worth
- "Only a handful of companies in this country capable of truly moving the needle"; "no possibility of eye-popping performance"
- "Never risk permanent loss of capital" — unchanged rule; "the casino now resides in many homes"
- Coke and AmEx: 20+ year "Rip Van Winkle slumber"; "patience pays, and one wonderful business can offset the many mediocre decisions that are inevitable"
- Hugh McCulloch (1863): "Never deal with a rascal under the expectation that you can prevent him from cheating you"
- "Berkshire is built to last"; "extreme fiscal conservatism is a corporate pledge"
- Market value: +15.8% (S&P +26.3%); CAGR 19.8%; overall gain 4,384,748%

## Relevance

This letter is the watershed of the Munger era. The architect/contractor metaphor reframes the entire Berkshire origin story — Munger, not Buffett, was the strategic visionary. The BHE regulatory crisis is the most serious operational warning since the 2008 financial crisis, introducing genuine uncertainty about utility returns. The scale problem is stated with unprecedented candor: Berkshire may be too large to outperform meaningfully. The Japanese trading companies represent the first major international equity commitment since ISCAR (2006). Occidental marks a return to concentrated public equity positions with a Buffett-era thesis (energy independence).
