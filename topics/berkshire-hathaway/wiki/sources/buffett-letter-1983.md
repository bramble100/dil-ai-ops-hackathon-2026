## Summary

The 1983 letter is one of the most foundational in the series. It opens with Buffett's 13 "Owner's Manual" business principles — a codification of the partnership philosophy he and Munger apply. The highlight is the acquisition of Nebraska Furniture Mart and the remarkable story of Rose Blumkin ("Mrs. B"). The letter covers Buffalo News achieving #1 weekday penetration nationally, See's cost control challenges, and the worst industry insurance results in decades (combined ratio 111). The famous appendix on "Goodwill and its Amortization: The Rules and The Realities" uses See's Candies to explain why economic goodwill — rooted in consumer franchises and low tangible asset requirements — is the real source of durable business value. Book value reaches $975.83/share (22.6% CAGR from 1964).

## Key Claims

- 13 business principles codified: partnership attitude, director skin-in-the-game, per-share intrinsic value as goal, two-pronged ownership approach, ignore accounting consequences, conservative leverage, no wish-list acquisitions at shareholder expense, $1 retained → $1 market value test, never issue shares below intrinsic value, reluctance to sell good businesses, candor in reporting, investment secrecy
- Nebraska Furniture Mart acquired (90% for undisclosed price): $100M+ annual revenue from a single 200,000 sq ft store; Mrs. B (Rose Blumkin) started with $500 in 1937, now 90 and still on the sales floor seven days a week
- "I'd rather wrestle grizzlies than compete with Mrs. B and her progeny" — on NFM's competitive position
- Book value is "a conservative but reasonably adequate proxy for growth in intrinsic business value" — but the two concepts are very different; the college education analogy illustrates
- Berkshire's intrinsic value "considerably exceeds" book value due to unrealized gains and economic goodwill
- Economic Goodwill appendix: See's earned $13M after tax on $20M net tangible assets — the economic Goodwill (capitalized excess return) far exceeds accounting Goodwill; this economic goodwill tends to rise with inflation because asset-light businesses need minimal additional capital
- "Asset-heavy businesses generally earn low rates of return — rates that often barely provide enough capital to fund the inflationary needs of the existing business" — overturning traditional wisdom that tangible assets hedge inflation
- "During inflation, Goodwill is the gift that keeps giving" — asset-light franchise businesses are the true inflation hedges
- Buffett's intellectual evolution: taught by Graham to favor tangible assets, but "business experience, direct and vicarious, produced my present strong preference for businesses that possess large amounts of enduring Goodwill and that utilize a minimum of tangible assets"
- Stock splits rejected: they would attract short-term traders and "downgrade the quality of our present shareholder group"
- "A hyperactive stock market is the pickpocket of enterprise" — frictional costs of trading consume investor wealth equivalent to the combined profits of the four largest Fortune 500 companies
- Buffalo News: #1 weekday penetration among 100 largest US papers, #3 Sunday; 50% news hole (vs. industry avg high 30s)
- See's sales $133.5M, profits $13.7M; poundage stagnant (24.65M lbs); cost control improving under Chuck Huggins
- Blue Chip Stamps merger completed; Berkshire shares outstanding: 1,146,909 vs. 1,137,778 at start of 1965 — "virtually no net dilution"
- GEICO combined ratio 96 vs. industry 111; special $21M distribution from proportional tender
- Structured settlements and loss reserve assumptions introduced as new insurance lines
- Book value: $975.83/share (22.6% CAGR over 19 years); "nothing close to this rate of return can be sustained"

## Relevance

This is arguably the most important single letter for establishing Buffett's investment philosophy in codified form. The 13 principles become the foundation for evaluating all future Berkshire actions. The Economic Goodwill appendix revolutionized how investors think about intangible assets and inflation protection. Nebraska Furniture Mart provides a new case study of an exceptional franchise business. The Buffalo News data demonstrates the franchise value concept in media.
