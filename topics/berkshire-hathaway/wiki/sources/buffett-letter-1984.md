## Summary

The 1984 letter reports a $152.6M gain in net worth (13.6% — well below the historical 22% CAGR) and warns explicitly that the mushrooming capital base is dragging returns. Buffett delivers an extended essay on why share repurchases at below-intrinsic-value are the best possible use of capital — contrasting this with the "odious" practice of greenmail. He introduces a detailed dividend policy framework (restricted vs. unrestricted earnings, the $1-for-$1 retention test). The insurance section is unusually candid about Berkshire's loss-reserving errors and the structural challenges of property/casualty accounting. A major WPPSS bond position ($139M) is analyzed as a business investment — earning 16.3% after-tax, better than most operating businesses available for purchase.

## Key Claims

- Net worth gain of $152.6M ($133/share); book value reaches $1,108.77/share (22.1% CAGR from $19.46 in 1964), but 1984's single-year gain was only 13.6%
- Berkshire needs ~$3.9B in aggregate profits over the next decade to earn 15% annually — "a few big ideas" required; small ones won't do
- The four biggest stock holdings (GEICO, General Foods, Washington Post, Exxon) all engaged in significant share repurchases at prices below intrinsic value
- Greenmail is "odious and repugnant" — three-party exploitation where innocent shareholders fund the extortionist's payoff
- Share repurchases at below-intrinsic-value deliver two benefits: (1) arithmetic — buying $2 of value for $1; (2) signaling — management demonstrates pro-shareholder orientation, raising the market's estimate of future returns
- It has been "over ten years since it has been as difficult as now to find equity investments" meeting both qualitative and quantitative standards — "masterly inactivity" is the hardest strategy
- WPPSS bonds ($139M cost, $22.7M after-tax income, 16.3% return on unleveraged capital) treated as a business: better economics than most operating businesses available for purchase
- In bond markets, investors routinely accept economics they would laugh at in a business context — 1946 AAA tax-exempts at ~1% yield
- Dividend policy framework: "restricted" earnings (consumed by inflation to maintain position) vs. "unrestricted" earnings (genuinely available for distribution); unrestricted earnings should be retained only if they create ≥$1 of market value per $1 retained
- NFM sales reach $115M (one store); Mrs. B receives Honorary Doctorate from NYU at 91; gross margin roughly half of Levitz's 44.4%
- See's: $135.9M revenue, 24.76M lbs; same-store volume declined 1.1%; per-pound realization $5.49 (only 1.4% increase)
- Buffalo News: record profits; 50.9% news hole; #1 weekday and #2 Sunday penetration; dominant newspaper economics are "among the very best in the business world" regardless of editorial quality
- Insurance: combined ratio 134 (third straight year worse than industry at 117.7); loss-reserving errors consistently understated losses for four consecutive years
- GEICO: 36% ownership; $885M direct volume ($320M Berkshire's share); market value $397.3M; Jack Byrne, Bill Snyder, Lou Simpson praised
- Berkshire is "almost without question the strongest property/casualty insurance operation in the country" — financial strength becoming a competitive advantage
- Capital Cities purchase announced March 18, 1985 as subsequent event: 3M shares at $172.50/share

## Relevance

The 1984 letter introduces the dividend policy framework (restricted vs. unrestricted earnings) — a concept that shapes Berkshire's zero-dividend policy for decades. The share repurchase analysis goes well beyond mechanics to explain the signaling value of buybacks. The WPPSS analysis pioneers treating bonds as businesses — a mental model that recurs in later letters. The unflinching loss-reserving confession and the insurance industry analysis ("the corpse is supposed to file the death certificate") provide the most detailed insurance accounting discussion in the letters to date. The Capital Cities announcement foreshadows the largest investment of the 1985 letter.
