## Summary

The 1991 letter opens with Buffett's appointment as Interim Chairman of Salomon Inc following the Treasury auction scandal — which he uses to illustrate Berkshire's management depth. The letter delivers the definitive franchise-vs-business distinction (the media industry analysis), the 20-year See's Candies retrospective as a franchise teaching case, and the acquisition of H.H. Brown with its unusual owner-compensation model. Buffett introduces his "Mistake Du Jour" feature to confess the Fannie Mae omission ($1.4B in forgone gains), extends the look-through earnings framework, and discusses USAir's deepening troubles amid airline industry kamikazes.

## Key Claims

- Book value grew 39.6% to $6,437/share; $19 (1964) → $6,437 (1991), 23.7% CAGR over 27 years
- Growth driven by P/E expansion of Coca-Cola and Gillette: ~$1.6B of $2.1B net worth gain came from these two stocks
- Salomon crisis: Buffett became Interim Chairman August 18, 1991; stepped down after 10 months
- H.H. Brown acquired 7/1/91: leading North American work shoe/boot maker; ~$25M annual pre-tax; Frank Rooney as CEO; $7,800 salary + profit-share compensation system
- See's 20-year retrospective: cost $25M (1972) → $196M revenue; $42.4M pre-tax 1991; only $25M net worth required; $410M cumulative pre-tax profits distributed in 20 years; "untapped pricing power" was the key insight
- Fannie Mae omission: bought 7M shares in 1988 (should have bought 30M at $350-400M); forgone gain by yearend 1991: ~$1.4B
- Look-through earnings 1991: $516M (down 14% from prior year); media, Gillette conversion, and Wells Fargo break-even hurt
- Franchise vs. business distinction: (1) needed/desired, (2) no close substitute, (3) not price-regulated = franchise that can "tolerate mismanagement"; businesses need low cost or tight supply
- Newspaper 6% growth perpetual-annuity thesis debunked: same $1M of earnings now worth $10M (10x earnings) vs. $25M (25x) under old model
- Super-cat: estimated 1991 underwriting profit ~$11M; Japan typhoon was biggest 1991 cat at $4-5B
- Guinness PLC: new $264.8M position; Berkshire's first significant non-U.S. investment
- Wells Fargo: break-even in 1991 as dividends offset negative retained earnings
- Insurance float: $1.895B; cost of funds 6.31%

## Relevance

The franchise-vs-business distinction is one of the most actionable frameworks in all 49 letters — a precise test for why some businesses can sustain pricing power and others cannot. The See's retrospective is the most complete capital-efficiency case study in the corpus. The Fannie Mae confession is the canonical errors-of-omission lesson.
