## Overview

The 1997 letter covers a 34.1% book value gain to $25,488/share — but Buffett deflates the apparent triumph immediately, noting that in a roaring bull market any duck paddles up with the tide. GEICO accelerates explosively (16% policy growth), float reaches $7.1B for the fifth consecutive year at negative cost, and two acquisitions (Star Furniture; International Dairy Queen) expand the retail constellation. The letter introduces a formal confession that Berkshire's stock-for-stock deals have, in aggregate, cost shareholders money — and extends the Mr. Market framework with the hamburger analogy for why ongoing investors should welcome falling prices.

## Key Claims

- Book value gained 34.1% to $25,488/share; 33-year CAGR 24.1%. "Duck rating for 1997: Quack" — passive S&P investors rose almost as fast, and the index bears no tax costs.
- Look-through earnings: $1,930M = $1,292M operating + $743M investee retained − $105M hypothetical tax. Seven major investees: Amex 10.7% ($161M), Coke 8.1% ($216M), Disney 3.2% ($65M), Freddie Mac 8.6% ($86M), Gillette 8.6% ($82M), WaPo 16.5% ($30M), Wells Fargo 7.8% ($103M).
- Float: $7,093.1M (5th consecutive year at negative cost — underwriting profit). 21.7% CAGR since 1967. One-line history: $17.3M (1967) → $7,093M (1997).
- Catastrophe bonds: an "Orwellian misnomer." A true bond obligates the issuer to pay; these instruments lay the provisional promise on the *buyer*. Promoters structured them to circumvent state licensing laws, then labeled them "bonds" to make naive buyers assume low risk. Mispriced risk may go undetected for a decade (75.4% probability of no 12 on a pair of dice in 10 annual rolls).
- GEICO: 16% policy growth (913,176 new policies; 2,949,439 in-force) — "then in 1997, growth jumped to 16%." Underwriting profit 8.1% — too high; goal is 4%; rates reduced 3.3% in 1997. Profit-sharing 26.9% of salary ($71M); marketing budget raised to $100M+ for 1998. Tony Nicely's best year at age 54 after 36 years.
- NICO and other primaries aggregate: 15.0% underwriting profit. Don Wurster (traditional NICO, 32.9% profit), Rod Eldred (homestate, 14.1%), Brad Kinstler (workers' comp, +1.5% 3-yr avg), John Kizer (Central States, record volume), Don Towle (Kansas Bankers Surety, exceeded expectations).
- Lorimer Davidson, GEICO's former CEO, turned 95. The mentor who taught a 20-year-old Buffett insurance for four hours on a Saturday in 1951.
- Unconventional investments: (1) Oil derivatives — 14M barrels remaining (from 45.7M barrel position established 1994–95); $61.9M gain on settled 31.7M barrels; $11.6M unrealized gain at yearend. (2) Silver — 111.2M ounces purchased; $97.4M unrealized pre-tax gain. (3) Zero-coupon Treasuries — $4.6B amortized cost; $598.8M unrealized pre-tax gain as rates fell.
- Ongoing-investor analogy: investors who expect to keep buying stocks should *welcome* falling prices, just as hamburger eaters welcome lower beef prices. "Edit the headline 'Investors lose as market falls' to 'Dis-investors lose as market falls — but investors gain.'"
- Margin of safety warning: "Today's price levels, though, have materially eroded the 'margin of safety' that Ben Graham identified as the cornerstone of intelligent investing." (Dow 7,071, T-bond 5.92%.)
- Stock-for-stock acquisition confession: aggregate of all Berkshire's stock-only mergers (excluding affiliated companies) shows shareholders are "slightly worse off." "When I've issued stock, I've cost you money." The cause: Berkshire's existing roster is .380 hitters; acquiring a .350 hitter by trading a .380 hitter makes no mathematical sense regardless of how attractive the acquiree is.
- Star Furniture acquired July 1, 1997: 12 stores in Houston metro; Melvyn Wolff and sister Shirley Toomin; family since 1924; Irv Blumkin referral. Sellers made large special payments to all employees from their own pockets at closing.
- International Dairy Queen agreed (closed early 1998): 5,792 DQ stores in 23 countries, 409 Orange Julius, 43 Karmelkorn; John Mooty/Mike Sullivan CEOs. Buffett tilted consideration toward cash — only 45% of shares elected cash anyway.
- Convertible preferred retrospective: all four profitable. Salomon merged into Travelers (Deryck Maughan and Bob Denham saved the company); USAir common rose from $4 to $73 ("Next time I make a big, dumb decision... Phone Mr. Wolf"); Gillette $600M → $4.8B; First Empire $40M → $236M.
- Sources table: Operating earnings $1,715.7M pre-tax ($1,194.5M after tax/minority); GEICO underwriting $280.7M; super-cat $283.0M; FlightSafety first full year $139.5M; Buffalo News $55.9M; See's $58.6M.
- Ted Williams batting zone: "Swinging only at balls in his 'best' cell, he knew, would allow him to bat .400... reaching for balls in his 'worst' spot... would reduce him to .230." Berkshire is "swinging at balls just catching the lower outside corner" — will accept low returns or hold and wait.
