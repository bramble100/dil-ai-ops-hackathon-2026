## Summary

The 2021 letter organizes Berkshire around "Four Giants" — insurance, Apple, BNSF, and BHE — as the pillars of intrinsic value. Buffett emphasizes that Berkshire is primarily an infrastructure company ($158B PP&E, #1 in America), not a financial holding company. Share repurchases dominate capital allocation: $51.7B over two years retired 9% of shares, boosting float per share by 25%. Cash sits at $144B ($120B in T-bills) while Buffett waits for opportunities. A long tribute to Paul Andrews (TTI founder, died 2021) becomes the narrative backbone — his choice of Berkshire as TTI's permanent home led directly to the BNSF acquisition.

## Key Claims

- Berkshire owns more US "infrastructure" assets (PP&E $158B) than any other American corporation
- Float grew from $19M (1967) to $147B; 55-year underwriting profit on aggregate; float per share increased 25% over two years to $99,497
- Ajit Jain hired in 1986 with zero insurance experience; still leading 35 years later — "my lucky day"
- Apple: 5.55% ownership; GAAP records only $785M dividends, but Berkshire's share of Apple earnings = $5.6B; Tim Cook praised as "brilliant CEO"
- BNSF: record $6B earnings (2021); 143M miles traveled; 535M tons of cargo; "BNSF will be a key asset for Berkshire and our country a century from now"
- BHE: record $4B earnings (+30x from $122M in 2000); 91.1% Berkshire ownership; Greg Abel credited alongside David Sokol for renewable transformation
- Three paths to value creation: (1) internal growth (best returns, limited scale), (2) public equities (little excites currently), (3) share repurchases (easiest path when price is right)
- $51.7B in buybacks over 2020-2021 retired 9% of shares outstanding; appetite "remains large but will always remain price-dependent"
- "We are not stock-pickers; we are business-pickers" — stated twice
- Paul Andrews (TTI): started with $500, grew to $7.7B revenue, 8,043 employees, 673% earnings growth; died 2021; Berkshire's Fort Worth board meeting led to BNSF acquisition
- Berkshire pays ~$9M daily in federal taxes (vs. $100/day in its textile years); $3.3B in 2021; tax partnership with government emphasized
- Cash: $144B ($120B T-bills) — "not some deranged expression of patriotism"; cash-heavy positions "never pleasant; never permanent"
- Market value CAGR: 20.1% (1965-2021); overall gain 3,641,613%

## Relevance

The letter crystallizes Berkshire's late-era identity: an infrastructure conglomerate with an unmatched insurance float, deploying capital primarily through buybacks when acquisitions and equities offer poor value. The "business-pickers not stock-pickers" formulation and Four Giants framework provide a simplified mental model for understanding Berkshire's value. The Paul Andrews/TTI tribute is one of Buffett's finest entrepreneurial narratives and connects Berkshire's culture of permanent ownership to its most transformative acquisition (BNSF).
