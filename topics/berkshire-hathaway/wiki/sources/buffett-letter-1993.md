## Overview

Buffett reports 14.3% book value growth ($8,854/share), closing 29 years at 23.3% CAGR. Three major themes dominate: the Dexter Shoe acquisition (paid for with Berkshire stock — a decision Buffett will revisit), a landmark treatment of beta vs. real investment risk, and an updated look-through earnings framework showing $856M on the path to the $1.85B year-2000 target.

## Key Claims

- Book value increased 14.3% to $8,854/share; 29-year CAGR from $19 is 23.3%. But market price rose 39%, meaning Berkshire ended 1993 at a higher premium to intrinsic value than it started.
- Dexter Shoe of Dexter, Maine acquired November 7, 1993 for 25,203 shares of Berkshire stock. Harold Alfond (founded 1956 with $10,000) and Peter Lunder. 7.5M pairs/year (mostly made in US — competing successfully against imports). 77 retail outlets; ~15% of US golf shoe output. Projected 1994 shoe operations (Brown + Lowell + Dexter): $550M+ revenue, $85M+ pre-tax earnings. 7,200 Berkshire employees in shoes.
- Dexter sellers received Berkshire shares rather than cash — allowing them to exchange 100% of a single terrific business for a smaller interest in a large group of terrific businesses, with no tax at exchange, and future results parallel to Buffett's.
- Look-through earnings 1993: $856M = $478M reported + $439M undistributed investee earnings − $61M hypothetical tax. 1992 was $604M. Target is $1.85B by 2000 (raised from $1.8B due to share issuance); $856M is "on track."
- Taxes: Berkshire paid $390M in 1993 federal income taxes ($200M from operating earnings, $190M from capital gains) — about 0.5% of all US corporate income taxes. The Li'l Abner deferred-tax demonstration: doubling once at 35% annual tax leaves $22,370 after 20 years; a single investment held and compounded produces ~$130M. Tax deferral on unrealized gains is "an interest-free loan from the U.S. Treasury."
- Insurance float: $2,624.7M at negative cost (underwriting profit of $31M). Super-cat: no losses in 1993 (Midwest floods did not trigger, as flood is privately uninsured), but the same storm that cost $125M in 1992 (Andrew) would cost $600M today given expanded exposure. New reinsurance capacity (~$5B raised by newly formed entities) beginning to cause price deterioration.
- **Beta demolition and real risk framework**: Beta measures past price volatility — useless for assessing investment risk. Washington Post's stock dropped sharply in 1973, making its "beta" higher while its actual business risk fell at the lower price. The true investment risk is whether aggregate after-tax receipts will preserve purchasing power plus a modest return. Five factors: (1) certainty of long-term economic characteristics; (2) management ability to realize potential; (3) management integrity; (4) purchase price; (5) taxation and inflation.
- Concentration vs. diversification: "For a know-something investor, able to understand business economics and to find five to ten sensibly-priced companies with important long-term competitive advantages, conventional diversification makes no sense." Index fund for know-nothing investors; concentration for know-something investors. "Paradoxically, when 'dumb' money acknowledges its limitations, it ceases to be dumb."
- Portfolio (year-end 1993 market values): Coca-Cola $4,168M (93.4M shares, 7.2% ownership), GEICO $1,760M (34.25M shares, 48.4%), Gillette $1,431M (24M shares, 10.9%), Washington Post $440M, Wells Fargo $879M, Capital Cities/ABC $1,239M (2M shares, 13%), Freddie Mac $681M, General Dynamics $401M, Guinness $271M.
- Corporate governance: Three situations — (1) no controlling shareholder (board must act for absentee owners); (2) owner-manager (Berkshire today — board can persuade but not force); (3) controlling non-management owner (Berkshire's future — most effective structure). Howard Buffett joins board 1993; Susie on board earlier. "We're prepared for the truck."
- Mrs. B (Rose Blumkin) turns 100 on December 3, 1993. NFM sales reach $200M — largest home furnishings store in the US. Katharine Graham retires as chairman of Washington Post.

## Source Quality

Primary source: Buffett's direct writing. The risk-framework discussion is one of the most rigorous analytical treatments in the entire corpus. The Dexter acquisition story (paid with stock) is told positively in 1993; the mistake dimension won't be apparent for years.
