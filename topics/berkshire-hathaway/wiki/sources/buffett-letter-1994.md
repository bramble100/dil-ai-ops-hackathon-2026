## Overview

Buffett reports 13.9% book value growth ($10,083/share), closing 30 years at 23% CAGR. The letter delivers three major contributions: a masterclass on book value vs. intrinsic value using Scott Fetzer as a 9-year worked example, the "fat wallet" problem stated definitively, and the "Mistake Du Jour" section confessing both the Cap Cities partial sale (silver) and the USAir preferred investment (gold).

## Key Claims

- Book value +13.9%; $10,083/share; 30-year CAGR 23%. "Future performance won't come close to matching the performance of the past." Net worth reached $11.9B (from $22M at start).
- **Fat wallet problem**: "A fat wallet is the enemy of superior investment results." At $11.9B net worth, a minimum $100M position is required (otherwise negligible impact). The investment universe has shrunk dramatically. Ted Williams: "My argument is, to be a good hitter, you've got to get a good ball to hit... If I have to bite at stuff out of my happy zone, I'm not a .344 hitter."
- Macro-forecast irrelevance: "Thirty years ago, no one could have foreseen the Vietnam expansion, wage/price controls, two oil shocks, presidential resignation, Soviet dissolution, Dow down 508 in a day, or T-bills from 2.8% to 17.4%. But none of these made the slightest dent in Ben Graham's investment principles." Fear is "the foe of the faddist, but the friend of the fundamentalist."
- **Book value vs. intrinsic value**: College education analogy (same cost = same book value, wildly different intrinsic value). Scott Fetzer case: purchased $315.2M for $172.6M book ($142.6M premium). Nine-year table shows earnings growing from $40.3M (1986) to $79.3M (1994) while book value oscillates between $87.9M and $133.3M. By 1994 SF carries at $148.2M on Berkshire's books yet earns twice what it did at acquisition. SF return on equity would rank 4th in Fortune 500 (and #1 if adjusting for bankruptcy-fresh-start anomalies); double the company ranking 10th.
- Purchase-premium amortization: $142.6M premium amortized down to $54.2M by yearend 1994. Non-cash, non-tax-deductible — a "carrying value reduction" that causes Berkshire to report lower earnings and lower book even as Scott Fetzer's intrinsic value grows. "Clearly, the intrinsic value of the business has consistently grown, even though we have just as consistently marked down its carrying value."
- Look-through earnings 1994: $1,030M = $606M reported + $492M undistributed − $68M hypothetical tax. 1993 was $841M (revised from $856M due to different composition). "Modestly ahead of schedule" for the $1.85B year-2000 target, though super-cat results have been above trend-line.
- Insurance float: $3,056.6M at negative cost; second straight year of underwriting profit. Super-cat: "1994 should be regarded as close to a best-case." Only significant losses from LA earthquake in January ($6.8 magnitude; "if it had been 7.5 it would have been a different story"). Wrote $400M single earthquake policy in 1994 — "no one else in the world would take $400M for their own account." Kobe earthquake (early 1995) expected to be nominal loss for Berkshire.
- **Mistake Du Jour**:
  - Silver: Sold 10M Cap Cities shares (late 1993) at $63. Year-end 1994 price: $85.25. Loss vs. holding: $222.5M. "I've become a repeat offender" (previously sold and repurchased Cap Cities in 1978-80). "Maybe it's time to get a guardian appointed."
  - Gold (5-year ripening): USAir $358M preferred — dividend suspended September 1994; written down to $89.5M (25 cents on dollar). "Sloppy analysis" of a high-cost carrier in a deregulated commodity business. "When deregulation came, the capacity of low-cost carriers forced old-line high-cost airlines to cut fares — a day of reckoning only delayed by capital infusions." Bankruptcy has become "a health spa" for airlines (Herb Kelleher). "You don't have to make it back the way you lost it."
- **Compensation analysis**: Scott Fetzer/Ralph Schey as the gold standard. Capital charge in bonus formula: incremental capital earns a bonus only above a hurdle rate; sub-standard returns cost the manager. "Alignment means being a partner in both directions, not just on the upside." Stock options: "combination of ten-year option, low dividend payout, and compound interest can provide lush gains to a manager who has done no more than tread water." Arrangement with Schey "worked out in five minutes, without lawyers or compensation consultants."
- CEO acquisition bias: "Many CEOs attain their positions because they possess an abundance of animal spirits and ego... When encouraged by advisors to make deals, he responds much as would a teenage boy encouraged by his father to have a normal sex life." The "Aw, fellas, all the other kids have one" story.
- Portfolio (year-end 1994): AmEx 27.76M shares at $723.9M cost/$818.9M market (5.5%); Cap Cities 20M shares $345M/$1,705M (13%); Coke 100M shares $1,298.9M/$5,150M (7.8%); Freddie Mac $270.5M/$644.4M; GEICO $45.7M/$1,678.3M (50.2%); Gillette $600M/$1,797M (10.8%); Wells Fargo $423.7M/$984.7M (13.3%); Gannett $335.2M/$365M (4.9%); PNC Bank $503M/$411M (8.3%); Washington Post $9.7M/$419M.
- New: Gannett (4.9%) and PNC Bank (8.3%) appear for the first time as significant positions. PNC already showing a loss ($503M cost, $411M market).
- Dan Burke (Capital Cities) and Carl Reichardt (Wells Fargo) retire — "two CEOs who have done great things for Berkshire shareholders." Both left companies in "outstanding hands."

## Source Quality

Primary source. The Scott Fetzer teaching section is the single most detailed worked example of book value vs. intrinsic value in the entire corpus. The Mistake Du Jour section is unusually candid — particularly on USAir, where Buffett acknowledges sloppy analysis rather than blaming circumstances.
