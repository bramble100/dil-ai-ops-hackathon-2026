## Summary

The 2002 letter is a banner year for Berkshire (+10.0% book value vs. S&P −22.1%) and one of the most intellectually ambitious letters in the corpus. It features the famous "financial weapons of mass destruction" derivatives essay, a scathing corporate governance analysis (boardroom atmosphere, audit committee four questions, stock-option expensing history), and documents the General Re turnaround under Joe Brandon and Tad Montross. Float reaches $41.2B at 1% cost, GEICO delivers $6.9B in premiums with internet growth of 75%, and Ajit Jain's operation holds $13.4B in float. Non-insurance businesses now earn $272M monthly — up from $272M annually a decade earlier. Major acquisitions: Fruit of the Loom, Larson-Juhl, CTB, Garan, and The Pampered Chef. MEHC acquires Kern River and Northern Natural Gas pipelines.

## Key Claims

- **[2002 Letter]** Book value +10.0% to $41,727/share; S&P −22.1%; 32.1 percentage point outperformance. 38-year CAGR 22.2% ($19 → $41,727)
- **[2002 Letter]** Float reaches $41.2B (GEICO $4.7B, General Re $22.2B, Other Reinsurance $13.4B, Other Primary $943M) at 1% cost — down from 12.8% in 2001
- **[2002 Letter]** General Re: $1.31B additional charge for prior-year reserving errors; total 2002 underwriting loss $1.39B. But Joe Brandon and Tad Montross restore underwriting discipline, eliminate NCB (nuclear/chemical/biological) aggregation risk, and reprice aggressively. Gen Re now sole AAA-rated major reinsurer after competitors' downgrades
- **[2002 Letter]** "Derivatives are financial weapons of mass destruction, carrying dangers that, while now latent, are potentially lethal" — the most quoted sentence in the entire letter corpus
- **[2002 Letter]** Gen Re Securities (derivatives dealer) lost $173M in 2002; still 14,384 contracts outstanding with 672 counterparties. Being wound down but "like Hell, both are easy to enter and almost impossible to exit"
- **[2002 Letter]** Mark-to-model degenerates into "mark-to-myth" — derivatives accounting allows both counterparties to show profits simultaneously using fanciful assumptions
- **[2002 Letter]** Daisy-chain risk: derivatives create systemic linkages similar to pre-Federal Reserve banking; there is "no central bank assigned to the job of preventing dominoes toppling in insurance or derivatives"
- **[2002 Letter]** LTCM 1998 as precedent: "a firm unknown to the general public and employing only a few hundred people — could well have posed a serious threat to the stability of American markets"
- **[2002 Letter]** GEICO premiums $6.9B (up from $2.9B in 1996); underwriting profit $416M; internet new business +75%; 19,162 associates received profit-sharing at 19% of salary. Tony Nicely praised for 41-year devotion
- **[2002 Letter]** GEICO 1981-83 umbrella/product liability venture: $72K net premium retained → $94.1M in losses (130,000% loss ratio); $90.3M from deadbeat reinsurers. "So much for 'cheap' reinsurance"
- **[2002 Letter]** Ajit Jain: $13.4B float from a standing start in 1986 with a workforce of only 20. Underwriting profit $534M after $428M retroactive insurance charge. "We would rather earn a lumpy 15% over time than a smooth 12%"
- **[2002 Letter]** Non-insurance pre-tax earnings now $272M monthly — up from $272M annually a decade ago
- **[2002 Letter]** Acquisitions completed: Fruit of the Loom (33% of US men's/boys' underwear, John Holland CEO), Larson-Juhl (Steve McKenzie), CTB (poultry/hog equipment, Vic Mancinelli), Garan (Garanimals, Seymour Lichtenstein), The Pampered Chef ($700M sales, 67,000 kitchen consultants, Doris Christopher founded with $3,000 in 1980)
- **[2002 Letter]** MEHC acquires Kern River pipeline ($1.2B expansion to double throughput) and Northern Natural Gas (16,600 miles; recovered from Enron/Dynegy collapse). MEHC to carry ~8% of all US gas after expansion. HomeServices of America: 2nd largest residential brokerage, $37B transactions (up 100%). Dave Sokol and Greg Abel are "huge assets for Berkshire"
- **[2002 Letter]** Corporate governance: "boardroom atmosphere" sedates fiduciary genes; directors too collegial to challenge CEO pay or foolish acquisitions. Four audit committee questions that would revolutionize accounting oversight. Stock-option expensing: FASB voted 7-0, Senate voted 88-9 against, "so much for independence"
- **[2002 Letter]** "Trumpeting EBITDA is a particularly pernicious practice" — depreciation is paid up front, making it worse than a cash expense, not better
- **[2002 Letter]** Three investor suggestions: (1) weak accounting = weak management elsewhere (cockroach in kitchen); (2) unintelligible footnotes = untrustworthy management; (3) earnings projections/targets invite fabrication
- **[2002 Letter]** Junk bonds: $8.3B deployed; 100:1 gain/loss ratio on equities over 38 years
- **[2002 Letter]** Pro-forma irony: Berkshire's pro-forma earnings were actually lower than reported (no megacat + favorable bond strategies inflated results). "First time in history"
- **[2002 Letter]** Eddie Bennett batboy metaphor: "to be a winner, work with winners"
- **[2002 Letter]** 38 years, never had a single subsidiary CEO elect to leave Berkshire for another employer. Six managers over 75; target: eight in four years
- **[2002 Letter]** Shaw Industries: $424M pre-tax (up from $292M in 2001); Bob Shaw and Julian Saul — "terrific operators"
- **[2002 Letter]** Widow obituary joke: "'Fred Brown died, golf clubs for sale'" — on Berkshire's cost-consciousness

## Relevance

The 2002 letter is one of the five most important in the corpus for its intellectual content. The derivatives essay became prescient — almost exactly describing the mechanisms that caused the 2008 financial crisis six years later. The corporate governance analysis is the most comprehensive treatment of boardroom failure, director independence, and audit committee reform across all 49 letters. General Re's turnaround under Brandon/Montross marks the beginning of the end of the post-acquisition crisis. MEHC's pipeline acquisitions transform it from a regional utility into a major energy infrastructure company.
