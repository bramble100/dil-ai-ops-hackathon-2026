## Summary

Buffett's 1982 letter addresses the decline in reported operating ROE (down to 9.8% from 15.2%) and argues the yardstick has lost utility because the growing importance of unreported "economic" earnings from non-controlled holdings distorts the picture. The letter contains two major teaching essays: (1) a structural analysis of the insurance industry as a commodity business with perpetual overcapacity, and (2) a detailed examination of why equity issuances for acquisitions almost always destroy shareholder value. Book value reaches $737.43/share (22.0% CAGR from 1964). The first formal listing of Berkshire's six acquisition criteria appears. Phil Liesche and Ben Rosner retire.

## Key Claims

- Operating ROE declined to 9.8% from 15.2%, primarily due to insurance underwriting deterioration, expanded equity base, and growing commitment to non-controlled businesses whose retained earnings aren't reported
- Berkshire's share of undistributed 1982 operating earnings from four major holdings (GEICO, General Foods, Washington Post, R.J. Reynolds) exceeded $40M — greater than total reported earnings
- The insurance industry operates under perpetual overcapacity because new capacity requires only "capital plus an underwriter's willingness to sign his name" — no physical plants or long lead times
- Insurance historically earned decent returns through quasi-administered pricing (bureau rates, regulatory negotiations) — that era is ending as new capacity uses price as a weapon
- Only a "megadisaster" (natural or financial) will produce the supply contraction needed for profitability
- GEICO is "a magnificent illustration of the high-profit exception" to the commodity insurance problem — a company with "a wide and sustainable cost advantage"
- Stock issuance for acquisitions is equivalent to selling part of the company — and managers routinely accept terms they'd reject if selling the whole business
- "For gold valued as gold cannot be purchased intelligently through the utilization of gold — or even silver — valued as lead" — on using undervalued shares as acquisition currency
- The "double whammy": dilutive issuances destroy intrinsic value AND cause the market to permanently discount the management's future behavior
- Berkshire's formal acquisition criteria listed for the first time: (1) large ($5M+ after-tax earnings), (2) consistent earning power, (3) good ROE with little debt, (4) management in place, (5) simple businesses, (6) offering price known
- Book value: $737.43/share (22.0% CAGR over 18 years), net worth gain of $208M (40%) during 1982
- "Geometric progressions eventually forge their own anchors" — warning that returns will diminish
- Portfolio market value: $945.6M on $424M cost basis; GEICO alone worth $309.6M
- Blue Chip Stamps merger under consideration; Phil Liesche retires at 65, Ben Rosner at 79

## Relevance

This letter provides two of Buffett's most reusable analytical frameworks: the commodity business structural analysis (overcapacity + undifferentiated product = poor returns) and the equity issuance value-destruction mechanics. The commodity framework applies far beyond insurance — Buffett uses candy bars vs. sugar and C&H as examples. The equity issuance analysis is his most detailed warning against empire-building through dilutive stock deals. The formal acquisition criteria become a recurring feature of future letters.
