## Summary

The 1985 letter — coinciding with Halley's Comet — reports a spectacular 48.2% gain ($613.6M) in net worth, bringing book value to $1,643.71/share. It chronicles four transformative events: the Capital Cities/ABC investment ($517.5M), the Scott & Fetzer acquisition (~$320M), the Fireman's Fund quota-share contract, and the General Foods sale ($338M pre-tax gain). The most emotionally powerful section is the definitive account of the textile business shutdown, including the devastating Burlington Industries comparison and the auction of machinery for scrap ($163,122 from $13M original cost). Buffett delivers a landmark essay on incentive compensation — exposing how fixed-price stock options reward retained earnings, not managerial performance — and contrasts this with Berkshire's unit-based bonus system.

## Key Claims

- Book value reaches $1,643.71/share (23.2% CAGR from $19.46 in 1964); net worth gain of $613.6M (48.2%) — "neither will be seen again in my lifetime"
- Two factors make this rate unachievable: (1) a stock market offering little opportunity vs. the 1964-1984 era; (2) equity capital now 20x what it was a decade ago
- "An iron law of business is that growth eventually dampens exceptional economics" — no company with $1B+ equity has maintained 20%+ ROE while reinvesting all earnings over a decade
- Needs $5.7B over the next decade for 15% CAGR (up from $3.9B last year)
- **Textile shutdown (July 1985):** 21 years of operation; aggregate sales of $530M produced aggregate loss of $10M over the nine years before Buffett's purchase. "I should be faulted for not quitting sooner"
- Burlington Industries: $3B in capex over 21 years; stock at 34 (split-adjusted) vs. 60 in 1964; CPI tripled — each share commands about one-third the purchasing power
- "A good managerial record is far more a function of what business boat you get into than it is of how effectively you row"
- "Should you find yourself in a chronically-leaking boat, energy devoted to changing vessels is likely to be more productive than energy devoted to patching leaks"
- Textile machinery: $13M original cost (incl. $2M spent 1980-84), $866K book value, replacement cost $30-50M → sold at auction for $163,122 gross. Looms bought for $5,000 in 1981 sold for $26 (less than removal costs)
- "The economic goodwill attributable to two paper routes in Buffalo — or a single See's candy store — considerably exceeds" the textile machinery proceeds
- NFM, See's, and Buffalo News combined: $72M pre-tax (vs. $8M fifteen years ago) with only $40M additional capital — average American business needs $5 of additional capital per $1 of additional pre-tax earnings
- Stock option critique: fixed-price options reward retained earnings, not performance; a Rip Van Winkle manager benefits identically to a star performer; options lack the burden of capital costs that real owners face
- Berkshire's compensation: unit-based incentive bonuses with no cap; top managers sometimes receive 5x base salary; no seniority consideration
- Capital Cities/ABC: 3M shares at $172.50/share; Tom Murphy and Dan Burke are "the best of any publicly-owned company in the country"; Buffett gives Murphy voting rights over Berkshire's shares
- Scott & Fetzer: ~$320M acquisition; $700M sales from 17 businesses; World Book Inc. (~40% of sales) sells more encyclopedias than four biggest competitors combined; Ralph Schey praised
- General Foods sale: $338M pre-tax gain; four factors: bargain purchase price, fine underlying economics, able management, buyer willing to pay full value
- Washington Post: bought mid-1973 at ≤¼ of $400-500M intrinsic value; $10.6M cost → $221M total realized; Kay Graham praised for buybacks and managerial skill
- Insurance: combined ratio improves from 134 to 111; premium volume more than tripled; financial strength attracts "flight to quality" buyers
- National Indemnity runs $1M+ minimum premium ad — draws 600 replies, produces ~$50M in premiums
- GEICO: excellent premium growth; Jack Byrne leaves for Fireman's Fund; Bill Snyder (Chairman) and Lou Simpson (Vice Chairman) succeed
- Fireman's Fund: 7% quota-share contract on all business in force; Berkshire holds the float; ~$3B annual FFIC volume
- Market price has recently eliminated the historical discount to intrinsic value; occasionally a modest premium prevails — bad news for new purchasers
- Acquisition criteria raised: minimum after-tax earnings $10M (up from $5M)

## Relevance

The 1985 letter is a landmark for several reasons. The textile closure narrative — complete with the Burlington comparison, the capex trap analysis, and the devastating auction — is Buffett's most thorough case study of why bad businesses destroy capital regardless of management quality. The incentive compensation essay remains one of the most cited critiques of stock options. The Capital Cities and Scott & Fetzer transactions mark Berkshire's shift toward large-scale acquisitions. The insurance section shows Berkshire's financial strength transforming from a passive attribute into an active competitive weapon.
