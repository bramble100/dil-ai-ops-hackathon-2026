## Summary

Reports a strong year (19.4% ROE) and the Diversified Retailing merger, raising Blue Chip ownership to ~58%. The textiles section delivers a textbook explanation of why undifferentiated, capital-intensive businesses earn inadequate returns. Buffett deepens his argument for concentrated equity investing, introduces the idea that retained earnings of investees create long-term value even when unreported, and praises SAFECO as the best-run P&C insurer — bought below book value on the open market when mediocre companies command premiums in negotiated deals.

## Key Claims

- "The textile industry illustrates in textbook style how producers of relatively undifferentiated goods in capital intensive businesses must earn inadequate returns except under conditions of tight supply or real shortage"
- Four criteria restated: "We get excited enough to commit a big percentage of insurance company net worth to equities only when we find (1) businesses we can understand, (2) with favorable long-term prospects, (3) operated by honest and competent people, and (4) priced very attractively"
- Concentrated portfolio policy: "We try to avoid buying a little of this or that when we are only lukewarm about the business or its price"
- SAFECO purchased below book value — "We paid less than 100 cents on the dollar for the best company in the business, when far more than 100 cents on the dollar is being paid for mediocre companies in corporate transactions"
- Retained earnings of well-run investees "may well eventually have a value to shareholders greater than 100 cents on the dollar"
- Pension fund managers invested 122% of net funds in equities in 1971 at full prices, then only 21% in 1974 after the bottom fell out — classic pro-cyclical behavior
- Dow declined from 852 to 805 over three years while Berkshire's unrealized + realized equity gains totaled ~$112M — "a marvelous period for the value-oriented equity buyer"
- Illinois National Bank earning ~2.1% on average assets, about 3x major bank averages

## Relevance

Reinforces the four-filter framework and adds the argument for portfolio concentration. The textiles discussion is the clearest early articulation of why business economics matter more than managerial effort. The SAFECO analysis demonstrates the arbitrage between public market prices and negotiated acquisition prices — a core Berkshire insight.
