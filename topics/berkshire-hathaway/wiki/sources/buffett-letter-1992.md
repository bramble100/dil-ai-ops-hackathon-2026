## Summary

The 1992 letter contains Buffett's most thorough treatise on intrinsic valuation — the growth-vs-value false dichotomy demolished, the John Burr Williams discounted-cash-flow equation stated precisely, and the "slug percentage not batting average" volatility philosophy articulated. Berkshire acquires Central States Indemnity and makes a large new position in General Dynamics (starting as arbitrage, becoming a long-term hold). The letter also contains the most forceful attack on stock-option accounting and a nuanced treatment of two new accounting rule changes (deferred taxes, post-retirement health benefits). Mrs. B returns to the NFM orbit after a three-year competition.

## Key Claims

- Book value grew 20.3% to $7,745/share; $19 (1964) → $7,745 (1992), 23.6% CAGR over 28 years
- Intrinsic value framework: value = discounted cash flows; growth is a variable in that calculation, not a separate concept; "value investing is redundant"
- Growth benefits investors only when incremental returns are "enticing" — otherwise growth hurts
- Four-filter criteria unchanged since 1977 except "very attractive price" → "attractive price" (scale constraint)
- General Dynamics: started as arbitrage, became long-term hold when Bill Anders's strategy was understood; 14% stake in one month
- Central States Indemnity acquired December 1992: ~$90M annual premiums, ~$10M profit, credit-card disability/unemployment coverage; Bill Kizer
- Salomon: Buffett as Interim Chairman stepped down June 1992; Salomon Brothers posted record pre-tax earnings, up 34%
- Hurricane Andrew: largest insured loss in history; ~$125M Berkshire super-cat loss, roughly equal to annual super-cat premium income; GEICO lost ~$50M net ($25M Berkshire share)
- Stock-option accounting: "If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it?" — most forceful statement of position
- Post-retirement health benefits: new GAAP rule requires recognition; Berkshire has inconsequential liability because acquisitions avoided such companies
- Scott Fetzer: record $110M pre-tax on $116M equity; distributed more than 100% of earnings to Berkshire while growing; Ralph Schey
- Mrs. B: at 99, sold her building/land to NFM; will continue running carpet business; Buffett "snapped at" the non-compete she offered
- Secondary market vs. new-issue: secondary market "ruled by mass folly" sets clearing prices with occasional x-for-1/2x opportunities; new-issue ruled by sellers who won't offer bargains
- Look-through earnings 1992: $604M; target $1.8B by year 2000 to sustain 15% growth

## Relevance

The growth-vs-value framework clarification (both are inputs to the same DCF calculation) is the most intellectually complete statement of Buffett's valuation philosophy. The stock-option section is historically significant — Buffett was a decade ahead of the FASB on expensing options. The General Dynamics story illustrates the transition from arbitrage to conviction investing.
