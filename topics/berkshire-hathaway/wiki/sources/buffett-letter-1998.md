## Overview

The 1998 letter reports a 48.3% book value gain to $37,801/share, but Buffett leads with a caveat: the leap is "not as good as it looks" because General Re was acquired for stock. Berkshire's two transformative acquisitions — General Re ($22B, Dec 21) and Executive Jet Aviation ($725M, Aug 7) — define the year. General Re nearly doubles the float to $22.7B overnight and brings the second major reinsurer under the Berkshire umbrella; EJA/NetJets plants Berkshire in the fractional-aircraft business Rich Santulli invented in 1986. GEICO continues its march (market share 3%→3.5%, $143M marketing budget). The letter contains one of Buffett's longest accounting philippics: three interlinked attacks on stock-option expensing, restructuring charges, and pooling-vs-purchase — unified under the label "Son of Gresham: bad accounting drives out good."

## Key Claims

- Book value gained 48.3% to $37,801/share; 34-year CAGR 24.7%. Gain is inflated by General Re stock issuance: intrinsic value per share grew less than stated.
- General Re acquired Dec 21, 1998: largest US property-casualty reinsurer, ~$7B revenues, $22B consideration paid in Berkshire stock. Includes 82% of Cologne Re (Germany). Ron Ferguson CEO. Float reaches $22.7B by yearend ($3.1B GEICO + $4.3B NICO and affiliates + $15.3B General Re). Structural rationale: reinsurers face Wall Street pressure to smooth earnings and limit catastrophe exposure — Berkshire can absorb volatility and write volatile policies that competitors cannot.
- Executive Jet Aviation (EJA / NetJets) acquired Aug 7, 1998 for ~$725M in cash and stock. Rich Santulli invented the fractional-aircraft model in 1986; EJA has 163 aircraft and 1,000+ customers. Largest fractional operator by far. Buffett and Munger are customers who independently concluded it was the best aviation value for non-commercial travelers.
- GEICO: market share 3.0%→3.5%; 32.3% profit-sharing ($103M to 9,313 associates); $143M marketing spend; market-share gains accelerating; Tony Nicely's singular focus credited. "The more we see of GEICO, the more we admire it."
- Scott Fetzer: record year — $96.5M after-tax on $112M net worth (86% ROE). "One of the most undervalued companies in the country when we bought it in 1986."
- CEO 3-point mission: (1) protect extraordinary collection of businesses and managers; (2) continue growth of per-share intrinsic value at rate above S&P average; (3) maintain financial strength/flexibility. "Our favorite holding period is forever — but only when we can't find something to buy at a sensible price."
- Operating earnings: $1,899M (pre-tax and after-tax breakdown in sources table). FlightSafety full year contributed $169.7M; GEICO underwriting $269.6M; super-cat $154.6M; Buffalo News $53.4M; See's $72.0M.
- Stock-option expensing critique: "Alice-in-Wonderland" accounting. If options aren't an expense, what are they? If compensation expense doesn't belong in the income statement, where does it belong? FASB capitulated to corporate lobbying; SEC chair Arthur Levitt publicly agrees with Buffett. "The truth is that options are hugely expensive and that their issuance has cost shareholders of countless companies dearly."
- Restructuring charges golf-score analogy: "Like the golfer who reports his handicap by excluding 'bad' weeks," CEOs back out one-time charges quarter after quarter. "The biggest restructuring charge in recent memory was a $1.7B write-off at a major company — where the following year they announced another $1.4B restructuring." Earnings 'before restructuring charges' become the baseline for option grants and bonuses.
- "Son of Gresham: bad accounting drives out good." Three mechanisms: (1) stock options expensed nowhere; (2) restructuring charges excluded from "core" results; (3) pooling vs. purchase debate lets acquirers avoid goodwill amortization. Companies that adopt honest accounting look worse by comparison and face competitive pressure to cheat. Arthur Levitt's SEC speech calling for reform is cited with approval.
- Pooling vs. purchase accounting: pooling combines balance sheets at book value (goodwill never recognized); purchase creates goodwill amortized over 40 years. Buffett argues goodwill amortization is often economically meaningless (See's goodwill is rising, not decaying), but so is the current FASB rule. The FASB debate will change accounting standards in coming years.
- Super-cat reinsurance: Ajit Jain's 1998 book is highlighted — $154.6M operating earnings, limited catastrophe losses. "Ajit has created tens of billions of dollars of value for Berkshire."
- Catastrophe bond update from 1997: Berkshire participated cautiously; the market continues to misprice these instruments.
- Look-through earnings: suspended this year because General Re was acquired so late (Dec 21) that including partial-year investee earnings would distort the picture. Buffett notes full integration into look-through for 1999.
