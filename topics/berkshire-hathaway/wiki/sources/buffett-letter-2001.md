## Overview

The 2001 letter is defined by September 11: Berkshire absorbed a $2B+ loss, suffered its worst float cost year since 1984 (12.8%), and confronted the full extent of General Re's underwriting failures. Yet the same year delivered GEICO's recovery to profitability, six new acquisitions (all cash), and some of the most lucid insurance thinking Buffett ever wrote. The three underwriting principles, the experience-vs.-exposure framework, and the "Noah rule" are the intellectual centerpieces.

## Key Claims

**Book value and performance:**
- Net worth loss $3.77B; per-share book value -6.2% to $37,920. 37-year CAGR 22.6%
- S&P also fell -11.9%; relative advantage +5.7 percentage points
- "Per-share intrinsic grew somewhat faster than book value during these 37 years, and in 2001 it probably decreased a bit less"
- "Future: we won't come close to replicating our past record... we were then working with far less money than we now have. Some years back, a good $10 million idea could do wonders for us... Today, the combination of ten such ideas and a triple in the value of each would increase the net worth of Berkshire by only ¼ of 1%. We need 'elephants' to make significant gains now — and they are hard to find."

**September 11 and insurance:**
- Float cost 12.8% in 2001 — "worst year in float cost since 1984"; total underwriting loss ~$4.3B (General Re: $4.3B pre-tax loss alone)
- "I allowed General Re to take on business without a safeguard I knew was important, and on September 11th, this error caught up with us"
- XTRA tender offer expiration date was September 11; Berkshire completed the transaction despite stock market closure; Lew Rubin's son-in-law escaped the World Trade Center

**Three principles of insurance underwriting (most explicit statement ever):**
1. Accept only risks evaluable within circle of competence, after assessing all relevant factors including remote loss scenarios, with expectancy of profit — ignore market-share considerations
2. Limit business to guarantee no aggregation of losses from a single event that threatens solvency — "ceaselessly search for possible correlation among seemingly-unrelated risks"
3. Avoid moral risk: "No matter what the rate, trying to write good contracts with bad people doesn't work"

**Experience vs. exposure — the fundamental underwriting insight:**
- "We made a fundamental underwriting mistake by focusing on experience, rather than exposure, thereby assuming a huge terrorism risk for which we received no premium"
- Exposure-based (not experience-based) underwriting required for: (a) terrorism, (b) D&O insurance at market peaks (few suits when stocks rise, but exposure exploding), (c) any new or emerging risk class
- "Using experience as a guide to pricing is not only useless, but actually dangerous" in certain contexts

**The Noah rule:**
> "Predicting rain doesn't count; building arks does." — 2001 Letter

Buffett confessed: he recognized the terrorism risk before September 11 but did not convert thought into action. "I consequently let Berkshire operate with a dangerous level of risk — at General Re in particular."

**General Re rehabilitation:**
- $800M of loss costs from prior years finally recognized in 2001 — not new losses, but "losses that were there all along; what developed was management's understanding"
- General Re broke all three underwriting rules: wrong circle of competence (terrorism), aggregation failure, and severe underreserving creating miscalculated costs
- Joe Brandon named CEO in September; Tad Montross as president
- "Joe has many of Jack [Welch]'s characteristics: smart, energetic, hands-on"

**"Loss development" accounting critique:**
- "Loss development" is a misnomer: "What developed was management's understanding of the losses"
- Recommended replacement: "loss costs we failed to recognize when they occurred (or maybe just 'oops')"
- Insurance accounting is "a self-graded exam" — auditors certify numbers they know are likely to differ dramatically from true earnings
- Discounting reserves would be "folly" — reserves are always subject to unanticipated developments; discounting would provide "a new tool for companies that are inclined to fudge"
- General Re's $800M catch-up: an honest mistake, but caused woefully inadequate pricing for years and premature incentive compensation/taxes

**Ajit Jain post-September 11:**
- $578M South American refinery coverage above $1B threshold
- $1B non-cancelable third-party liability coverage for international airlines
- £500M North Sea oil platform property coverage
- Sears Tower coverage including terrorism above $500M threshold
- World Cup Soccer and 2002 Winter Olympics
- "At Berkshire, we retain our risks and depend on no one. And whatever the world's problems, our checks will clear."
- "It's impossible to overstate his value to Berkshire."

**GEICO 2001 recovery:**
- Premium volume +6.6%; float +$308M to $4.25B; underwriting profit $221M — "actually paid that amount to hold float"
- Preferred customers +1.6%; standard/non-standard fell 10.1%; total policies fell 0.8%
- New business improving (closure rate from phone inquiries up; internet business growing)
- Tony Nicely: "an owner's dream"

**Float reaches $35.5B:**
- GEICO $4.25B, General Re $19.3B, Other Reinsurance $11.3B, Other Primary $685M
- "Our cost has been less than zero in about half of the years in which we've operated — that is, we've actually been paid for holding other people's money. Over the last few years, however, our cost has been too high, and in 2001 it was terrible."

**Six acquisitions (all cash):**
1. Shaw Industries (completed January 8, 2001; also bought out 12.7% minority for 4,740 BRK shares → 100% owned)
2. Johns Manville (completed February 27, 2001)
3. MiTek ($600M+?; world's leading "connector plate" producer for roofing trusses; Gene Toombs CEO; 55-person management team bought 10% with own cash)
4. XTRA (truck trailer leasing; Lew Rubin CEO; Julian Robertson Tiger Fund liquidation)
5. Larson-Juhl (custom picture frames; Craig Ponzio grew $3M → $300M; 18,000 framing shops in US; industry leader in Canada and Europe; $250M?)
6. Fruit of the Loom (pending creditor approval; John Holland CEO; Buffett bought FotL debt at 50% of face value earning ~15% current return before bidding for the company)

**Building products group first year:**
- Shaw + Johns Manville + Benjamin Moore + Acme Brick: ~$659M pre-tax combined
- Shaw Industries revenues ~$4B; now Berkshire's largest non-insurance operation

**Dexter Shoe — three confessions:**
> "I've made three decisions relating to Dexter that have hurt you in a major way: (1) buying it in the first place; (2) paying for it with stock and (3) procrastinating when the need for changes in its operations was obvious." — 2001 Letter
- Shoe operations lost $46.2M pre-tax; Dexter now under Frank Rooney / Jim Issler (H.H. Brown)

**MidAmerican Energy expansion:**
- Swapped English properties for Yorkshire Electric (2.1M customers) → now 2nd largest UK electric utility with 3.6M UK customers
- Also owns 2nd-largest US residential real estate brokerage (Ron Peltier); ~106,000 transactions, ~$20B property value annually
- "This may surprise you" — significant capital absorber with regulated predictable returns

**R.C. Willey Las Vegas:**
- Henderson (adjacent to Las Vegas) store outsells all others in the R.C. Willey chain — twice what Buffett had anticipated
- Bill Child: closed Sundays despite conventional wisdom; Buffett was wrong to be skeptical about Boise, and even more wrong about Las Vegas

**Corporate governance critique:**
- Enron "has become the symbol for shareholder abuse, there is no shortage of egregious conduct elsewhere"
- Buffett/Munger: "We will not take cash compensation, restricted stock or option grants that would make our results superior to yours"
- "Charlie and I are disgusted by the situation, so common in the last few years, in which shareholders have suffered billions in losses while the CEOs... have walked away with extraordinary wealth"
- The options-repricing joke: CEO at party purrs, "I'll do anything you want — just tell me what you'd like." Reply: "Reprice my options."

**MiTek ownership model:**
- 55 managers bought 10% with own cash (minimum $100K each, many borrowed): "all of these managers are true owners. They face the downside of decisions as well as the upside. They incur a cost of capital. And they can't 'reprice' their stakes."

**Distressed debt:**
- FINOVA: Berkshire and Leucadia formed "Berkadia" — borrowed $5.6B via FleetBoston; Berkshire guaranteed 90%; credit arrangement yielding ~2% spread; loans reduced from $3.9B level as of letter date
- Fruit of the Loom bonds: 10% of senior debt at ~50% of face, earning ~15% current return; expected return ~70% of face

**Risk tolerance articulation:**
- "We are perfectly willing to lose $2 billion to $2½ billion in a single event if we have been paid properly for assuming the risk that caused the loss (which on that occasion we weren't)"
- Berkshire's unique advantages: massive liquid resources, substantial non-insurance earnings, favorable tax position, knowledgeable shareholder constituency

**37-year manager retention record:**
- "We now have completed 37 Berkshire years without having a CEO of an operating business elect to leave us to work elsewhere"
