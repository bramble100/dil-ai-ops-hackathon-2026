## Summary

The 1988 letter covers a year of superb operating earnings ($418M pre-tax) and introduces several landmark topics: a complete framework for evaluating arbitrage, a forceful takedown of Efficient Market Theory, the Coca-Cola and Freddie Mac investments, the Borsheim's acquisition, and a GAAP transparency manifesto. Sainted Seven earn 67% on equity. Book value rises 20% to $2,974.52 (23.0% CAGR). Berkshire lists on the NYSE.

## Key Claims

- Net worth gain $569M (20.0%); book value $2,974.52/share (23.0% CAGR over 24 years)
- Intrinsic business value "significantly exceeds" book value; in 1988, book value grew faster
- Scale drag warning: 15% annual return now requires $10.3B over the next decade (up from $3.9B four years ago)
- Sainted Seven earn 67% on average equity capital with no financial leverage — up from 57% in 1987
- Coca-Cola: 14,172,500 shares purchased in 1988 at $592.5M cost; market value $632.4M at yearend
- Freddie Mac: 2.4M preferred shares at $71.7M cost; market value $121.2M — maximum allowed by law
- Borsheim's: 80% interest acquired early 1989 — "an outstanding business run by people we like, admire, and trust"
- Arbitrage framework: four questions to evaluate any announced-event opportunity — (1) probability of completion, (2) capital duration, (3) chance of a better outcome, (4) downside if event fails
- Rockwood & Co. (1954): Buffett's first arbitrage at age 24 — cocoa beans for stock certificates, subway tokens as only expense
- RJR Nabisco: $281.8M position, ultimately $64M pre-tax profit
- Efficient Market Theory demolished: 63-year arbitrage record (Graham-Newman → Buffett Partnership → Berkshire) averaging 20%+ annually — $1,000 would have grown to $97M vs. $405K at market's 10%
- NYSE listing (Nov 29, 1988): goal is narrower spreads and lower transaction costs, not stock promotion; "we do not want to maximize the price at which Berkshire shares trade" — want price centered on intrinsic value
- CEO accountability gap: an incompetent secretary is fired; an incompetent CEO keeps his job because the Board never measures itself
- GAAP critique: three key questions financial statements should answer — (1) what is the company worth? (2) can it meet obligations? (3) how good is management? — supplementary four-sector presentation introduced
- David L. Dodd eulogy: "Everything he taught me, directly or through his book, made sense"
- Proposition 103 (California): threatens GEICO's 10% California premium volume; socialized auto insurance risk
- Insurance: combined ratio 104 (statutory); reserve development favorable for second straight year; volume declining; Fireman's Fund 7% contract expires August 1989
- "Favorite holding period is forever" — first use of this formulation, applied to Coca-Cola and Freddie Mac
- "Too much of a good thing can be wonderful" (Mae West) — on concentrated investing
- Superstar managers: no mandatory retirement; Mrs. B at 95; "newly-minted MBAs... short on personal commitment"

## Relevance

The 1988 letter is foundational for two topics previously unaddressed: **arbitrage** (a complete investor education on the discipline, with the Arcata Corp. case study and four-question framework) and the **demolition of EMT** (using 63 years of documented arbitrage returns). The Coca-Cola purchase marks the beginning of Berkshire's most famous common stock investment. The "favorite holding period is forever" formulation becomes one of Buffett's most-quoted lines. The GAAP manifesto — three questions, four-sector supplementary reporting — advances the [owner earnings](../principles/owner-earnings.md) principle into practical corporate governance.
