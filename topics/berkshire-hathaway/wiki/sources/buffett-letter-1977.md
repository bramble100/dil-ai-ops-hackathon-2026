## Summary

Buffett's first letter as Chairman reports operating earnings of $21.9M (19% ROE on beginning equity) and introduces several foundational themes. Textiles remain poor; insurance — led by Phil Liesche at National Indemnity — delivers outstanding results. Buffett articulates his four stock selection criteria for the first time and emphasizes return on equity over earnings-per-share growth as the correct performance yardstick. The letter highlights the importance of being in businesses with structural tailwinds and names the key equity holdings: Washington Post, Capital Cities, GEICO, and others.

## Key Claims

- ROE on beginning equity capital is the appropriate measure of single-year performance, not EPS growth — "even a totally dormant savings account will produce steadily rising interest earnings each year because of compounding"
- Four equity selection criteria: (1) understandable business, (2) favorable long-term prospects, (3) honest and competent management, (4) very attractive price
- "One of the lessons your management has learned — and, unfortunately, sometimes re-learned — is the importance of being in businesses where tailwinds prevail rather than headwinds"
- Insurance companies' only products are promises — no patents, trademarks, or location advantages — so individual managers have outsized impact on performance
- Pro-rata portions of outstanding businesses sometimes sell in securities markets at large discounts vs. negotiated full-company acquisitions
- Insurance premiums grew from $22M (1967) to $151M (1977) without issuing additional Berkshire shares — almost 600% growth funded by operations
- See's Candies pre-tax earnings grew from $4.2M to $12.6M since 1972 purchase, with little additional capital — in a no-growth industry
- Capital Cities: "we can obtain a better management result through non-control than control"

## Relevance

The founding document of the Berkshire shareholder letter tradition. Establishes return on equity as Buffett's primary metric, articulates the four-filter framework for stock selection, and introduces the tailwinds-vs-headwinds principle that would recur for decades. First public statement of the philosophy that minority stock ownership of great businesses can outperform controlling ownership of mediocre ones.
