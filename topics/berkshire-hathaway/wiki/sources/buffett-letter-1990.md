## Summary

The 1990 letter formally coins and defines "look-through earnings" as Berkshire's primary performance metric, quantifying the $220M+ in investee retained earnings invisible to GAAP. Buffett analyzes the media industry's structural decline — not a cyclical dip but a permanent franchise erosion — and establishes Wells Fargo as a major new equity position despite a chaotic bank-stock market. The letter also delivers a comprehensive demolition of junk bonds using the "dagger on the steering wheel" metaphor, and articulates the float-cost framework for evaluating insurance operations.

## Key Claims

- Book value grew 7.3% to $4,612.06/share; $19.46 (1964) → $4,612 (1990), 23.2% CAGR over 26 years
- Look-through earnings 1990: ~$590M = $371M reported operating + $250M investee retained earnings − $30M hypothetical tax
- Four permanent holdings renamed "permanent four": Capital Cities/ABC, Coca-Cola, GEICO, Washington Post
- Wells Fargo: $290M for ~10%; Carl Reichardt and Paul Hazen; 20%+ ROE; 1.25% return on assets; "best managers in the business"
- Insurance float: $1.637B; cost of funds 1.63% vs. 8.24% long-term government bonds
- Super-cat capacity: $250M per-event; Berkshire "largest U.S. writer" of super-cat business
- Junk bonds: "a plan for disaster" — dagger-on-steering-wheel metaphor; "margin of safety" remains the right three words 42 years after Graham
- Media businesses "will prove considerably less marvelous than I, the industry, or lenders thought" — structural, not cyclical
- Non-insurance operations: 51% after-tax return on average equity in 1990 — top 20 of 1989 Fortune 500
- Borsheim's: sales up 18% in poor retail year; 18% operating costs vs. ~40% for competitors
- RJR Nabisco bonds: $440M position; yield compensates for risk; distinct from "manufactured junk"
- Convertible preferreds: Gillette upgrading to common April 1; USAir "worth substantially less" than cost

## Relevance

This letter marks the formal adoption of look-through earnings as Berkshire's yardstick, replacing reported operating earnings as the primary intrinsic value measure. The media analysis is the first full acknowledgment that franchise economics can deteriorate — directly applicable to valuing any "moat" business. The Wells Fargo analysis is a master class in buying a leveraged business through temporary panic.
