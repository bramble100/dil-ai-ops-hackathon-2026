## Summary

The 1987 letter is one of Buffett's most quotable. It opens by demonstrating that Berkshire's seven largest non-financial units earned a combined 57% after-tax return on equity capital — a figure only six of the Fortune 1,000 matched over the prior decade. The letter then presents the full Mr. Market parable from Ben Graham, one of the most important passages in the entire corpus. The October 1987 crash is addressed via a devastating critique of "portfolio insurance" (farm and house analogies). The three permanent holdings — Capital Cities/ABC, GEICO, Washington Post — are formally identified as "a permanent part of Berkshire rather than merchandise." The CEOs-and-capital-allocation analysis (Carnegie Hall/Federal Reserve) explains why most managers destroy value. Berkshire's debt philosophy is stated: "acceptable results under extraordinarily adverse conditions, rather than optimal results under normal conditions." The $700M Salomon Inc convertible preferred is the year's largest investment.

## Key Claims

- Book value grew 19.5% to $2,477.47/share; 23.1% CAGR over 23 years
- Seven non-financial units earned $180M pre-tax on $175M equity — combined after-tax ROE of ~57%
- Fortune study: only 25 of 1,000 companies averaged >20% ROE for a decade with no year below 15%; 24 of 25 outperformed the S&P 500. Most used very little leverage and operated mundane businesses.
- "The best business returns are usually achieved by companies that are doing something quite similar today to what they were doing five or ten years ago"
- "Severe change and exceptional returns usually don't mix"
- **Mr. Market parable** (full Graham allegory): your business partner names a daily buy-sell price driven by emotion, not fundamentals. "Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you." "If he shows up some day in a particularly foolish mood, you are free to either ignore him or to take advantage of him."
- "In the short run, the market is a voting machine but in the long run it is a weighing machine" — Ben Graham
- Portfolio insurance: "$60 billion to $90 billion of equities were poised on this hair trigger in mid-October." Farm/house analogies: would you sell off farmland because a neighbor sold at a lower price?
- "Volatility caused by money managers who speculate irrationally with huge sums will offer the true investor more chances to make intelligent investment moves"
- "Our goal is to find an outstanding business at a sensible price, not a mediocre business at a bargain price. Charlie and I have found that making silk purses out of silk is the best that we can do; with sow's ears, we fail."
- Three permanent holdings formally designated: Capital Cities/ABC ($517.5M cost, $1,035M market), GEICO ($45.7M cost, $756.9M market), Washington Post ($9.7M cost, $323.1M market)
- Permanent holdings combined: $573M cost → $2.1B market; but only $11M in reported earnings (GAAP dividend accounting)
- CEOs and capital allocation: "it's as if the final step for a highly-talented musician was not to perform at Carnegie Hall but, instead, to be named Chairman of the Federal Reserve"
- After ten years, a CEO retaining 10% of net worth annually has deployed >60% of all capital at work
- Controlled companies give Berkshire two advantages: (1) capital allocation rights, (2) tax advantages (80%+ ownership = full consolidation)
- Debt philosophy: "both foolish and improper to risk what is important... for some extra returns that are relatively unimportant"
- "If you want to shoot rare, fast-moving elephants, you should always carry a loaded gun" — maintain liquidity for future opportunities
- $250M debentures issued at ~10%; currently earning 6.5% on the proceeds — $160K/week negative carry, willingly accepted
- Insurance: commodity economics — "hundreds of competitors, ease of entry, product that cannot be differentiated"
- Berkshire's two insurance differentiators: (1) unmatched financial strength, (2) total indifference to volume
- Social/judicial inflation: industry revenues must grow ~10% annually just to break even
- Combined ratio 105 (statutory); float growing faster than premiums — improving profitability even with rising ratio
- Salomon Inc: $700M 9% convertible preferred, convertible at $38/share after three years. Trust in CEO John Gutfreund.
- Arbitrage: annual pre-tax returns "of at least 25%"; only large, publicly announced deals; no greenmail plays
- WPPSS bonds: $240M cost, $316M market value, $34M annual tax-exempt income
- Trade deficit: "claim checks" piling up with foreigners; government finance follows Blanche DuBois approach; inflation pressure from fiscal profligacy
- Texaco bonds ($104M cost, $119M market) purchased after bankruptcy — worst-case analysis suggested capital preservation
- Washington Post purchased in 1973 at $5.63/share; 1987 per-share operating earnings $10.30 — current earnings nearly double purchase price
- GEICO purchased 1976-80 at average $6.67/share; 1987 per-share after-tax operating earnings $9.01

## Relevance

The 1987 letter contributes three landmark teachings: (1) the Mr. Market parable — the most frequently cited passage in the entire 49-year corpus; (2) the CEOs-as-capital-allocators insight, which explains a structural flaw in corporate governance; and (3) the permanent-holdings philosophy, which distinguishes Berkshire from every other conglomerate. The portfolio insurance critique, written just months after Black Monday, is characteristically contrarian — Buffett sees the crash as caused by institutional lunacy, not fundamental failure.
