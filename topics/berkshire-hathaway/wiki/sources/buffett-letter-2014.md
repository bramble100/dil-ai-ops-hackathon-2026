## Summary

The 50th anniversary "Golden Anniversary" letter is the most ambitious in the series — comprising Buffett's regular 2014 operating review, a major retrospective essay ("Berkshire – Past, Present and Future") covering Berkshire's entire history and forward-looking predictions, and Charlie Munger's independent letter codifying the "Berkshire system" into 15 elements. The operating year was strong: book value +8.3% to $146,186, Powerhouse Five earned a record $12.4B, float reached $83.9B (12 consecutive profitable years, $24B cumulative), and 31 bolt-on acquisitions totaling $7.8B were contracted. Buffett formally adds market price to the performance table, acknowledging book value's growing inadequacy as an intrinsic value proxy.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share grew 8.3% to $146,186; 50-year CAGR of 19.4% (vs. S&P 500 at 9.9%); market price 50-year CAGR of 21.6%
- Powerhouse Five (BHE, BNSF, IMC/Iscar, Lubrizol, Marmon) earned record $12.4B pre-tax, up $1.6B from 2013; only BHE owned a decade ago
- Float reached $83,921M with $2.7B underwriting profit — 12 consecutive profitable years totaling $24B cumulative
- GEICO market share reached 10.8% (vs. 2.5% in 1995); Tony Nicely completed 53 years of service
- BHSI (commercial insurance) expanded internationally under Peter Eastwood; predicted to reach billions in volume within years
- National Indemnity: $111B GAAP net worth, largest of any insurer in the world; $3B single-premium reinsurance policy written
- BNSF disappointed customers with service failures; $6B capex committed for 2015 (~26% of revenues, nearly 50% more than any railroad's single-year record)
- Berkshire Hathaway Energy (renamed from MidAmerican): AltaLink $3B (Canadian) acquired; 6% US wind / 7% US solar; 11-state retail utility; 100% earnings retention
- Van Tuyl Automotive acquired: 78 dealerships, $9B sales, fifth-largest US auto group; Larry Van Tuyl and Jeff Rachor
- Duracell acquisition contracted (to close 2015) via exchange of Procter & Gamble stock; placed under Marmon
- 31 bolt-on acquisitions contracted, $7.8B aggregate, ranging from $400K to $2.9B
- Big Four (AmEx 14.8%, Coke 9.2%, IBM 7.8%, Wells Fargo 9.4%): $4.7B combined look-through earnings (vs. $3.3B three years prior); $42B unrealized gains; each 0.1% ownership increase = $50M additional earnings share
- Bank of America warrants: 700M shares at $5B exercise, worth $12.5B at yearend; likely exercised before September 2021 expiry
- Tesco position fully exited: $444M after-tax loss; "cockroach" lesson on dawdling when management sours
- Todd Combs and Ted Weschler each given oversight of a new subsidiary as Chairman — "make them even better investors"
- Record $15B subsidiary capex, ~90% in the US; 340,499 employees (25 at HQ)
- Manufacturing/service/retail segment: $97.7B revenue, $6.8B pre-tax, 18.7% after-tax ROE on $24B tangible assets
- Non-real amortization charges: $1.15B total, ~80% "non-real" — will increase with further acquisitions
- Clayton Homes: 30,871 homes sold (45% of US manufactured homes vs. 14% at 2003 acquisition); $13B mortgage portfolio; blue-collar borrowers outperformed higher-income counterparts in the crisis
- 3G Capital/Heinz partnership praised; Tim Hortons/Burger King financing role; more partnerships expected
- Book value now materially understates intrinsic value because operating businesses carried at cost never revalued upward; market price added to performance table as better long-term proxy
- Two quantitative intrinsic value factors: per-share investments $140,123 (+8.4%); per-share non-insurance earnings $10,847 (+19%)
- Five building blocks for future growth: (1) improve existing earnings, (2) bolt-on acquisitions, (3) investee growth, (4) share repurchases at meaningful discount, (5) occasional large acquisitions
- Acquisition criteria minimum raised to $75M pre-tax earnings; ideal acquisition range $5-20B
- Berkshire as permanent home for sellers: third choice beyond competitors (who fire people) and private equity (who pile on debt); "equity is a dirty word for many private-equity buyers"
- Conglomerate defense: Berkshire's form enables tax-free, frictionless capital reallocation; spin-offs destroy value (LTV/Jimmy Ling parable); investment bankers' "mental flexibility" on control premiums critiqued
- Volatility ≠ risk: 50-year stock/dollar comparison (S&P 11,196% vs. dollar −87%); currency-denominated instruments "far riskier" than diversified equity portfolios over decades; leverage condemned
- Index fund endorsement deepened: Jack Bogle's _The Little Book of Common Sense Investing_ recommended; institutional investors underperform "the unsophisticated index-fund investor"
- "Borrowed money has no place in the investor's tool kit" — Berkshire stock fell ~50% three times since 1965
- Financial staying power requires three strengths: (1) large reliable earnings stream, (2) massive liquid assets ($20B+ minimum in T-bills always), (3) no significant near-term cash requirements
- "Cash is to a business as oxygen is to an individual"; never substitute claimed-liquid instruments for T-bills
- Derivatives interest ended: newly-written contracts require full collateralization, eliminating profit potential
- ABCs of business decay: arrogance, bureaucracy, complacency — "the cancers that metastasize even the strongest companies"
- Succession: Howard Buffett suggested as non-executive Chairman (safety valve); CEO successor identified — "will do a better job than I am doing in certain important respects"; Todd Combs and Ted Weschler on investment team
- Munger's 15-point Berkshire system codified: extreme subsidiary autonomy, tiny HQ, float-funded investing, no stock options, no budgets, no HR/PR/IR/strategy departments, cash acquisitions, CEO as capital allocator
- Munger names Ajit Jain and Greg Abel as "world-leading" successors — "In some important ways, each is a better business executive than Buffett"
- Munger's biggest error: errors of omission — "Berkshire's net worth would now be at least $50 billion higher" if it had seized several near-certain opportunities
- Munger's four factors for Berkshire's success: (1) Buffett's constructive peculiarities, (2) the Berkshire system, (3) good luck, (4) intense devotion of shareholders/admirers
- Buffett's Wooden analogy via Munger: concentrating all playing time on the best players; Buffett "out-Woodened Wooden" by concentrating skill in one person for 50 years
- Berkshire's competitive advantages: (1) unmatched business collection, (2) outstanding managers, (3) extraordinary earnings diversity and financial strength, (4) first-choice ranking for sellers, (5) rock-solid culture
- Historical retrospective: $11.375 tender offer from Seabury Stanton; Berkshire textile purchase as "monumentally stupid" cigar butt; NICO for Berkshire (not BPL) as "$100 billion" mistake; Charlie's blueprint: "Forget buying fair businesses at wonderful prices; instead, buy wonderful businesses at fair prices"
- See's Candies retold: $25M purchase, $1.9B cumulative pre-tax earnings, only $40M additional investment; "rabbits breeding"
- Dexter Shoe cost revised to $5.7B (current value of Berkshire shares used for payment); "deserves a spot in the Guinness Book of World Records"
- 98% of B-share votes rejected dividend resolution (660.8M to 13.9M)

## Relevance

This is the most comprehensive letter in the series — a definitive retrospective on 50 years of Berkshire combined with Munger's independent assessment. It provides the richest material for principle pages (capital allocation, business quality, Mr. Market/volatility), the most explicit succession roadmap, the strongest private equity critique, the fullest defense of the conglomerate form, and several landmark data points (50-year CAGR, See's $1.9B, Dexter $5.7B, float $84B). Munger's letter is invaluable for its codification of the "Berkshire system" and its naming of Jain and Abel as successors.
