## Summary

The 2012 letter reports a $24.1B total gain (14.4% book value increase to $114,214/share, 48-year CAGR 19.7%) but notes this underperformed the S&P for the ninth time in 48 years — and warns the five-year winning streak may soon snap. Three major essays anchor the letter: the dividend policy analysis (mathematical proof that sell-off beats dividend for a high-ROE company), the newspaper economics essay (28 dailies acquired for $344M despite secular decline), and the Heinz announcement ($12B investment with Jorge Paulo Lemann). The Powerhouse Five top $10.1B pre-tax; float reaches $73.1B with ten consecutive profitable years; record $9.8B capex and $2.3B in bolt-on acquisitions (26 companies) demonstrate Berkshire's capital deployment machine at full throttle.

## Key Claims

- Book value $114,214/share (+14.4%); 48-year CAGR 19.7%; overall gain 586,817%
- Total gain $24.1B; $1.3B used for share repurchases; $22.8B net worth increase
- Heinz: $12B (50% with Jorge Paulo Lemann; $4B common equity each + $8B 9% preferred + 5% warrants)
- Powerhouse Five (BNSF, Iscar, Lubrizol, Marmon, MidAmerican): $10.1B aggregate pre-tax, ~$600M more than 2011
- Float $73.1B (up from $70.6B); ten consecutive underwriting profit years; $18.6B cumulative pre-tax
- GEICO market share 9.7% (from 2.5% in 1995 under control); $16.7B premiums; Hurricane Sandy: largest single loss in history (46,906 vehicles), >3x Katrina
- Ajit Jain: $35B float + significant cumulative underwriting profit; "a feat that no other insurance CEO has come close to matching"
- General Re: "enthusiastic about international life reinsurance business"
- Todd Combs and Ted Weschler: each outperformed S&P by double-digit margins; portfolios increased to ~$5B each; first $1B Todd/Ted holding (DIRECTV) appears in Berkshire's portfolio list
- Record $9.8B capex (88% in US, 19% above 2011's record); record $2.3B bolt-on acquisitions (26 companies)
- Five building blocks for per-share value: (1) improve subsidiary earnings, (2) bolt-on acquisitions, (3) investee growth, (4) repurchase at discount, (5) occasional large acquisition; rarely issue shares
- Dividend policy essay: mathematical proof that sell-off policy outperforms dividend for a company earning 12% on equity trading at 125% of book. Buffett's own case: gave away 4¼% annually for seven years; book value of remaining shares grew from $28.2B to $40.2B despite lower share count
- Share repurchase limit raised from 110% to 120% of book value (Dec 2012; large block available at ~116%)
- Newspaper acquisition: 28 dailies for $344M; local news still indispensable; pay model emerging (Arkansas Democrat-Gazette exemplar); "Survival of the Fattest" → Internet destroyed
- Stan Lipsey retires as publisher of Buffalo News after 30+ years; story of 1977 Sunday edition battle and four-year near-death experience
- BNSF: $20.8B revenue, $6.0B operating earnings, $5.4B pre-tax (9.6x interest coverage); 15% US ton-miles; 500K barrels/day oil transport (~10% lower 48); $4B planned capex 2013
- MidAmerican: $1.96B operating earnings; 89.8% owned; 6% US wind generation; solar projects to reach 14% US capacity; $13B total renewables portfolio cost; Greg Abel
- Marmon: to 90% ($700M additional write-off); implied $12.6B value for 90%; ~$4.6B excess over book; 150 companies; Union Tank Car/Procor: 97,000 tank cars ($4B net book); UTLX logo
- Clayton: 25,872 homes (+13.5%); ~4.8% of all US single-family; 332,000 mortgages ($13.7B); America's #1 homebuilder
- BNSF + Marmon benefiting from US oil production resurgence
- HomeServices: $42B in home sales (+33%); Prudential/Real Living franchise operations acquired (67%); rebranding to "Berkshire Hathaway HomeServices"
- Guard Insurance acquired (workers comp, ~$300M premiums)
- Big Four (AmEx 13.7%, Coke 8.9%, IBM 6.0%, Wells Fargo 8.7%): $3.9B share of earnings; $1.1B dividends received; $26.7B unrealized gains
- Wells Fargo: 456.2M shares, $15.6B value; "non-real" amortization of core deposits ~$1.5B/year
- Intangible amortization essay: $600M in section; ~20% "real," 80% "non-real" (customer relationships, etc.); IBM's "adjusted operating earnings" as model
- Derivatives winding down: credit-protection contracts expire next year (~$1B profit); equity puts: $3.9B settlement value (down from $6.2B), $7.5B GAAP liability
- Investment record: per-share investments grew 19.4% CAGR since 1970; per-share non-insurance earnings grew 20.8% CAGR
- "America's destiny has always been clear: ever-increasing abundance"; first stock purchase spring 1942 during Pacific war setbacks
- 288,462 total employees (up 17,604); headquarters: 24 people; 21,500-page federal tax return

## Relevance

The dividend essay is Buffett's definitive statement on why Berkshire doesn't pay dividends — backed by mathematical proof and his own personal example of growing wealth while giving away shares. The newspaper essay extends the franchise-erosion analysis from 1990-91 into the Internet era, while providing an investment thesis for local papers. The Heinz deal (announced Feb 2013, included in this letter) introduces the Jorge Paulo Lemann partnership model. The five building blocks codify Berkshire's capital allocation playbook. The intangible amortization discussion extends the owner-earnings framework to distinguish real from phantom expenses.
