## Summary

The 2003 letter documents Berkshire's strong recovery — book value +21.0% to $50,498/share, float $44.2B at zero cost with $1.7B underwriting profit, and General Re finally fixed ($145M profit). It features two signature acquisitions: Clayton Homes (from reading Jim Clayton's autobiography, gifted by Tennessee students) and McLane ($23B sales from Wal-Mart, completed in 29 days with no due diligence). Buffett enters the foreign currency market for the first time ($12B in five currencies), warns about the US trade deficit, and provides his most comprehensive treatment of Berkshire's own governance — "owner-capitalism" with directors holding $4M+ each, no options, no D&O insurance. The mutual fund scandal validates his 2002 warnings about director independence.

## Key Claims

- **[2003 Letter]** Book value +21.0% to $50,498/share; S&P +28.7%; −7.7pp relative. 39-year CAGR 22.2% ($19 → $50,498). Intrinsic value "somewhat exceeded" 22.2% growth because Berkshire morphed from "a struggling northern textile business" to "a widely diversified enterprise worth far more than book"
- **[2003 Letter]** Float reaches $44.2B (GEICO $5.3B, General Re $23.7B, Other Reinsurance $13.9B, Other Primary $1.3B) at zero cost — $1.7B pre-tax underwriting profit, all segments positive
- **[2003 Letter]** General Re FIXED: $145M underwriting profit (from −$1.39B in 2002 and −$3.67B in 2001). "Gen Re is fixed. Thank Joe Brandon." AAA sole survivor among major reinsurers after competitor downgrades
- **[2003 Letter]** GEICO market share reaches 5.0% (from 2.1% in 1992 under Tony Nicely); premiums $8.1B (from $2.2B in 1992). Preferred policyholders +8.2%, standard/non-standard +21.4%. Buffalo customer service center announced (2,500 jobs, Governor Pataki instrumental)
- **[2003 Letter]** Ajit Jain: PepsiCo $1 billion prize policy ($250M present-value exposure); $1.4B underwriting profit (ex-retroactive). "If we ever put a photo in a Berkshire annual report, it will be of Ajit. In color!"
- **[2003 Letter]** Clayton Homes acquired (Aug 7): manufactured housing industry's "class act." Origin: Tennessee finance students gave Buffett Jim Clayton's autobiography. Kevin Clayton (CEO) called; deal based "solely on Jim's book, my evaluation of Kevin, the public financials of Clayton and what I had learned from the Oakwood experience." Clayton begins retaining loans instead of securitizing: "every tub on its own bottom" philosophy. Agreed to buy Oakwood assets
- **[2003 Letter]** McLane acquired (May 23): $23B sales, ~1% pre-tax margins; grocery/nonfood distribution to convenience stores, mass merchandisers, restaurants. Wal-Mart deal: single 2-hour meeting with CFO Tom Schoewe, handshake, money in 29 days, no due diligence. "We knew everything would be exactly as Wal-Mart said it would be — and it was." Byron Trott (Goldman Sachs) now instrumental in three Berkshire acquisitions
- **[2003 Letter]** Operating earnings compounded growth table (1968-2003): $2.69/share → $3,531.32/share; 22.8% CAGR from 1968, 28.2% from 1998
- **[2003 Letter]** Foreign currency: first-ever positions, $12B at yearend in five currencies. Bearish on dollar due to trade deficit. "The cemetery for seers has a huge section set aside for macro forecasters"
- **[2003 Letter]** Gen Re Securities still unwinding: 7,580 tickets with 453 counterparties (from 23,218 with 884 in early 2002). Pre-tax loss $99M in 2003. Buffett confession: "I'm sure I could have saved you $100 million or so, pre-tax, if I had acted more promptly to shut down Gen Re Securities"
- **[2003 Letter]** Mutual fund scandal vindicates 2002 governance warnings: fund management companies trampled shareholder interests to boost already-enormous profits. "Prior to their transgressions... these management companies were earning profit margins and returns on tangible equity that were the envy of Corporate America"
- **[2003 Letter]** Berkshire governance — "owner-capitalism": 11 directors each with $4M+ in Berkshire stock (family holdings); no options, no restricted shares, no D&O liability insurance. "Who ever washes a rental car?" Four new directors: David Gottesman, Charlotte Guyman, Don Keough, Tom Murphy. Primary board job: select Buffett's successor from four internal candidates
- **[2003 Letter]** Taxes: $3.3B paid for 2003 (top-10 US taxpayer). "If only 540 taxpayers paid the amount Berkshire will pay, no other individual or corporation would have to pay anything to Uncle Sam." Pamela Olson (Treasury) exchange. 2002 return: 8,905 pages, seven feet tall
- **[2003 Letter]** Designated Gifts Program terminated — abortion-related boycotts hurt Pampered Chef's independent kitchen consultants. "We see nothing charitable in harming decent, hard-working people just so we and other shareholders can gain some minor tax efficiencies"
- **[2003 Letter]** MEHC: $1.076B pre-tax earnings (up from $790M). Pipelines now carry 7.8% of US natural gas. HomeServices: $48.6B in transactions (up from $37B), 16 brokerage firms, 16,343 brokers, 16 states. UK utilities: 6,800 → 2,539 employees since 1990 denationalization, same customers, more electricity — "a victory for those who believe that profit-motivated managers will find efficiencies that government never will"
- **[2003 Letter]** NetJets: $41M pre-tax loss; $32M aircraft inventory writedown; 70% industry share by value; European losses continue; Mayo Clinic Executive Travel Response program. Rich Santulli praised
- **[2003 Letter]** FlightSafety: $150M "normal" operating earnings (down from $183M); $37M simulator obsolescence charge; $1.2B original simulator cost (3x nearest competitor). Business aviation slowdown
- **[2003 Letter]** Fruit of the Loom: 42.3% of men's/boys' underwear at mass marketers; women's/girls' share 11.3% → 13.9%
- **[2003 Letter]** NFM Kansas City store: "country's largest-volume home-furnishings store"; 25+ acres of parking; President Bush claimed paternity. Mrs. B quote: "If you have the lowest price, customers will find you at the bottom of a river"
- **[2003 Letter]** Shaw: record earnings $436M pre-tax; Dixie Group acquisition (+$240M sales → ~$5B total)
- **[2003 Letter]** Manufacturing/retail group: 20.7% return on average tangible net worth; $32.1B revenues
- **[2003 Letter]** Cottage cheese analogy: "About as far as we will go down that path is to occasionally eat cottage cheese a day after the expiration date on the carton" — on credit risk tolerance
- **[2003 Letter]** PetroChina: 1.3% stake (2,339M shares) at $488M cost; $1,340M market value at yearend
- **[2003 Letter]** Junk bonds: $8B purchased in 2002 now repriced upward. "Yesterday's weeds are today being priced as flowers"
- **[2003 Letter]** Book recommendations: Bull! (Maggie Mahar), The Smartest Guys in the Room (McLean/Elkind), In an Uncertain World (Rubin), The Intelligent Investor revised edition (Zweig)

## Relevance

The 2003 letter marks the turning point from the post-bubble hangover: General Re is fixed, float is costless, operating earnings are at record levels, and Berkshire's acquisition machine is firing. Clayton Homes introduces manufactured housing finance — a business that will grow to $5B+ and previews Berkshire's later expansion into consumer lending. McLane demonstrates the Berkshire acquisition advantage at scale: no due diligence, fast close, permanent home. The foreign currency position is unusual — Buffett's first-ever macro bet, and one he approaches with appropriate humility. The governance essay introduces "owner-capitalism" as a formal concept.
