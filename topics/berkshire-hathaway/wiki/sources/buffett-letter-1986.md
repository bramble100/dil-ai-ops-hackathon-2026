## Summary

The 1986 letter introduces Berkshire's management philosophy at length — attracting great managers and then staying out of their way ("neither would get a lot of directives from me about how to swing"). Buffett wrestles with capital allocation at scale: operations generate cash but equities are overpriced. The Fechheimer Bros. acquisition (uniform maker, ~$55M) arrives via letter from a shareholder. The insurance section articulates the commodity pricing cycle with vivid metaphor ("not more statesmen, but less corn"). GEICO delivers a 96.9 combined ratio with 16% premium growth. The Tax Reform Act of 1986 receives the most extensive single-topic treatment in any letter to date. The appendix introduces the formal owner earnings equation — (a) reported earnings + (b) depreciation/amortization − (c) required capital expenditure — and demolishes the "cash flow" fallacy. The iconic "be fearful when others are greedy" line appears for the first time.

## Key Claims

- Berkshire's book value grew 26.1% to $2,073.06/share; 23.3% CAGR over 22 years with shares outstanding up less than 1%
- Operating earnings rose to $131.5M (after-tax) from $92.9M; security sales contributed $150.9M
- Fechheimer Bros. acquired: 84% purchased at ~$55M total valuation; uniform manufacturing and distribution, founded 1842, Heldman family three generations deep
- Fechheimer mirrors the NFM acquisition pattern: family sellers wanted a permanent non-reselling buyer; Berkshire visited neither headquarters
- Capital allocation at $2B+ net worth is the "considerably more important challenge" — but nothing attractive to buy in equities
- "Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy only when others are fearful" — stated as investment policy
- GEICO: 41% owned, combined ratio 96.9, premium volume up 16%; expense ratio 23.5% vs. 38%+ for peers
- GEICO's cost advantage is "a kind of moat that protects a valuable and much-sought-after business castle"
- Lou Simpson's GEICO equity portfolio: 23.7% to 45.8% annual returns (1980-86); "consistently invested in undervalued common stocks"
- Insurance pricing cycle: premium growth must exceed ~10% annually to hold profitability (social/judicial inflation); the 1986 boom is already fading
- "Not more statesmen, but less corn" — in commodity insurance, calling for pricing discipline is futile; the only cure is reduced capacity
- NHP, Inc.: $23.7M for ~50% of multifamily housing developer/manager; 500 properties, 80,000 units, $2.5B cost
- Marketable securities: $700M in medium-term tax-exempt bonds; no equities attractive at current prices
- Tax Reform Act of 1986: corporate rate drops 46% → 34% (helps franchise businesses); capital gains rate rises 28% → 34% (hurts); insurance companies face accelerated tax payments, reduced dividend/bond exclusions, General Utilities Doctrine repeal
- Tax incidence framework: strong franchises keep tax cuts (no regulator or competitive force to pass through); commodity businesses pass cuts to customers; property/casualty is a special case (non-uniform tax equations among competitors)
- "Stockholders as a whole and over the long term must inevitably underperform the companies they own" due to transaction and management costs
- "Bull markets can obscure mathematical laws, but they cannot repeal them"
- **The Owner Earnings Equation (Appendix):** Owner earnings = (a) reported earnings + (b) depreciation, depletion, amortization, and other non-cash charges − (c) required annual capex. "Cash flow" (a+b without subtracting c) is meaningless for manufacturing/retail/extractive businesses
- Scott Fetzer example: "Company O" (pre-acquisition) earns $40.2M; "Company N" (post-acquisition) earns $28.6M — identical economics, only the accounting differs. Owner earnings are the relevant figure.
- Corporate jet purchased for $850K used — self-deprecating admission after years of criticizing them
- Managers: "If each of us hires people who are smaller than we are, we shall become a company of dwarfs" (Ogilvy)
- Working with people you like: "marrying for money — probably a bad idea under any circumstances, but absolute madness if you are already rich"

## Relevance

This letter is foundational on three fronts: (1) the owner earnings formula gets its definitive statement, settling the valuation methodology that underpins everything Berkshire does; (2) the insurance commodity cycle analysis is the most complete version — the corn metaphor explains why discipline alone can't fix structural overcapacity; (3) the tax incidence framework (franchise vs. commodity) creates a durable analytical tool. The Fechheimer acquisition establishes the pattern: Berkshire's annual-report "ad" attracts family-owned businesses seeking a permanent home.
