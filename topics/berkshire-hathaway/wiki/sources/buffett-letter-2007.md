## Summary

The 2007 letter is anchored by the landmark "Great, Good, and Gruesome" essay — Buffett's most structured taxonomy of business quality — using See's Candies, FlightSafety, and the airline industry as archetypes. Net worth grew $12.3B (+11%); book value reached $78,008 per share (43-year CAGR 21.1%). The Marmon Group was acquired for $4.5B (60% initially) on Christmas Day in a handshake deal with the Pritzker family. Housing bubble concerns permeate the letter. Insurance float reached $58.7B with $3.37B underwriting profit. The letter also contains an extended derivatives disclosure (94 contracts in two categories), a pension-return-assumptions exposé, and the confession that a Tom Murphy NBC-station offer was foolishly turned down.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share: $78,008 (+11.0%); 43-year CAGR 21.1%; overall gain 400,863% vs. S&P 6,840%
- Marmon Group acquisition: 60% for $4.5B cash (Christmas Day), remaining ~40% over six years via formula price; $7B revenue, 20,000 employees, 125 businesses in nine sectors; Union Tank Car owns 94,000 rail cars ($5.1B original cost); Byron Trott facilitated; Jay Pritzker (met 1954 at Rockwood Chocolate) → Bob Pritzker → family reorganization
- **Great/Good/Gruesome business taxonomy:** "savings accounts" metaphor — great = high rate that rises (See's); good = attractive rate earned on additional deposits (FlightSafety); gruesome = inadequate rate requiring ever-more deposits (airlines)
- See's 35-year retrospective: purchased 1972 for $25M ($8M capital required, 60% pre-tax ROE); 2007 sales $383M, pre-tax profits $82M; only $32M reinvested since 1972; cumulative pre-tax earnings $1.35B; "Adam and Eve" cash generation metaphor
- FlightSafety capital-intensive comparison: 1996 pre-tax $111M on $570M fixed assets; 2007 pre-tax $270M but capex $1.635B vs. $923M depreciation; net fixed assets now $1.079B; good but not See's-like returns on incremental investment
- Airlines as gruesome: "a farsighted capitalist present at Kitty Hawk would have done his successors a huge favor by shooting Orville down"
- Dexter Shoe cost revised: $433M in stock = 25,203 A shares, now worth $3.5B (1.6% of company); "the worst deal that I've made"
- Tom Murphy NBC/Dallas-Fort Worth station confession: turned down $35M offer; station earned $73M pre-tax in 2006; cumulative earnings since >$1B; capital value ~$800M; "my brain had gone on vacation"
- GEICO market share 7.2% (up from 2.5% at 1995 acquisition); ad spending $31M → $751M; motorcycle share 2.1% → 6%; entered ATV, RV, commercial auto
- General Re restored under Joe Brandon/Tad Montross — "first-class business in a first-class way"
- Ajit Jain: built specialty reinsurance from scratch since 1986; Equitas early experience good
- Insurance five-year table: float $44.2B (2003) → $58.7B (2007); underwriting profit $1.7B → $3.37B
- Derivatives: 94 contracts — (1) 54 high-yield bond default CDS ($3.2B premiums received, $472M losses paid, $4.7B max remaining); (2) 40 equity index puts on S&P 500 + 3 foreign indices ($4.5B premiums, $4.6B liability, 15-20 year terms, exercisable only at expiration)
- PetroChina sold: bought 2002-03 for $488M (1.3% of company valued at $37B); sold 2H 2007 for $4B when company reached $275B; tax footnote: $1.2B IRS payment funded US government for ~4 hours
- MidAmerican: $2.09B pre-tax; $35.05/share purchase price anecdote (Buffett's "one-price" approach, grudging nickel concession); 2007 earnings $15.78/share (normalized $15.01)
- Per-share investments $90,343; per-share non-insurance pre-tax earnings $4,093
- Two-yardstick reframed: per-share investments (CAGR declining: 42.8% → 25.6% → 14.3%) and per-share non-insurance earnings (accelerating: 11.1% → 19.1% → 23.5%)
- Pension return assumptions: S&P companies average 8%, requiring 9.2% equity returns after fees on 72% equity allocation; 20th century Dow 5.3% CAGR; "beware the glib helper who fills your head with fantasies while he fills his pockets with fees"
- Stock-option backdating scandal discussed; 1994 FASB/Senate history revisited (only 2 of 500 S&P companies chose preferred expensing route)
- Moat essay: "a truly great business must have an enduring moat"; low-cost producer (GEICO, Costco) or powerful brand (Coke, Gillette, AmEx); "Roman Candles" = companies whose moats proved illusory; business that requires a superstar ≠ great business (Mayo Clinic vs. solo surgeon)
- Succession: 3 CEO candidates, 4 investment manager candidates identified (young to middle-aged, well-to-do to rich)
- Housing bubble: "swimming naked when the tide goes out"; weakened lending practices; widespread pain
- Trade deficit: $2B daily IOUs; Brazilian real 100→199 (2002-07); Amazon euro bonds profit
- Richline Group formed: jewelry supplier, Dennis Ulrich
- Manufacturing group: 23% return on average tangible net worth; Shaw/Acme/JM/MiTek hurt by housing (combined earnings $941M vs. $1.3B in 2006); See's profits +50% under Brad Kinstler; Borsheim's +15.1%; NFM record ~$400M each store; Iscar Dalian (China) plant; NetJets 487 US + 135 Europe planes

## Relevance

The 2007 letter provides Buffett's most structured framework for categorizing business quality (Great/Good/Gruesome), with the See's vs. FlightSafety vs. Airlines comparison becoming a canonical teaching tool. The Marmon acquisition demonstrates Berkshire's "buyer of choice" advantage in its purest form — a Christmas Day handshake deal with no advisors and no nit-picking. The derivatives disclosure introduces two major new themes that will become critical in 2008: equity index puts and high-yield CDS. The housing bubble commentary proves prescient.
