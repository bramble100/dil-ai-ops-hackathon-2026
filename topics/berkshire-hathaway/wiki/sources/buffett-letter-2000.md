## Overview

The 2000 letter covers a year of contrasts: Berkshire outpaced a falling S&P 500 (+6.5% book value vs. -9.1% S&P), completed eight acquisitions totaling ~$8B (97% cash), and added 58,000 employees — yet float costs ballooned to 6%, GEICO growth stalled, and Buffett confessed that his Dexter Shoe acquisition was a compounded error. The letter contains Buffett's fullest articulation of his universal valuation framework: Aesop's "birds in the bush" as the timeless DCF equation.

## Key Claims

**Book value and performance:**

- Net worth gain $3.96B; per-share book value +6.5% to $40,442. 36-year CAGR 23.6%
- Outpaced S&P 500 (-9.1%), but "intrinsic value moderately exceeded its gain in book value"
- Look-through earnings 2000: $2,415M ($1,779M reported operating + $740M undistributed investees − $104M tax)

**Eight acquisitions (~$8B total; 97% cash, 3% stock):**

1. MidAmerican Energy (~$2B; 76% stake, one-line consolidation due to PUHCA; "would have been $5B greater revenues if fully consolidated")
2. CORT Business Services (~$386M cash to Wesco; Paul Arnold CEO; national leader in "rent-to-rent" furniture)
3. U.S. Liability and sister companies (half cash/half stock; Tom Nerney CEO; "excess and surplus lines"; rare combination of excellent growth and unusual profitability)
4. Ben Bridge Jeweler (half cash/half stock; Ed and Jon Bridge, fourth generation; 65 stores, West Coast; seven consecutive years of same-store sales gains)
5. Justin Industries ($570M cash; Western boot brands [Justin, Tony Lama, Nocona, Chippewa] plus Acme Brick — 11.7% of national brick output; John Justin died Feb 2001)
6. Shaw Industries (~87.3%; ~$4B sales; Bob Shaw and Julian Saul; world's largest carpet manufacturer)
7. Benjamin Moore ($1B cash; 117-year paint maker; thousands of independent dealers; Richard Roob / Yvan Dupuy)
8. Johns Manville (~$1.8B cash; Dec agreement; nation's leading commercial/industrial insulation maker; asbestos bankruptcy backstory; Jerry Henry CEO)

**Acquisitions advantage:**

- "We are often the buyer of choice for the seller" — emotional attachment by owners signals honest accounting, product pride, customer respect
- LBO buyers disadvantaged: junk bond market dried up; all-equity analysis made Berkshire more competitive
- "How much better it is for the 'painter' of a business Rembrandt to personally select its permanent home than to have a trust officer or uninterested heirs auction it off"

**Float and insurance:**

- Float grows to $27.9B (GEICO $3.9B, General Re $15.5B, Other Reinsurance $7.8B, Other Primary $598M)
- Float cost 6% — underwriting loss $1.6B; "not happy with its cost"
- Retroactive reinsurance explained: "pain-today, gain-tomorrow" — accept large first-year loss in exchange for long-duration float; Ajit Jain's domain
- Ajit Jain: $2.4B single premium (retroactive UK policy — possibly largest in history); Texas Rangers / Alex Rodriguez disability policy ($252M contract); Grab.com $1B prize insurance

**GEICO slowdown:**

- Policy growth halted: 1,472,853 new policies vs. 1,648,095 in 1999; policies-in-force grew only 4,696,842 from 4,328,900
- Advertising not cost-effective: Buffett admits "I told you last year that we would get our money's worth from stepped-up advertising at GEICO in 2000, but I was wrong"
- Four factors: ad frequency overdone; low-hanging fruit already picked; stricter underwriting required; State Farm's aggressive pricing undercut competitive dynamics
- State Farm float cost estimated at 23%; GEICO 6.1% — "the willingness of the largest player in the industry to tolerate such a cost makes the economics difficult for other participants"
- But: GEICO has "much the better business model" — low cost makes it "inevitable" over time; Tony Nicely praised

**Aesop's universal valuation formula:**

- "A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush" — Aesop, 600 B.C.
- Three questions: (1) How certain that birds are in the bush? (2) When will they emerge and how many? (3) What is the risk-free interest rate?
- "IBT — Inefficient Bush Theory" — occasionally even conservative estimates reveal price startlingly low relative to value
- "Common yardsticks such as dividend yield, P/E or P/B... have nothing to do with valuation except to the extent they provide clues to the amount and timing of cash flows"
- Growth as dual-natured: "usually a plus, sometimes a minus — in the value equation"
- Speculation: "focus is not on what an asset will produce but rather on what the next fellow will pay for it"
- Tech bubble diagnosis: "Cinderella at the ball... they are dancing in a room in which the clocks have no hands"

**Full reporting and accounting:**

- EBITDA critique: "does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?"
- CEO growth predictions: "more money, it has been noted, has been stolen with the point of a pen than at the point of a gun"
- Snowballing accounting: once a company moves earnings between periods, subsequent shortfalls require "accounting maneuvers that must be even more 'heroic' — can turn fudging into fraud"
- WSJ Conseco/Finova error episode: media prints rumors as facts; First Amendment allows printing anything; journalism's first principle should require scrupulousness

**Dexter Shoe:**

- "I clearly made a mistake in paying what I did for Dexter in 1993. Furthermore, I compounded that mistake in a huge way by using Berkshire shares in payment."
- All remaining accounting goodwill from Dexter written off in 2000

**Scott Fetzer — Ralph Schey retires:**

- Ralph Schey retires end of 2000 after 15 years running Scott Fetzer
- During his tenure: distributed $1.03B to Berkshire against net purchase price of $230M
- "Ralph belongs in Berkshire's Hall of Fame"

**Berkshire preferences — whole businesses vs. stocks:**

- 80%+ subsidiary tax advantage: $1M in earnings → no additional tax if upstreamed; vs. 14% on dividends / 35%+ on capital gains for <80% holdings
- "Preferring to purchase businesses rather than stocks" has been in Owner's Manual since 1983

**50-year Ben Graham anniversary:**

- "A few hours spent at the feet of the master proved far more valuable to me than had ten years of supposedly original thinking"
