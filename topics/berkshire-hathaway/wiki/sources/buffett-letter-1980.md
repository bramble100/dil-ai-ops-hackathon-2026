## Summary

Reports $41.9M operating earnings (17.8% ROE) and develops the concept of "non-controlled ownership earnings" at length — explaining how GAAP ignores retained earnings of sub-20% holdings, making "less than half of our earnings iceberg" visible. The GEICO analysis is a centerpiece: $47M cost, ~$20M earning power, replacement cost $200M+ — demonstrating the power of buying outstanding businesses at market prices. Distinguishes true turnarounds from businesses with a "localized excisable cancer" (GEICO, American Express). Explains how unrealized bond losses force insurers into "asset maintenance underwriting." Closes with a moving tribute to Gene Abegg, who ran Illinois National Bank for nearly fifty years.

## Key Claims

- Non-controlled ownership earnings: retained earnings of sub-20% holdings don't appear in GAAP results, but "the value of those retained earnings is determined by the use to which they are put" — "If a tree grows in a forest partially owned by us, but we don't record the growth in our financial statements, we still own part of the tree"
- Unreported ownership earnings from investees now exceed Berkshire's total reported operating earnings
- GEICO: $47M cost, ~$20M share of earning power, $105M market value; to buy comparable earning power outright would cost $200M minimum
- GEICO and American Express (1964 salad oil scandal) are not true turnarounds but "extraordinary business franchises with a localized excisable cancer"
- "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for poor fundamental economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact"
- Share buybacks praised: "if a fine business is selling in the market place for far less than intrinsic value, what more certain or more profitable utilization of capital can there be?"
- Insurers with unrealized bond losses are forced into "asset maintenance underwriting" — writing business at any price to avoid selling bonds at a loss
- Waumbec closure: "Your Chairman made a costly mistake in not facing the realities of this situation sooner"
- Acquisition preference: "businesses that generate cash, not those that consume it"
- Book value from $19.46 to $400.80 (16 years), 20.5% CAGR — "achieved despite many mistakes"
- Gene Abegg tribute: "From the time we first met, Gene shot straight 100% of the time — the only behavior pattern he had within him"

## Relevance

The definitive statement of the "owner earnings" concept that became central to Buffett's valuation framework. The GEICO analysis is the best early example of his "wonderful business at a fair price" approach. The turnaround distinction (excisable cancer vs. fundamental disease) is a powerful diagnostic tool. The "asset maintenance underwriting" concept explains structural industry dysfunction.
