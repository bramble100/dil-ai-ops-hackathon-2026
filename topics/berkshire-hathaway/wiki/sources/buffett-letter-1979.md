## Summary

Reports 18.6% ROE and book value growth from $19.46 (1964) to $335.85 — a 20.5% CAGR over fifteen years. Introduces the "investor's misery index" (inflation rate + tax drag) and warns that 20% compounded returns may yield zero real purchasing power at 14% inflation. Contains the famous admission that "turnarounds seldom turn" after the Waumbec Mills acquisition mistake. Features a long critique of long-term bond investing in an inflationary world, using the irony that insurers won't price auto policies for 12 months but will lend money for 30 years. Phil Fisher's restaurant analogy explains Berkshire's shareholder constituency philosophy.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share: $19.46 (1964) → $335.85 (1979) = 20.5% CAGR; achieved with low leverage and no significant share issuance
- "Investor's misery index": inflation rate + tax rate on nominal gains; when this exceeds ROE, real capital shrinks even with zero consumption
- At 14% inflation, a 20% pre-tax return leaves "very close to zero" real after-tax purchasing power
- "Turnarounds seldom turn, and the same energies and talent are much better employed in a good business purchased at a fair price than in a poor business purchased at a bargain price" — reflecting on Waumbec
- Long-term bonds are the "last major fixed price contract of extended duration still regularly initiated in an inflation-ridden world"
- Insurance underwriting discipline: Phil Liesche "if business makes sense, he writes it; if it doesn't, he rejects it" — policy to not lay off workers despite volume fluctuations
- Phil Fisher restaurant analogy: corporations attract the shareholder constituency they seek, like a restaurant attracts diners based on its menu — can't be all things to all people
- "Your Chairman has a firm belief that owners are entitled to hear directly from the CEO" — no delegation of annual reports to PR staff
- Network TV stations earn "extraordinary returns on tangible capital" almost unavoidably — selling at "one thousand cents or more on the dollar"
- Predicts combined ratios of ~105 average for the industry over next five years

## Relevance

The inflation analysis is among Buffett's most important intellectual contributions — the "investor's misery index" framework shows how inflation and taxes conspire to destroy real returns. The Waumbec admission introduces the "turnarounds seldom turn" principle. The long-bond critique demonstrates Buffett's ability to identify structural mispricing across asset classes.
