## Summary

Reports $39.7M operating earnings (15.2% ROE — declining as equity base grows) and delivers two of Buffett's most celebrated analyses. The "General Acquisition Behavior" section introduces the toad-and-prince metaphor, categorizes successful acquirers into two types (inflation-adapted businesses and managerial superstars like Tom Murphy), and confesses Buffett's own mediocre acquisition record. The "Equity Value-Added" section argues that with long-term tax-exempt bonds yielding 14%, most American businesses earning 14% on equity actually destroy value for individual owners after taxes and inflation — making them economically "bad" businesses. Introduces the shareholder-designated charitable contributions program, conceived by Charlie Munger.

## Key Claims

- "Investors can always buy toads at the going price for toads. If investors instead bankroll princesses who wish to pay double for the right to kiss the toad, those kisses had better pack some real dynamite. We've observed many kisses but very few miracles."
- Three motivations for overpaying acquisitions: (1) animal spirits, (2) size worship over profitability, (3) belief in the transformative managerial kiss
- Two categories of successful acquirers: Category 1 — businesses that can raise prices easily and grow with minimal capital; Category 2 — managerial superstars (Heineman, Singleton, Zaban, Murphy)
- Tom Murphy at Capital Cities: "a real managerial 'twofer'" (Category 1 focus + Category 2 talent)
- "We would rather buy 10% of Wonderful Business T at X per share than 100% of T at 2X per share"
- "Inflation acts as a gigantic corporate tapeworm. That tapeworm preemptively consumes its requisite daily diet of investment dollars regardless of the health of the host organism"
- With 16%+ bond yields and 14% corporate ROE, "a typical American business is no longer worth one hundred cents on the dollar to owners who are individuals"
- Parable of the Talents (Matthew 25:14-30) applied to capital allocation: high-return businesses should retain earnings; low-return businesses should pay them out
- Non-controlled ownership earnings in just four holdings (GEICO, General Foods, R.J. Reynolds, Washington Post) probably exceed $35M in 1982 — unrecorded by accounting
- Book value from $19.46 to $526.02 (17 years), 21.1% CAGR — "highly likely to drift downward in future years"
- Industry combined ratio table 1972-1981 showing direct correlation between premium growth rates and underwriting profitability
- Shareholder-designated contributions: 95.6% participation rate, Charlie Munger conceived the program

## Relevance

Contains two of the most important analytical frameworks in the entire letter corpus: the acquisition taxonomy (toads, princes, Category 1 & 2) and the equity value-added analysis showing inflation can make all corporate equity worthless to taxable individuals. The tapeworm metaphor is Buffett's most vivid description of inflation's effect on capital-intensive businesses. Together with the 1979 and 1980 letters, completes a three-year arc on inflation as the central enemy of the investor.
