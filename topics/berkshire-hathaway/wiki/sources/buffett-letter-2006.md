## Summary

Buffett reports a record $16.9B gain in net worth (18.4% book value growth to $70,281/share; 42-year CAGR 21.4%), driven by a benign hurricane season that turned insurance "red ink to very black" ($3.838B underwriting profit on $50.9B float). The letter's intellectual centerpieces are the two-yardstick framework (per-share investments vs. per-share operating earnings), the Equitas/Lloyd's retroactive reinsurance deal ($7.12B float for up to $13.9B coverage), and a devastating analysis of newspaper industry decline. Buffett continues the Gotrocks critique of Wall Street fees (now targeting 2-and-20 hedge fund math) and pays tribute to Walter Schloss as an EMT-demolishing exemplar. The letter marks two strategic milestones: Berkshire's first international acquisition (ISCAR, $4B for 80%) and a formal plan to hire a younger Chief Investment Officer to eventually succeed Buffett.

## Key Claims

- Record $16.9B net worth gain; per-share book value $70,281 (+18.4%); 42-year CAGR 21.4% vs. S&P 10.4%
- Two-yardstick framework: per-share investments $80,636 (27.5% CAGR 1965-2006, declining to 12.6% over last 11 years) vs. per-share pre-tax operating earnings $3,625 (17.9% CAGR, accelerating to 31.7% over last 11 years) — showing the shift from securities to operating businesses
- Insurance: $3.838B total underwriting profit (best year ever); float $50.9B; GEICO $1.314B, B-H Reinsurance $1.658B, General Re $526B, Other Primary $340M
- GEICO: 8.1M policies (42% growth 2003-2006 with 3.5% fewer employees = 47% productivity gain); $631M advertising spend (up from $238M in 2003, $31M in 1995)
- ISCAR acquired July 5 for $4B (80%); first international acquisition; Eitan Wertheimer's 1¼-page letter; cutting-tool business in 61 countries; "makes money because it enables its customers to make more money"
- TTI acquired November 15 (morning meeting, deal before lunch); Paul Andrews Jr., 80% owner; $1.3B revenue electronic components distributor; rejected strategic buyers and PE; Berkshire as "buyer of choice"
- Equitas retroactive reinsurance: $7.12B in cash/securities for up to $13.9B of future claims — effectively insuring Lloyd's asbestos/environmental tail; $5.7B margin above Equitas's own estimates
- Gen Re Securities wound down: 197 contracts from 23,218 (at start of 2002 wind-down); $409M cumulative pre-tax loss; $5M loss in 2006; "All's well that ends" (Shakespeare, slightly modified)
- Newspaper industry: detailed history of monopoly economics, now crumbling; "days of lush profits from our newspaper are over"; online potential is "at best a small fraction" of print; non-economic buyers will emerge as in sports franchises
- Fruit of the Loom: Russell Corp acquired for ~$1.2B (including debt); VF Corp intimate apparel business agreed in December; combined ~$2.2B additional sales, 23,000 employees
- MiTek: stock $10,000/share at purchase → $71,699 after five years; 14 acquisitions; paid off $200M Berkshire debt and holds $35M cash; 55 employee-owners
- NetJets: $143M pre-tax profit (worldwide); European profitability achieved under Mark Booth; 589 net new European customers 2005-2006; cumulative European pre-tax loss $212M before turnaround
- MidAmerican: $1.718B pre-tax earnings; PacifiCorp consolidated (1.7M customers, 6 western states); 86.6% economic interest; HomeServices volume down 9% to $58B; lending-practices critique foreshadows 2008
- Clayton Homes: $513M pre-tax + $86M Berkshire credit fee; $10B installment receivable portfolio; industry lowest unit sales since 1962; 14,787 employees (from 6,661 at purchase)
- Manufacturing/service/retail sector: $52.66B revenue; $3.526B pre-tax; 25% return on average tangible net worth
- Portfolio: $61.5B market value on $23.0B cost basis; AmEx $9.2B, Coke $9.7B, P&G $6.4B, Wells Fargo $7.8B; PetroChina $3.3B (cost $488M); Moody's $3.3B (cost $499M)
- Foreign currency: $2.2B total gain since 2002 position inception; $186M realized in 2006; direct positions nearly eliminated; shift to indirect exposure via foreign equities
- Trade deficit: $0.76T "pseudo-trade" imports (6% GDP); US investment income turned negative in 2006 (first time since 1915); "reverse compounding"; political backlash predicted
- Gotrocks sequel: 2-and-20 hedge fund math — manager keeps 3.6% of 10% gross return ($108M on $3B fund), leaving 6.4% for investors vs. 15% index; "the fellow with money ends up with the experience"
- Walter Schloss tribute: 47-year partnership, no college, one file cabinet → four; only "outside" information; EMT refuted; "We try to buy stocks cheap"
- Succession: three CEO candidates confirmed; investment successor needed (Lou Simpson only six years younger); plan to hire younger CIO with risk-avoidance temperament
- Board: Kim Chace retires (Chace family connection since 1929 merger); Susan Decker (Yahoo! CFO, age 44) joins; director independence critique ("Ratchet, Ratchet and Bingo" fees $150-250K)
- Compensation: Berkshire's one-man comp committee; zero CEO departures in 42 years; incentives tied to operating results of each CEO's business, not Berkshire stock price; $61B equity swings irrelevant to subsidiary CEO pay
- Philanthropy: bulk of holdings going to five foundations; perpetual foundations argument; will stipulates all remaining shares to philanthropy within 13 years of death; foundation selling won't depress stock (adds ~3% to 15% annual turnover)
- Bob Shaw retires from Shaw Industries at 75; Vance Bell succeeds (31-year veteran)
- "Be fearful when others are greedy, and be greedy when others are fearful" — applied to super-cat insurance pricing: rates fell, Berkshire reduced wind exposures

## Relevance

The 2006 letter bridges two eras. The two-yardstick framework quantifies Berkshire's structural shift from a portfolio-driven business (investments per share: 27.5% CAGR) to an operating-business conglomerate (pre-tax earnings per share: 31.7% recent CAGR). ISCAR marks the globalization of Berkshire's acquisition reach. The Equitas deal demonstrates the retroactive reinsurance strategy at scale — $7.12B float with DCRA amortization mechanics explained in detail. The newspaper analysis is one of Buffett's most prescient industry obituaries, explicitly identifying internet disruption years before the industry acknowledged it. The succession discussion advances significantly: CIO hiring plan formalized, acknowledging Lou Simpson's age limits.
