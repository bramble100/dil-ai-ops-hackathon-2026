## Summary

The 1989 letter is one of the most philosophically rich in the entire corpus. It features the legendary "Mistakes of the First Twenty-five Years" retrospective (cigar butt critique, institutional imperative, cockroach theory), a devastating takedown of zero-coupon/PIK bond abuses, the Rip Van Winkle tax advantage of long-term investing, three new convertible preferred investments (Gillette $600M, USAir $358M, Champion $300M), the full Coca-Cola investment story, the Borsheim's acquisition in detail, Mrs. B's departure from NFM, and the "underwriting cycle" debunking. Book value soars 44.4% to $4,296.01.

## Key Claims

- Net worth gain $1.515B (44.4%); book value $4,296.01/share (23.8% CAGR over 25 years)
- Intrinsic value has compounded "somewhat faster" than book value; but current stock prices of investees are "not inappropriately low" — the "double-dip" catch-up is over
- Growth anchor: Carl Sagan bacteria analogy — "from Berkshire's present base of $4.9 billion in net worth, we will find it much more difficult to average 15%"
- Rip Van Winkle tax advantage: $1 doubling annually for 20 years and sold each time yields $25,250; a single 20-year 2^20 investment yields $692,000 — 27:1 difference from tax-timing alone
- "Favorite holding period is forever" restated: "We would rather stay put, even if that means slightly lower returns... akin to marrying for money — a mistake under most circumstances, insanity if one is already rich"
- Look-through earnings: Berkshire's share of investees' retained earnings = $212M, unreported; if distributed, operating earnings would have been ~$500M vs. $300M reported
- Coca-Cola: holdings increased from 14.2M to 23.35M shares; $1,024M cost, $1,804M market value; Roberto Goizueta's "truly rare blend of marketing and financial skills"; Buffett's 52-year delay in buying: "I duly observed the extraordinary consumer attractiveness... Of a certainty, it was in 1936 that I started buying Cokes at the rate of six for 25¢ to sell for 5¢ each"
- Convertible preferreds: Gillette ($600M, 8¾%, convert at $50), USAir ($358M, 9¼%, convert at $60), Champion International ($300M, 9¼%, convert at $38) — all with unrestricted voting; "mediocre return if industry economics hinder, reasonably attractive if they earn average returns"
- Zero-coupon/PIK bond critique: "It is impossible to default on a promise to pay nothing"; EBDIT as "sawed-off yardstick"; investment bankers as irresponsible bartenders; "the bezzle" (Galbraith) dwarfed by zero-coupon nonsense; S&L buyers recording phantom income
- **Mistakes of the First 25 Years:**
  - Cigar butt investing: "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price"; Hochschild Kohn as the teaching case
  - "Never is there just one cockroach in the kitchen"
  - "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre"
  - Good jockeys on good horses: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact"
  - "Easy does it": identify one-foot hurdles to step over, not seven-footers
  - **Institutional imperative** defined: (1) inertia resists directional change; (2) corporate projects soak up available funds; (3) any leader's craving is supported by detailed studies; (4) peer behavior is mindlessly imitated
  - Only work with people you like, trust, and admire: "We've never succeeded in making a good deal with a bad person"
  - Errors of omission: "thumb-sucking" on purchases he understood and that were served on a platter
  - Conservative leverage validated: 99:1 odds favoring higher leverage rejected — "A small chance of distress or disgrace cannot be offset by a large chance of extra returns"
- Borsheim's: detailed acquisition story; Friedman family (Ike, Alan, Marvin Cohn, Don Yale); "If you don't know jewelry, know the jeweler"; no audit, no inventory — one-page contract, large check
- Sainted Seven Plus One: 57% return on average equity with no net leverage; Borsheim's joins
- Mrs. B quits NFM at 96 after carpet department dispute; starts competing business; carpet department market share rises to 75.3% under Blumkin sons
- Insurance: combined ratio 110.4 (est.); "underwriting cycle" debunked — no rhythmic quality, just commodity economics; capacity = "mental state of insurance managers"; CAT cover opportunity after Hugo/earthquake — Berkshire offers $250M capacity; "We will accept more reinsurance risk for our own account than any other company"
- $902.6M zero-coupon convertible debentures issued (44.31% of par, 5.5% effective); convertible to Berkshire at $9,815/share (15% premium)
- Berkshire now rated AAA by S&P — only 15 other US industrial/property-casualty companies

## Relevance

The 1989 letter is transformational for the wiki. The "Mistakes" section crystallizes principles that Buffett had been developing piecemeal across 12 prior letters — particularly the **cigar butt critique** (the definitive departure from Graham-era net-net investing), the **institutional imperative** (a wholly new principle), and **errors of omission** (a category of mistake not previously discussed). The zero-coupon critique extends the [owner earnings](../principles/owner-earnings.md) principle into debt markets, demolishing EBDIT as a yardstick. The Coca-Cola investment story provides the supreme example of [business quality](../principles/business-quality.md) recognized belatedly. The insurance section formally debunks the "underwriting cycle" and introduces the CAT reinsurance strategy that becomes a Berkshire hallmark.
