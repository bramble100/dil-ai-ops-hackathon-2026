## Summary

The 2010 letter reports a 13% book value gain and presents the most structured articulation of Berkshire's intrinsic value: three pillars (investments, non-insurance earnings, and retained-earnings deployment efficacy). BNSF's first year "working out even better than expected" — adding ~40% to normal pre-tax earning power. Buffett provides a 60-year GEICO retrospective as a case study in intrinsic value far exceeding book value ($14B economic goodwill vs. $1.4B carried). Eight consecutive underwriting profit years with $66B float. Todd Combs is hired as the first investment manager successor. The "Life and Debt" essay — anchored by grandfather Ernest Buffett's 1939 cash-reserve letter — articulates Berkshire's extreme aversion to leverage. The letter includes a forceful defense of operating earnings over net income and an extended Black-Scholes critique.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share: $95,453 (+13.0%); 46-year CAGR 20.2%; overall gain 490,409%
- BNSF first full year: $16.85B revenue, $3.988B pre-tax earnings; "working out even better than I expected"; increases "normal" earning power ~40% pre-tax, 30%+ after-tax
- Normal earning power estimated: ~$17B pre-tax, ~$12B after-tax (excluding gains/losses)
- Three pillars of intrinsic value: (1) $158B investments at market; (2) non-insurance pre-tax earnings $5,926/share (21.0% CAGR over 40 years); (3) retained-earnings deployment efficacy
- Per-share investments: $66 (1970) → $94,730 (2010); 19.9% CAGR over 40 years
- Three competitive advantages: (a) talented owner-oriented managers ("hire well, manage little"); (b) unlimited capital allocation universe; (c) hard-to-duplicate culture
- GEICO: 8.8% market share (#3); economic goodwill ~$14B vs. $1.4B book carry; premium $14.3B; $1.117B underwriting profit; Tony Nicely: 2.0% → 8.8% market share; 769,898 new homeowner policies sold via agency
- Float: $65.8B (eight consecutive profitable years; $17B cumulative underwriting profit); $2.013B profit in 2010
- Ajit Jain: $30B float from standing start in 1985; ~$2B annual life reinsurance premium
- Tad Montross (General Re): four insurance commandments articulated
- NetJets turnaround: $207M pre-tax profit (swing of $918M from 2009); Dave Sokol; Berkshire charges $38M guarantee fee
- Marmon: moving to 80% ($1.5B for 17%); 100% expected 2013-2014; 130 businesses; Frank Ptak
- ISCAR: profits up 159%; Tungaloy integration proceeding
- TTI: record sales (+21% above prior peak) and earnings (+58%); Paul Andrews
- Construction downturn: Shaw + Johns Manville + Acme + MiTek combined $362M (vs. $1.3B in 2006)
- BNSF: moves 28% of railroad freight, 11% of US inter-city ton-miles; 23,000 miles of track; "social compact"
- MidAmerican: 2,909 MW wind by end 2011; $5.4B invested in wind; Iowa rates not increased since 1999; Northern Natural pipeline ranked #2 (from #43 at purchase)
- Clayton: 23,343 homes (47% industry); 200,804 mortgages; avg FICO 648; net loss rates 1.17-1.86%
- Todd Combs hired as first investment manager successor; $1-3B initial mandate; Lou Simpson retires at 74
- Goldman Sachs and GE preferred likely to be called; $1.4B aggregate premium; earning power reduced
- Coca-Cola dividends: $88M (1995) → $376M (2011 expected); could double in a decade; "Time is the friend of the wonderful business"
- Wells Fargo: 358.9M shares / $11.1B market value; dividend freeze by Fed; "enormous financial strength"
- Derivatives: high-yield contracts nearly expired with underwriting profit; equity puts $6.7B liability vs. $3.8B settlement value; Black-Scholes critique continued
- "Life and Debt" essay: leverage lethal; "credit is like oxygen"; Ernest Buffett's 1939 $1,000 cash letter; $10B minimum cash pledge; $20B customary hold
- Operating earnings vs. net income: "Ignore our net income figure"
- Berkshire HQ: $270,212 rent; $301,363 furnishings; 20 employees on one floor
- "America's best days lie ahead" — restated with historical references (Dec 6, 1941; Oct 18, 1987; Sept 10, 2001)
- Capital spending: $6B in 2010 (90% US); $8B planned for 2011 (100% of increase in US)

## Relevance

The 2010 letter provides the clearest articulation yet of how to value Berkshire — the three-pillar framework becomes the definitive model. The GEICO goodwill case study is a teaching masterpiece on intrinsic value vs. book value. Todd Combs's hiring signals the first concrete succession step in the investment function. The "Life and Debt" essay, anchored by the grandfather's letter, is the definitive statement of Berkshire's leverage philosophy. BNSF's strong first year validates the capital-intensive pivot.
