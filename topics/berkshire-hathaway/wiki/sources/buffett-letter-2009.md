## Summary

The 2009 letter reports a $21.8B net worth gain (+19.8%), rebounding from 2008's crisis. The dominant event is the announced acquisition of Burlington Northern Santa Fe (BNSF) — Berkshire's largest-ever deal — which adds 65,000 shareholders and requires issuing stock for ~30% of the cost. Buffett uses the letter as both a "freshman orientation" for new shareholders and a refresher for veterans, restating the "What We Don't Do" principles. Float reaches $62B across seven consecutive profitable underwriting years. The GEICO credit card fiasco is confessed, Clayton's manufactured-home financing discrimination is analyzed at length, and the derivatives position is updated. The letter closes with a forceful call for CEO accountability in risk management and a critique of stock-for-stock acquisition math.

## Key Claims

- Book value per share: $84,487 (+19.8%); 45-year CAGR 20.3%; overall gain 434,057%
- BNSF acquisition announced: 100% purchase using ~$22B cash + ~6.1% new Berkshire shares; adds 65,000 shareholders
- Only ~30% of total BNSF cost paid in stock (existing BNSF shares were bought for cash at market); "the final decision was a close one"
- Float: $62B ($21.0B General Re, $26.2B BH Reinsurance, $9.6B GEICO, $5.1B Other); seven consecutive underwriting profit years ($1.559B in 2009)
- GEICO: 8.1% market share (#3 US auto insurer); $800M annual advertising; 7M net policyholders added since 1996; Tony Nicely (age 66); GEICO credit card fiasco: ~$6.3M pre-tax losses + $44M from selling $98M receivables at 55¢ on the dollar
- Ajit Jain: $7.1B single Lloyds premium contract; negotiated $50B potential life reinsurance contract; 30-person staff; "If Charlie, I and Ajit are ever in a sinking boat — and you can only save one of us — swim to Ajit"
- General Re: outstanding year under Tad Montross; 100% of Cologne Re acquired
- Derivatives: $6.3B derivatives float (separate from $62B insurance float); equity puts and high-yield indices; expect aggregate underwriting profit; CEO must never delegate risk control
- MidAmerican: $1.846B operating earnings before interest/taxes; $2.5B earned in three years, $3B spent on wind; Dave Sokol and Greg Abel
- Capital-intensive businesses now welcome: "Anticipating, however, that Berkshire will generate ever-increasing amounts of cash, we are today quite willing to enter businesses that regularly require large capital expenditures"
- BNSF described as having economic characteristics resembling utilities — "social compact" with regulators
- NetJets: $157M cumulative pre-tax loss over 11 years; $1.9B debt; Dave Sokol took over August 2009; "luckily, I have been bailed out"
- Clayton: housing starts 554,000 (lowest in 50 years); manufactured-home financing discrimination (9% vs. 5¼% for site-built); government policy critique
- Berkadia formed (50/50 with Leucadia): $235B commercial mortgage servicing portfolio
- Stock issuance analysis: mathematical proof that undervalued acquirer paying full value destroys shareholder wealth; "Don't ask the barber whether you need a haircut"
- CEO risk control: "It's my job to keep Berkshire far away from such problems... If Berkshire ever gets in trouble, it will be my fault"
- $20B+ cash reserve policy; "We sleep well"

## Relevance

The 2009 letter marks the pivot from crisis to recovery and the beginning of the "regulated, capital-intensive" era at Berkshire. The BNSF acquisition is the largest in company history and signals Buffett's willingness to embrace capital-heavy businesses — a major evolution from the asset-light preference of earlier decades. The stock issuance analysis and "social compact" framework are new conceptual contributions. The GEICO credit card confession adds a rare personal mistake to the collection.
