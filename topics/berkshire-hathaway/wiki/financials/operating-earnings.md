## Definition

Operating earnings = income from Berkshire's wholly-owned businesses + insurance underwriting income + investment income − corporate expenses. It excludes realized and unrealized capital gains/losses on the equity portfolio.

Buffett considers operating earnings the only meaningful performance measure because GAAP net income includes mark-to-market equity swings that can produce $10B+ quarterly fluctuations unrelated to business performance.

> "Operating earnings are what count most." — 2022 Letter

## The "Sainted Seven" Benchmark

**[1987 Letter]** Seven non-financial operating units earned $180M pre-tax on $175M equity = approximately **57% after-tax ROE** with essentially no leverage. For context: among Fortune's top 1,000 companies, only 6 averaged >30% ROE over the prior decade; the best (Commerce Clearing House) managed 40.2%.

**[1988 Letter]** The same group raised to approximately **67% on average equity capital**.

**[1989 Letter]** Now "Sainted Seven Plus One" (Borsheim's added): **57% return** on average equity with no net leverage (cash equivalents equaled funded debt).

This benchmark illustrates the [business quality](../principles/business-quality.md) Buffett demands: capital-light businesses earning returns that would be impossible with leverage, yet are achieved without it. The Sainted Seven set the standard against which all subsequent acquisitions are measured.

## Look-Through Earnings

### The Framework

**[1990 Letter]** Buffett's formal introduction:

> "Berkshire's fundamental earning power is best measured by a 'look-through' approach, in which we append our share of operating earnings retained by investees to our own reported operating earnings."

**Formula:**

```
Look-through = Reported operating earnings
             + Undistributed investee earnings (proportional share)
             − Hypothetical 14% tax on retention
```

### Why It Matters

GAAP only reports dividends received from equity holdings. But when Berkshire owns 9% of [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md), it economically owns 9% of Coke's earnings — whether distributed or not. The undistributed portion compounds inside the investee, building Berkshire's per-share value invisibly.

| Year | Reported | + Investee | − Tax | = Look-Through |
| ---- | -------- | ---------- | ----- | -------------- |
| 1990 | $371M    | $250M      | $30M  | $590M          |
| 1993 | $478M    | $439M      | $61M  | $856M          |
| 1996 | —        | —          | —     | $1,522M        |
| 1999 | $1,085M  | $921M      | $80M  | $1,926M        |

**Big Four gap (2013):** Berkshire received $1.4B in dividends from its Big Four holdings but owned $4.4B in proportional earnings — a $3B annual gap compounding invisibly inside the investees.

## Connection to Berkshire's Structure

- **[Float](float.md):** Insurance underwriting profits are a component of operating earnings. When underwriting is profitable, Berkshire earns operating income AND holds investable float — a double benefit.
- **[Book Value Per Share](book-value-per-share.md):** Operating earnings drive book value growth, but mark-to-market equity gains/losses create noise in the book value metric.
- **[Intrinsic Value vs. Book Value](intrinsic-value-vs-book-value.md):** The two-column framework (per-share investments + per-share operating earnings) is the best proxy for intrinsic value. Operating earnings growth in Column 2 has accelerated as Berkshire shifted from securities portfolio to operating conglomerate.
