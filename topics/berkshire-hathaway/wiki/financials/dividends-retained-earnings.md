## Definition

Berkshire has paid exactly one dividend in 60 years: **10¢ per A share** on January 3, 1967 — totaling $101,755. Buffett has joked he "must have been in the bathroom" when the decision was made. Every dollar earned since then has been retained, compounding inside the company at a rate that no dividend could match. This policy is not a quirk — it's the mathematical core of Berkshire's wealth creation.

The governing principle is the **"$1 test"**: every dollar retained must create at least $1 of market value. If management cannot deploy capital at returns exceeding what shareholders could earn independently, the money should be distributed. For 60 years, Berkshire has passed this test.

## The $1 Test Framework

**[1983 Letter]** The Owner's Manual introduced the test: retained earnings are justified only if each dollar retained creates at least one dollar of market value over time. This is the shareholder's protection against empire-building — if management hoards cash for sub-par returns, the stock should trade below book value, signaling the market's verdict that the capital is trapped.

**[1984 Letter]** Buffett refined the concept by distinguishing **restricted earnings** (consumed by inflation and maintenance capex) from **unrestricted earnings** (genuinely available for value-creating deployment). Many companies report growing earnings while retaining everything — but if inflation eats the reinvestment, shareholders receive nothing. Only unrestricted earnings count toward the $1 test[^1].

## The Mathematical Proof: Dividends vs. Sell-Off

**[2012 Letter]** Buffett devoted a full essay to proving mathematically that for a company earning high returns on equity, a **sell-off policy** (shareholders selling small portions of their holdings) outperforms a traditional dividend:

The worked example: a company earning 12% on equity, trading at 125% of book value. An investor who needs 4% annual income is better off selling 3.2% of shares each year than receiving a 4% dividend — because the retained earnings compound at 12% inside the company, while dividends are taxed immediately and reinvested (if at all) at market rates.

Buffett's own case: he gave away 4¼% of his holdings annually for seven years. Despite the constant distribution, book value of his remaining shares grew from $28.2B to $40.2B — because the retained earnings compounded faster than the distributions reduced them[^2].

The critique of dividends is not that cash distributions are inherently wrong — it's that **tax-inefficient** distributions from a high-ROE company destroy compounding. A dollar paid as dividend is taxed immediately; a dollar retained compounds tax-free until the shareholder chooses to sell.

## Retained Earnings as Invisible Engine

### The 2019 Essay: Edgar Lawrence Smith

**[2019 Letter]** Buffett traced the intellectual history to **Edgar Lawrence Smith's 1924 book**, which demonstrated that common stocks outperform bonds precisely because companies retain a portion of earnings. **John Maynard Keynes**, reviewing the book, identified the "element of compound interest" as the key insight. Before Smith, stocks were "speculations" and "Gentlemen preferred bonds."

The data from Berkshire's own holdings proved the point: in 2019, the 10 largest equity positions generated **$3.8B in dividends** to Berkshire but retained **$8.3B** — more than twice as much. The retained portion compounds invisibly inside the investees, building Berkshire's proportional wealth without appearing on any income statement[^3].

### The Secret Sauce (2022)

**[2022 Letter]** Two positions demonstrated retained-earnings power over decades:

| Holding                                             | Cost  | Dividends (start→2022) | Market Value | Return |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ----- | ---------------------- | ------------ | ------ |
| [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md)               | $1.3B | $75M → $704M           | $25B         | 19x    |
| [American Express](../entities/american-express.md) | $1.3B | $41M → $302M           | $22B         | 17x    |

The dividend growth alone (9x for Coke, 7x for AmEx) justified the original investment. But the real wealth came from _retained_ earnings compounding inside the businesses — growing brand value, market share, and competitive position without any action from Berkshire.

> "The weeds wither away in significance as the flowers bloom." — 2022 Letter[^4]

## BHE: The Extreme Case

[Berkshire Hathaway Energy](../case-studies/berkshire-hathaway-energy-acquisition.md) represents the 100% retention policy taken to its logical extreme. In 25 years of Berkshire ownership, BHE has **never paid a common stock dividend**, accumulating **$28B+ in retained earnings**. These retained dollars funded:

- PacifiCorp acquisition (1.6M customers)
- NV Energy acquisition ($5.6B)
- $16B+ in renewable energy investments
- Iowa rate freeze for 30 years (competitive moat)

The result: earnings grew from $122M (2000) to $4.3B (2022) — a **35-fold increase** — without Berkshire contributing additional equity capital. The compounding was entirely self-funded through retention[^5].

## Why Berkshire Doesn't Pay Dividends

Five reinforcing reasons:

1. **The $1 test is passed.** Every dollar retained has historically created more than $1 of market value — 19.7% CAGR over 60 years.
2. **Tax efficiency.** Dividends are taxed immediately at ordinary income rates. Retained earnings compound tax-free until the shareholder sells — the [Rip Van Winkle advantage](tax-strategy-deferred-gains.md).
3. **Acquisition optionality.** Cash retained gives Berkshire the ability to deploy "washtubs, not teaspoons" when opportunities appear. See [Cash & Treasury Management](cash-and-treasury-management.md).
4. **[Buybacks](share-repurchase-economics.md) are tax-superior to dividends.** When Berkshire repurchases shares below intrinsic value, remaining shareholders get a tax-free increase in per-share value. Dividends achieve the same cash distribution at a higher tax cost.
5. **Structural advantage for subsidiaries.** [Clayton Homes](../case-studies/clayton-homes-acquisition.md) and BHE use retained capital as competitive weapons — funding portfolios and infrastructure that competitors can't match.
