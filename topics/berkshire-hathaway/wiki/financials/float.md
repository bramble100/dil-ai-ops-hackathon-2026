## Definition

Insurance float = loss reserves + loss adjustment reserves + unearned premium reserves − agents' balances − prepaid acquisition costs − deferred charges.

In plain terms: policyholders pay premiums before claims are paid out. The money held between collection and payout is "float." If underwriting is profitable (premiums exceed claims + expenses), the float is held at **negative cost** — insurers are effectively paid to hold other people's money.

Float functions as long-duration, interest-free (or better-than-free) capital that can be invested for Berkshire's benefit. It is the structural engine behind Berkshire's compounding — arguably more important than any single investment decision.

## Why Float Matters More Than Earnings

Float is Berkshire's primary competitive advantage — not its stock-picking or acquisition skills. Consider:

1. **Scale.** $176B in float dwarfs Berkshire's ~$40B in retained earnings over the same period. Float has been the dominant capital source.
2. **Cost.** Most years, float has been free or better-than-free. Berkshire was paid to hold $176B in investable capital.
3. **Duration.** Float is "very sticky and immune from precipitous decline" — policyholders renew, claims take years to settle, and Berkshire's size ensures steady inflows.
4. **Compounding.** Each dollar of float, invested at even modest returns, compounds indefinitely. At negative cost, the compounding is pure profit.

> "Float has cost us less than nothing over our 55 years of operation." — 2013 Letter

## The Structural Advantage

### Why Only Berkshire Can Do This

Two moats protect Berkshire's float economics:

**1. Unmatched financial strength.** When claims take decades to settle (structured settlements, loss reserve assumptions), only Berkshire can guarantee payment. This makes Berkshire the natural counterparty for mega-catastrophe and long-tail risks. No competitor can offer $400B+ in catastrophe capacity[^1].

> "When 'the day after' arrives, Berkshire's checks will clear." — 2004 Letter

**2. Total indifference to volume.** Berkshire doesn't need premiums and can walk away when pricing deteriorates. Employees have a no-layoff promise regardless of premium volume — removing the institutional imperative to write underpriced business. See [Underwriting Discipline](../principles/underwriting-discipline.md).

### The Ajit Jain Effect

**[2002 Letter]** Ajit built $13.4B of reinsurance float "from a standing start in 1986 with no actuaries" and a 20-person staff. His operation handles risks no other insurer will touch — single events that could produce $250M+ losses. This float pool was created essentially from nothing, on talent and reputation alone[^2].

## Connection to Other Financial Metrics

- **[Book Value Per Share](book-value-per-share.md):** Float is carried as a liability on GAAP balance sheets, suppressing book value. Yet costless float is functionally equivalent to equity capital. This is a major source of the gap between book value and intrinsic value. See [Intrinsic Value vs. Book Value](intrinsic-value-vs-book-value.md).
- **[Operating Earnings](operating-earnings.md):** Underwriting profits are the "cost" side of float. When underwriting is profitable, float contributes directly to operating earnings AND provides investable capital — a double benefit.
- **[Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md):** Float is the capital pool from which acquisitions and equity investments are funded. Berkshire's acquisition capacity is a function of float, not earnings alone.
