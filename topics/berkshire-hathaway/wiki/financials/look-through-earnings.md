## Definition

Look-through earnings measure Berkshire's true earning power by adding its proportional share of investees' _retained_ earnings to reported operating earnings. GAAP only reports dividends received from equity holdings — but when Berkshire owns 9% of [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md), it economically owns 9% of Coke's total earnings, whether distributed as dividends or not. The undistributed portion compounds inside the investee, building Berkshire's per-share wealth invisibly.

**[1990 Letter]** The formal introduction:

> "Berkshire's fundamental earning power is best measured by a 'look-through' approach, in which we append our share of operating earnings retained by investees to our own reported operating earnings."[^1]

## The Formula

```
Look-Through Earnings = Reported operating earnings
                      + Undistributed investee earnings (proportional share)
                      − Hypothetical 14% tax on retained investee earnings
```

The 14% tax haircut reflects the tax Berkshire would pay if those retained earnings were distributed as dividends. It's conservative — most retained earnings compound inside investees tax-free indefinitely. But Buffett applied it to keep the framework honest.

**[1992 Letter]** Buffett set the target explicitly: look-through earnings must grow from $604M (1992) to **$1.8B by year 2000** to sustain a 15% annual growth rate in intrinsic value. The target was raised to $1.85B in 1993 after share issuance. By 2000, actual look-through reached $2,415M — 34% above the original target[^2].

## Why Look-Through Stopped

After 2000, Buffett gradually stopped publishing explicit look-through figures. Two factors drove the change:

1. **Berkshire's shift from securities portfolio to operating conglomerate.** As wholly-owned subsidiaries (BNSF, BHE, Lubrizol, PCC) grew to dominate earnings, the look-through gap between reported and economic earnings narrowed. Operating earnings from subsidiaries are fully consolidated — no "invisible" portion.
2. **Portfolio complexity.** With hundreds of smaller positions alongside the Big Four, calculating precise look-through became impractical and potentially misleading.

But Buffett never abandoned the _concept_. He continued to emphasize the gap between reported dividends and economic earnings from major holdings — particularly in the Big Four and Four Giants analyses.

## The Big Four / Four Giants Era (2011-2025)

Though formal look-through calculations stopped, Buffett provided the components:

### Big Four Analysis (2011-2014)

| Year | Dividends Received | Proportional Earnings Share | Invisible Earnings | Holdings                     |
| ---- | ------------------ | --------------------------- | ------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| 2011 | $862M              | $3.3B                       | **$2.4B**          | AmEx, Coke, IBM, Wells Fargo |
| 2013 | $1.4B              | $4.4B                       | **$3.0B**          | Same four                    |
| 2014 | —                  | $4.7B                       | —                  | Same four                    |

**[2014 Letter]** Each 0.1% increase in ownership = approximately **$50M** in additional annual earnings share. IBM's buybacks alone had increased Berkshire's ownership from 5.5% to 7.8% — adding ~$115M in annual invisible earnings at zero cost[^3].

### Retained Earnings Power (2019)

**[2019 Letter]** The retained earnings essay — tracing the idea from Edgar Lawrence Smith's 1924 book through Keynes — provided an updated snapshot:

**10 largest equity holdings:** Dividends received = **$3.8B**; Investee retained earnings (Berkshire's share) = **$8.3B**

The invisible portion was **2.2x** the visible dividend income. This $8.3B compounds annually inside the investees — growing their competitive positions, funding their own buybacks, and increasing Berkshire's proportional ownership — without appearing on any Berkshire income statement[^4].

### Four-Stock Core (2024-2025)

**[2025 Letter]** The top four US equity holdings — [Apple](../entities/apple.md) ($62B), [American Express](../entities/american-express.md) ($56B), [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md) ($28B), Moody's ($12.6B) — totaled **$158.6B on $9.1B cost**. Combined dividends: ~$2.5B annually.

The look-through logic explains why the $9.1B became $158.6B: decades of retained earnings compounding inside each investee, driving earnings growth, brand strengthening, competitive moat deepening, and share buybacks that increased Berkshire's ownership percentage without requiring additional capital[^5].

## The Practical Insight: Why It Matters

Look-through earnings solve a fundamental problem in financial analysis: **GAAP systematically understates the earning power of patient minority-stake investors.** A company receiving $862M in dividends from positions worth $47B appears to earn 1.8% on those holdings. The actual economic return — including the investees' retained earnings — is many multiples higher.

This insight underpins several Berkshire strategies:

- **[American Express](../case-studies/american-express-investment.md):** 27 years of "inaction" while AmEx buybacks drove Berkshire's ownership from 5.5% to 22.1% — all invisible to GAAP
- **[Apple](../case-studies/apple-investment.md):** Ownership rose from 5.2% to 5.55% through Apple's buybacks — $5.6B economic earnings vs. $785M reported dividends
- **[Coca-Cola](../case-studies/coca-cola-investment.md):** Dividends grew from $75M to $704M over 28 years; retained earnings grew even faster, funding global expansion invisible on Berkshire's books
