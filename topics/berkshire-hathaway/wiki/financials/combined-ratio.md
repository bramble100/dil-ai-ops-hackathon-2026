## Definition

The **combined ratio** measures an insurer's underwriting profitability:

$$\text{Combined Ratio} = \frac{\text{Losses + Loss Adjustment Expenses + Underwriting Expenses}}{\text{Premiums Earned}} \times 100$$

- **CR < 100%** → underwriting profit (the insurer earns money on the insurance business itself, before investment income)
- **CR = 100%** → breakeven underwriting (investment income is pure profit)
- **CR > 100%** → underwriting loss (the insurer pays more in claims and expenses than it collects in premiums)

The combined ratio determines the **cost of [float](float.md)**. When CR < 100%, float is free — or better, the insurer is _paid_ to hold other people's money. When CR > 100%, the excess over breakeven is the annual cost of maintaining the float[^1].

## Berkshire's Insurance Operations

### National Indemnity: The Ebb-and-Flow Model

[National Indemnity](../entities/national-indemnity.md) (NICO) pioneered Berkshire's insurance philosophy under Ajit Jain: write business only when pricing makes sense; shrink dramatically when it doesn't.

**[1986 Letter]** Premium volume swung from $366M (1985) to $723M when pricing was right. In soft markets, NICO would shrink to a fraction of prior volume rather than write unprofitable business. This produced volatile top-line revenue but consistent underwriting profitability over full cycles[^2].

The model required two structural advantages: (1) Berkshire's capital base eliminated any pressure to write volume for cash flow, and (2) management compensation was never tied to premium volume.

### GEICO: Scale Economics Shared

[GEICO](../entities/geico.md) operated on a fundamentally different model — high-volume, low-cost direct distribution — but achieved the same goal: CR consistently below industry averages.

**[2006 Letter]** GEICO's cost advantage over agent-based competitors was structural: no commission payments, no broker networks, no layered distribution. Market share grew from 2.5% (1996 acquisition) to 6.1% by 2006, to 14%+ by the 2020s. Every point of share gain came with improving unit economics — scale reduced per-policy costs while the direct model kept acquisition costs flat[^3].

GEICO's combined ratio typically ran **5-10 points below** the industry average. In a business where the industry averages ~100%, a persistent 5-10 point advantage compounds enormously over decades.

### General Re: Crisis to Discipline

[General Re](../entities/general-re.md)'s combined ratio told the turnaround story:

| Year  | CR (approx.) | Context                                  |
| ----- | ------------ | ---------------------------------------- |
| 1999  | ~120%        | Reserve inadequacy; soft-market collapse |
| 2001  | ~170%+       | 9/11 + $800M prior-year development      |
| 2003  | ~95%         | First profitable year; four commandments |
| 2006  | ~86%         | Best year under Berkshire ownership      |
| 2013+ | <100%        | Consistent profitability; "now a gem"    |

The 2001-2003 improvement — from ~170% to ~95% — is one of the most dramatic turnarounds in reinsurance history. See [General Re — From Crisis to Gem](../case-studies/general-re-turnaround.md) for the full case study[^4].

## The Four Commandments

**[2004 Letter]** Buffett codified the [underwriting discipline](../principles/underwriting-discipline.md) rules that govern combined ratio management:

1. **Understand all exposures** that might cause losses
2. **Conservatively assess** the probability of loss and its cost
3. **Set premiums** that deliver profit after all costs
4. **Walk away** if appropriate premium cannot be obtained

> "Many insurers pass the first three tests and flunk the fourth. They simply can't turn their back on business that their competitors are eagerly writing."

Rule #4 is what produces the ebb-and-flow pattern: the willingness to let premium volume drop 50%+ rather than write at inadequate pricing[^5].

## Industry Context

**[1980 Letter]** Buffett's first systematic discussion of industry combined ratios established the competitive landscape: the industry average CR typically runs 100-110%, meaning the average insurer loses money on underwriting and depends entirely on investment income.

Berkshire's structural advantage: because float grows even during profitable years, and because Berkshire never faces pressure to write volume, it can maintain CR < 100% through full insurance cycles — earning underwriting profit _plus_ investment income on the float. Most competitors can achieve only one.

**[2017 Letter]** 15 consecutive years of underwriting profit across all Berkshire insurance operations. The probability of this occurring by chance, given industry cyclicality, is essentially zero. It reflects structural competitive advantage, not luck[^6].

## Connection to Float Economics

Combined ratio and [float](float.md) are inseparable metrics:

| Scenario                       | CR    | Float Cost              | Economic Result                                          |
| ------------------------------ | ----- | ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Consistent underwriting profit | <100% | Negative (paid to hold) | Float is better than free money                          |
| Breakeven underwriting         | 100%  | Zero                    | Float generates pure investment income                   |
| Moderate underwriting loss     | 105%  | Moderate                | Float still profitable if investment returns exceed cost |
| Severe underwriting loss       | 120%+ | High                    | Float becomes expensive; may destroy value               |

Berkshire's combined ratio has averaged below 100% since 2003 — meaning the $176B+ float (as of 2024) is generated at _negative_ cost. This is the core of Berkshire's insurance competitive advantage.
