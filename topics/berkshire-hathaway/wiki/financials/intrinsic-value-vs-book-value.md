## The Core Distinction

**[1983 Letter]** The canonical definition:

> "Book value tells you what has been put in; intrinsic business value estimates what can be taken out."

Book value is an accounting artifact — historical cost minus depreciation/amortization. Intrinsic value is the present value of all future cash flows a business will generate. For most businesses, the two diverge over time, and the direction of divergence reveals whether the business creates or destroys value.

## Why the Gap Widens for Berkshire

### Three Sources of Divergence

**1. Operating businesses carried at historical cost.** GAAP requires marking equity securities to market quarterly but carrying wholly-owned subsidiaries at acquisition cost indefinitely. As Berkshire shifted from securities portfolio (1980s: equities = 114% of net worth) to operating conglomerate (2014+: equities < 50% of net worth), a growing share of Berkshire's value became invisible on the balance sheet[^1].

**2. Economic goodwill vs. accounting goodwill.** When Berkshire acquires a business above book value, GAAP creates "goodwill" that may be amortized or tested for impairment. But [economic goodwill](../principles/economic-goodwill.md) — the earning power of consumer franchises, competitive positions, and management quality — typically grows while accounting goodwill shrinks.

**[1983 Letter, Appendix]** [See's Candies](../case-studies/sees-candies-acquisition.md) as proof: purchased for $25M with $8M tangible assets, creating $17M in accounting goodwill. By 1983, accounting goodwill had been amortized down to $12.5M — yet economic goodwill had "increased in irregular but very substantial fashion" as earnings grew from $4.2M to $27.4M.

> "While accounting Goodwill regularly decreased from the moment of purchase, economic Goodwill increased in irregular but very substantial fashion." — 1983 Letter

**3. Float carried as liability.** [Insurance float](float.md) — $176B as of 2023 — appears as a liability on Berkshire's balance sheet, directly reducing book value. Yet costless or negative-cost float is functionally equivalent to equity capital. This single item creates a $100B+ gap between book value and intrinsic value.

### Buffett's Intellectual Shift

**[1983 Letter]** The confession that changed his investment philosophy:

> "My own thinking has changed drastically from 35 years ago when I was taught to favor tangible assets and to shun businesses whose value depended largely upon economic Goodwill. This bias caused me to make many important business mistakes of omission, although relatively few of commission."

**[2014 Letter]** The gap is now so wide that book value has become misleading: "The gap between Berkshire's intrinsic value and its book value has materially widened."

**[2018 Letter]** After 54 years, Buffett **abandons book value per share** as the primary metric, acknowledging it no longer tracks intrinsic value because operating subsidiaries (carried at cost) now dominate Berkshire's worth.

## The Two-Column Framework

**[1996 Letter]** Buffett's recommended proxy for intrinsic value — two independently assessable pillars:

| Column                                 | 1996    | 2005    | 2010    | 2013     | 2015     |
| -------------------------------------- | ------- | ------- | ------- | -------- | -------- |
| Per-share investments                  | $28,500 | $74,129 | $94,730 | $129,253 | $159,794 |
| Per-share operating earnings (pre-tax) | $421    | $2,441  | $5,926  | $9,116   | $12,304  |

**How to use it:** Value Column 1 at market (it already reflects market prices). Apply an appropriate P/E multiple to Column 2 based on business quality. Sum both. The result approximates intrinsic value far better than book value.

**The structural shift:** In 1996, Column 1 dominated — Berkshire was primarily an investment vehicle. By 2015, Column 2 had exploded (29x growth vs. Column 1's 5.6x) — reflecting the transformation into an operating conglomerate. The CAGR tells the story: investments grew at 18.9%, but operating earnings grew at **23.7%** from 1970.

> "It is no coincidence that the price of Berkshire stock over the 43-year period has increased at a rate very similar to our two measures of value." — 2013 Letter

## Practical Implications

### For Investors Analyzing Berkshire

1. **Don't use book value.** It understates intrinsic value by the sum of: (a) hidden gains in operating subsidiaries, (b) costless float carried as liability, (c) growing economic goodwill in franchise businesses.
2. **Use the two-column framework.** It separates the investment portfolio (observable) from operating businesses (require judgment), letting investors apply their own assumptions.
3. **Watch operating earnings growth, not GAAP net income.** See [Operating Earnings](operating-earnings.md) for why GAAP is misleading.

### For Investors Analyzing Any Business

**[1992 Letter]** The universal principle:

> "The value of any stock, bond or business today is determined by the cash inflows and outflows — discounted at an appropriate interest rate — that can be expected to occur during the remaining life of the asset."

Book value is irrelevant to this calculation. What matters is earning power, capital requirements, and durability. A business with $8M in tangible assets earning $80M annually (See's) is worth more than a business with $800M in tangible assets earning $80M annually — because the first generates excess cash while the second consumes it.
