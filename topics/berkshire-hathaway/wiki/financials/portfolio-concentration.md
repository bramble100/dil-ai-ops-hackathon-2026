## Definition

Berkshire's equity portfolio has always been concentrated — a small number of positions held with deep conviction for decades. This page traces the evolution of that concentration from early permanent holdings through the "Big Four" era to the final 9-holding structure, and documents how Buffett's philosophy of extreme concentration produced one of the greatest portfolio track records in history.

The core principle: **"It is better to have a partial interest in the Hope Diamond than to own all of a rhinestone."** — 2014/2015 Letters[^1]

## Philosophy: Why Concentrate?

### Circle of Competence Constrains Position Count

**[1978 Letter]** Buffett's earliest statement of concentrated investing: portfolio focused on businesses where he had genuine understanding. The [circle of competence](../principles/four-filters.md) naturally limits the number of investable opportunities. The "fat wallet problem" — as capital grows, fewer positions are large enough to matter — pushes in the same direction[^2].

**[1994 Letter]** At $11.9B net worth, the minimum meaningful position was $100M. The Ted Williams analogy: wait for a "good ball to hit" rather than swinging at every pitch.

### Permanent Holdings

**[1987 Letter]** Three holdings formally designated as permanent: Capital Cities/ABC, GEICO, and Washington Post — "a permanent part of Berkshire rather than merchandise to be disposed of once Mr. Market offers a high enough price." This was the first explicit statement that some positions would never be sold regardless of valuation[^3].

### Masterly Inactivity

**[1984 Letter]** Buffett's philosophy of minimal trading: "Our stay-put behavior reflects our view that the stock market serves as a relocation center at which money is moved from the active to the patient." The four largest holdings (GEICO, General Foods, Washington Post, Exxon) represented most of the portfolio's value — and all were repurchasing shares.

## The Big Four Era (2004-2017)

### Genesis: 2004

**[2004 Letter]** The "Big Four" label first appeared: [American Express](../entities/american-express.md), [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md), [Gillette](../entities/gillette.md), and [Wells Fargo](../entities/wells-fargo.md). These four positions had a combined cost of **$3.83B** and market value of **$36.3B** — a 9.5x return. Berkshire's share of their combined earnings was **$1.2B** (31.3% annual yield on cost), while cash distributions were $434M (11.3% of cost).

The insight: most of these companies' earnings were retained and reinvested, compounding invisibly within the investees. GAAP only reported dividends received; the true economic interest was 2.8x larger[^4].

[Lou Simpson](../entities/lou-simpson.md) ran a parallel demonstration: GEICO's $2.5B equity portfolio, managed independently, delivered **20.3% CAGR** over 25 years on a concentrated portfolio of 8-15 positions.

The Woody Allen quip: "The advantage of being bi-sexual is that it doubles your chances for a date on Saturday night" — applied to Berkshire's ability to own partial or full businesses[^5].

## Transition: Big Four to Four Giants (2017-2021)

The "Big Four" label described equity portfolio holdings. The **"Four Giants"** label, introduced in the [2021 Letter](../sources/buffett-letter-2021.md), described Berkshire's four largest _business_ pillars — a broader concept including wholly-owned subsidiaries:

1. **Insurance** ([NICO](../entities/national-indemnity.md)/[GEICO](../entities/geico.md)/[General Re](../entities/general-re.md)/[BHSI](../entities/national-indemnity.md)) — float $147B
2. **Apple** — equity holding, the single largest position
3. **[BNSF](../entities/bnsf.md)** — wholly-owned railroad
4. **[BHE](../entities/midamerican-energy.md)** — wholly-owned utility/energy

The shift reflected Berkshire's transformation from a holding company with a concentrated equity portfolio to a diversified conglomerate where wholly-owned businesses (insurance, railroad, energy) mattered as much as equity positions. PP&E reached **$158B — #1 in the U.S.**, surpassing every company in the country[^6].

**[2020 Letter]** The intermediate framing: four "family jewels" (insurance, BNSF, Apple, BHE) plus a growing buyback program. [Wells Fargo](../entities/wells-fargo.md) dropped below the top-15 holdings by 2020, ending its 30-year presence in the core portfolio.

## The Nine-Holding Endgame (2024-2025)

### Four-Stock US Core (2024)

**[2024 Letter]** Four US equity positions formally designated with "no intent to sell":

| Company                                             | Key Metric                                                               |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Apple                                               | Largest holding; Four Giants member                                      |
| [American Express](../entities/american-express.md) | 22.1% ownership via [buyback compounding](share-repurchase-economics.md) |
| [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md)               | 9.3%; $816M dividends on $1.3B cost                                      |
| Moody's                                             | 13.9% ownership                                                          |

> "Household names such as Apple, American Express, Coca-Cola and Moody's — many of these companies earn very high returns on the net tangible equity required for their operations." — 2024 Letter[^7]

The Japanese positions — built through yen-denominated bonds for natural currency hedging — added a second geographic pillar:

### The Complete Portfolio (2025)

**[2025 Letter]** Abel's first CEO letter reveals the full structure:

- **9 holdings** comprising **$194B** in market value on **$24.5B** total cost
- **Annual dividends:** $2.5B (~10% yield on cost)
- These 9 positions represent roughly **2/3 of the $297.8B** total equity portfolio
- **Cash and Treasuries:** $370B+

> "A large portion of our portfolio is concentrated in a small number of American companies... businesses we understand well, have a high regard for their leaders, and expect will compound over decades. This concentrated approach will continue, with limited activity in these holdings." — Abel, 2025 Letter[^8]

## The Economics of Extreme Concentration

### Why It Works: Compound Interest on Conviction

The 9-holding portfolio demonstrates several compounding mechanisms working simultaneously:

1. **Earnings growth** — the underlying businesses grow revenues and profits
2. **Investee buybacks** — each company repurchases shares, increasing Berkshire's ownership without additional investment
3. **Dividend growth** — dividends increase annually, compounding yield on the original cost basis
4. **Berkshire's own buybacks** — reduce Berkshire shares outstanding, increasing per-share exposure to each position

The combined effect: $24.5B in cost producing $194B in market value (7.9x) and $2.5B in annual dividends (10.2% yield on cost). The portfolio essentially manages itself — requiring, in Buffett's words, "limited activity."

### The Concentration Paradox

Diversification reduces risk but also dilutes the impact of correct decisions. Berkshire's approach accepts concentration risk in exchange for:

- **Deeper understanding** — fewer positions means more time to understand each business
- **Meaningful impact** — a single excellent investment (Apple at $62B, AmEx at $56B) materially affects the whole
- **Reduced decision frequency** — "one good decision every five years" (2022 Letter) rather than constant rebalancing

The [2022 Letter](../sources/buffett-letter-2022.md) distills it: "The weeds wither away in significance as the flowers bloom. Over time, it takes just a few winners to work wonders."
