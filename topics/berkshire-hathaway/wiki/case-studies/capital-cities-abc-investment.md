## Overview

In 1985, Berkshire invested **$517.5M** for 3 million shares of Capital Cities Communications at $172.50/share — financing Tom Murphy's $3.5B acquisition of ABC, the largest non-oil merger in US history at the time. Buffett considered Murphy and his partner Dan Burke "the best management of any publicly-owned company in the country." The investment was Berkshire's largest to date and Buffett's highest-conviction bet on management quality as a competitive moat.

Over the next decade, the position appreciated to $1.7B — a 3.3x return. But the case study's real lesson came from Buffett's partial sale in 1993: a "Mistake Du Jour" that cost shareholders $222.5M in forgone gains within a single year, and that Buffett used to teach himself — publicly — about the cost of violating permanent-holding discipline.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[1985 Letter]** Three elements made Capital Cities/ABC irresistible:

1. **Management quality.** Murphy and Burke had built Capital Cities into the strongest media company in America through relentless cost discipline and operational excellence. Buffett had admired Murphy for decades and had previously owned Capital Cities stock (purchased at $17.25 in 1977, sold at $43 in 1978-80 — an early regret).
2. **Media franchise economics.** In the mid-1980s, broadcasting networks and newspapers still possessed formidable pricing power. Advertising rates could be raised annually with minimal customer attrition — the "toll bridge" model that Buffett prized.
3. **The financing structure.** Buffett invested $517.5M and voluntarily granted Murphy voting rights over Berkshire's shares while accepting sale restrictions. Both provisions came from Buffett and Munger — not Murphy — to prevent the block from being used for hostile purposes and to let management "focus their efforts entirely upon running the businesses"[^1].

## Outcome

### Permanent Holding Designation

**[1986 Letter]** Capital Cities/ABC was designated one of three "permanent" holdings alongside [GEICO](../entities/geico.md) and Washington Post. Buffett declared that even if these securities appeared "significantly overpriced, we would not anticipate selling them, just as we would not sell See's or Buffalo Evening News." He explicitly critiqued the "modern manager" who treats all businesses as candidates for "restructuring" whenever Wall Street preferences shift[^2].

**[1987 Letter]** Market value doubled to $1,035M — the first permanent holding to achieve 2x. "With Tom Murphy and Dan Burke at Cap Cities... we simply couldn't be in better hands"[^3].

### Media Erosion Identified

**[1991 Letter]** Buffett spotted the secular threat early: media franchise economics were eroding. Cable, satellites, and proliferating channels were fragmenting audiences. "The forces are so powerful... that even outstanding management cannot fully offset them." Cap Cities/ABC earnings declined alongside Washington Post's — the first structural challenge to Buffett's media franchise thesis[^4].

Murphy and Burke's cost discipline partially offset revenue headwinds, but Buffett acknowledged the erosion was permanent, not cyclical. This early warning about media fragmentation would prove prescient for the entire industry.

### The Partial Sale: "Mistake Du Jour — Silver Medal"

**[1993 Letter]** Berkshire sold 1 million of its 3 million Cap Cities shares — reducing ownership from ~18% to ~13%.

**[1994 Letter]** Buffett's confession: the shares sold at $63 (split-adjusted) in late 1993; by year-end 1994 the price was $85.25. "The difference is $222.5 million for those who wish to avoid the pain of calculating the damage yourself."

This was not Buffett's first Capital Cities timing error. He had bought the stock at $17.25 in 1977 and sold at $43 in 1978-80 — years before the ABC merger. The $172.50 repurchase in 1985 was itself an admission of the earlier mistake.

> "I've become a repeat offender. Maybe it's time to get a guardian appointed." — 1994 Letter[^5]

### Dan Burke Retirement

**[1994 Letter]** Dan Burke retired — praised as "one of two CEOs who have done great things for Berkshire shareholders" (alongside Carl Reichardt at [Wells Fargo](../entities/wells-fargo.md)). Burke and Murphy "encountered very tough industry conditions but emerged with record earnings, added luster, and bright prospects." The transition was well-prepared; Burke left the company in outstanding hands[^6].

## Buffett's Retrospective

The Capital Cities saga contains three distinct confessions:

1. **1978-80: Selling at $43.** The original sin — selling a position in a Murphy-led company at a fraction of its future value.
2. **1985: Repurchasing at $172.50.** Implicitly an admission that the 1978-80 sale was wrong — Buffett paid 4x his original cost to get back in.
3. **1993-94: Selling again at $63.** History repeating. The "repeat offender" label is self-imposed and deeply felt.

The throughline: _when you own shares in a business run by the best operator in America, the correct holding period is forever_. Each sale — in 1978 and in 1993 — was followed by price appreciation that made the sale look foolish. The permanent-holding designation of 1986 was the right instinct; the 1993 sale violated it.

## Lessons

1. **Management quality is a compounding asset.** Murphy and Burke delivered returns through cost discipline that offset a deteriorating industry. The value of exceptional management is most visible when the operating environment turns hostile — mediocre managers would have produced losses. See [Business Quality](../principles/business-quality.md).

2. **Permanent-holding discipline must survive temporary doubts.** Buffett designated Cap Cities as "permanent" in 1986. He sold a third of the position seven years later. The cost: $222.5M in a single year. When conviction is justified, selling is the most expensive mistake available.

3. **Media franchise economics are not permanent.** Capital Cities/ABC was one of the first signals that broadcasting and newspaper franchises — once considered invulnerable — were subject to technological disruption. Buffett's 1991 observation about "forces too powerful for even outstanding management" foreshadowed the complete collapse of traditional media economics over the following two decades.

4. **Repeat mistakes reveal deep behavioral patterns.** Selling Cap Cities twice (1978 and 1993) at prices far below subsequent valuations suggests a systematic tendency to undervalue what you already own. Buffett's self-diagnosis — "repeat offender" — is the kind of honest self-assessment that prevents the third occurrence. The permanent-holding framework for [Coca-Cola](coca-cola-investment.md) and [American Express](american-express-investment.md) was partly forged from this pain.

5. **The voting-rights structure was ahead of its time.** Voluntarily granting management control over your shares — to protect _them_ from shareholder activism — inverted the typical governance dynamic. It reflected Buffett's conviction that aligned, competent management is more valuable than voting control. This philosophy later became central to Berkshire's acquisition model.
