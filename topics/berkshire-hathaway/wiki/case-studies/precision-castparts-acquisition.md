## Overview

In January 2016, Berkshire acquired Precision Castparts Corp. (PCC) for more than **$32B in cash** — the largest all-cash acquisition in Berkshire history. PCC was the world's premier supplier of aerospace components, led by CEO Mark Donegan, operating 162 plants in 13 countries with 30,466 employees. The business fit every one of Buffett's [four filters](../principles/four-filters.md) — except the fourth: price.

Four years later, Buffett wrote off **$11B** and delivered one of his most candid confessions: "I paid too much." By 2025, the business had recovered to generate **$2.4B in operating cash flows**, vindicating the franchise while confirming the overpayment. PCC is the mirror image of [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md) — same error (overpaying), opposite outcomes. Dexter's moat evaporated; PCC's endured.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[2015 Letter]** Todd Combs brought PCC to Buffett's attention — "one of my best moves" in the successor team's growing influence. The acquisition thesis:

1. **Irreplaceable market position.** PCC produces precision aerospace components under multi-year contracts, with **700+ sole-sourced parts** critical to major aircraft manufacturers. Customers cannot easily switch suppliers for safety-critical castings.
2. **Exceptional management.** Mark Donegan described as "the da Vinci of his craft" — alongside Jacob Harpaz at [ISCAR](../entities/iscar.md), one of two managers Buffett awarded the highest operational praise.
3. **Capital deployment scale.** At $32B+, PCC absorbed a meaningful fraction of Berkshire's cash pile and elevated the operating earnings base.
4. **Berkshire model fit.** All-cash purchase (no shareholder dilution), existing management retained, unlimited capital for bolt-on acquisitions. PCC made Berkshire the owner of **10¼ companies** that would individually qualify for the Fortune 500[^1].

## Outcome

### The Writedown: "I Paid Too Much"

**[2020 Letter]** Buffett's confession was the most candid acquisition-error admission since [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md)'s "Guinness Book" moment:

> "No one misled me in any way — I was simply too optimistic about PCC's normalized profit potential." — 2020 Letter

The $11B writedown was "almost entirely PCC." Buffett was explicit about which filter failed: the error was in **filter (4) — price** — not in filters (1)-(3) covering business quality, management, and simplicity. PCC remained "the best in its business." Donegan "pours the same energy into the business" as before the acquisition.

> "PCC is far from my first error of that sort. But it's a big one." — 2020 Letter[^2]

The aerospace downturn triggered by COVID accelerated the recognition, but the root cause was valuation misjudgment at purchase — paying for optimistic normalized earnings rather than conservative ones.

### The Recovery and Jenkintown Fire

**[2025 Letter]** PCC has "worked through the most challenging part of that period." Operating cash flows reached **$2.4B** — comfortably above the $1.7B pre-acquisition level and dramatically above the post-writedown trough.

The **Jenkintown, Pennsylvania fire** (February 2025) destroyed a facility producing **700+ sole-sourced parts** critical to major aerospace customers. Donegan's team responded by redistributing production across US and international sites "without compromising safety, quality, or delivery standards. No customer experienced a production line stoppage."

Greg Abel presented PCC's fire response as the exemplar of Berkshire's operating model: "decentralized leadership, clear accountability, and exceptional execution under pressure." The episode simultaneously demonstrated PCC's competitive moat — a supplier producing 700+ sole-sourced parts is genuinely irreplaceable — and the management quality that justified the acquisition in the first place[^3].

## Buffett's Retrospective

The PCC story unfolds in three acts:

1. **2015-2016: Confidence.** "Fits perfectly into the Berkshire model." Donegan as "da Vinci." Todd Combs's identification praised.
2. **2020: Confession.** "I paid too much." The most important distinction: the _business_ was never the problem. The _price_ was the problem. Buffett separates franchise quality from acquisition price — a nuance the market often misses.
3. **2025: Vindication of the franchise.** $2.4B operating cash flows prove the business was always excellent. The Jenkintown fire proves management was always exceptional. The error was purely one of overpaying for normalized earnings.

## Lessons

1. **Price discipline is its own filter — and the hardest to maintain.** Filters 1-3 (understandable business, durable competitive advantage, able and honest management) were all passed with flying colors. Filter 4 (sensible price) was the failure. When you're deploying $32B of cash and the business is genuinely wonderful, the temptation to rationalize a full price is enormous. See [Four Filters](../principles/four-filters.md).

2. **A great franchise survives overpayment; a mediocre one doesn't.** Compare PCC to [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md): both were overpaid acquisitions. Dexter's competitive advantage evaporated as manufacturing moved offshore — the business went to zero. PCC's moat (sole-sourced aerospace parts, multi-year contracts, irreplaceable manufacturing expertise) survived intact through a pandemic downturn. Overpaying for a great business is expensive; overpaying for a mediocre one is catastrophic.

3. **Honest confession preserves institutional credibility.** Buffett's "I paid too much" — with explicit absolution of management and the business — is a model for how CEOs should discuss acquisition errors. No blame-shifting, no euphemism, no restructuring charge buried in footnotes.

4. **Operating recoveries validate the "permanent ownership" model.** A public-market investor who bought PCC at the same valuation would have sold during the downturn. Berkshire's permanent-ownership structure allowed the franchise to recover on its own timeline — $2.4B in operating cash by 2025 would have been impossible to capture through a buy-sell-buy cycle.

5. **Successor teams create genuine value — and genuine risk.** Todd Combs identified PCC, and Buffett praised the identification. But the pricing decision was Buffett's alone. The lesson for post-Buffett Berkshire: the team can source great businesses, but [capital allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md) discipline on price must survive the founder.
