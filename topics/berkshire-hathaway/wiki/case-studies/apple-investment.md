## Overview

In 2011, Buffett made his first major technology bet — **$10.9B** for 63.9 million IBM shares (5.5% ownership). Six years later, he quietly exited at a loss and redirected the capital into Apple, which became the largest equity holding in Berkshire's history. By 2025, Berkshire's Apple position stood at **$62B on roughly $31B cost**, while IBM's $13.8B had produced nothing. The pivot from IBM to Apple is Buffett's technology education compressed into a single decade — and a masterclass in the difference between a business with a shrinking moat and one with a consumer franchise that deepens over time.

## The IBM Chapter (2011-2017)

### Rationale

**[2011 Letter]** IBM was positioned as the anchor of a new "Big Four" — alongside [American Express](../entities/american-express.md), [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md), and [Wells Fargo](../entities/wells-fargo.md). Buffett's thesis: IBM had transformed from a hardware manufacturer into a recurring-revenue services and software business. Its aggressive share repurchase program would compound Berkshire's ownership over time — the same mechanism that had worked spectacularly with American Express.

The letter contained Buffett's most detailed mathematical analysis of buyback economics, using IBM as the worked example: if IBM earns 20% on equity and repurchases 20% of shares, Berkshire's ownership rises dramatically even without buying additional shares. The counterintuitive conclusion: shareholders should hope for _lower_ stock prices during buyback periods[^1].

### Rising Ownership, Falling Value

| ---- | -------- | --------- | ------------ | ------ | -------------- |
| 2011 | 63.9M    | 5.5%      | ~$11B        | $10.9B | Big Four entry |
| 2012 | —        | 6.0%      | —            | —      | Buybacks work  |
| 2013 | 68.1M    | 6.3%      | $12.8B       | $11.7B | —              |
| 2014 | 77.0M    | 7.8%      | $12.3B       | $13.2B | **Below cost** |
| 2015 | 81.0M    | 8.4%      | $11.2B       | $13.8B | $2.6B loss     |
| 2017 | Mostly 0 | —         | —            | —      | Quietly sold   |

The buyback math worked exactly as Buffett described — Berkshire's ownership rose from 5.5% to 8.4% without purchasing additional shares. But the thesis had a fatal flaw: the business itself was declining. IBM's revenue fell for 22 consecutive quarters. When the underlying economics deteriorate, buybacks simply redistribute a shrinking pie more efficiently[^2].

### The Quiet Exit

**[2017 Letter]** IBM was "largely sold" — no fanfare, no confession essay, no "Guinness Book" moment like [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md). The exit was notably understated compared to Buffett's usual candor about mistakes. Apple, at $28.2B, replaced IBM as the largest equity holding in the same letter[^3].

## The Apple Chapter (2017-2025)

### Entry and Rationale

The Apple position was identified by **Todd Combs and Ted Weschler** — Berkshire's investment successors — demonstrating their growing influence. Buffett, who had avoided technology for decades, recognized in Apple something he'd missed in IBM: a **consumer franchise**, not a technology company. Apple's moat was brand loyalty and ecosystem lock-in — the same qualities that made [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md) a great investment. The iPhone was not a gadget; it was a daily habit with enormous switching costs.

### The Buyback Compounding Machine

The same repurchase thesis that failed with IBM succeeded spectacularly with Apple — because Apple's business was _growing_ while buying back shares:

**[2020 Letter]** Berkshire owned 5.2% of Apple in mid-2018. By year-end 2020, without buying a single additional share, ownership had risen to 5.4% through Apple's buybacks alone. Combined with Berkshire's own share repurchases, Berkshire shareholders owned **10% more Apple** than two years earlier — at zero cost[^4].

**[2021 Letter]** Ownership reached **5.55%**. GAAP reported only $785M in Apple dividends, but Berkshire's proportional share of Apple's earnings was approximately **$5.6B** — seven times the reported figure. Tim Cook praised as a "brilliant CEO"[^5].

> "Too much of a good thing can be... wonderful." — Mae West, quoted by Buffett regarding Apple (2020 Letter)

### Four Giants Status

**[2020 Letter]** Apple was elevated to one of four "family jewels" — the only equity (non-controlled) position alongside the insurance empire, BNSF, and BHE. **[2021 Letter]** Formalized as one of the "Four Giants": insurance float, Apple, [BNSF](../entities/bnsf.md), and [MidAmerican/BHE](../entities/midamerican-energy.md). Apple is the only non-controlled business in the top four — Buffett's strongest endorsement that a minority stake in a wonderful business can be more profitable than full ownership of a good one[^6].

### The Endgame: Household Name

**[2022 Letter]** The "Secret Sauce" comparison places Apple alongside Coke and AmEx as investments where patience and investee buybacks produced extraordinary returns from modest initial positions[^7].

**[2024-2025 Letters]** The four-stock US equity core: Apple ($62B), AmEx ($56B), Coke ($28B), Moody's ($12.6B) — totaling **$158.6B on $9.1B combined cost**. "Household names" that Berkshire has "no intent to sell"[^8].

| --------------------- | ----------------- | -------------------------- |
| Cost basis            | $13.8B            | ~$31B                      |

## Lessons

1. **Buybacks only compound wealth when the underlying business is growing.** IBM's aggressive repurchases couldn't overcome revenue decline. Apple's buybacks compounded a growing earnings base — the same mechanism, opposite results. See [Share Repurchase Economics](../financials/share-repurchase-economics.md).

2. **A consumer franchise beats a technology platform.** Buffett's error with IBM was categorizing it as a recurring-revenue franchise. It was a services business facing commoditization. Apple, despite being a "tech" company, is functionally a consumer brand with enormous switching costs — closer to Coca-Cola than to IBM.

3. **The successor team adds genuine value.** Todd Combs and Ted Weschler identified Apple — the most profitable single investment in Berkshire's history. The IBM-to-Apple pivot validates Buffett's succession strategy and demonstrates that the "business-picker" framework transfers across generations.

4. **Silent exits can be as instructive as loud confessions.** Buffett's extended mea culpas on [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md) and [Precision Castparts](precision-castparts-acquisition.md) set expectations for dramatic confessions. The IBM exit was quiet — suggesting the error was one of analytical judgment (fixable) rather than process failure (systemic).

5. **Patience with winners matters more than speed with losers.** Selling IBM avoided further losses, but the real value creation came from _holding_ Apple through volatility. The "Four Giants" designation explicitly commits to permanence — the same philosophy that produced [Coca-Cola](coca-cola-investment.md)'s 52-year compounding.
