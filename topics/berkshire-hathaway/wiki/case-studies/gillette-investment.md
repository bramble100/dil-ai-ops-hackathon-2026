## Overview

In July 1989, Berkshire invested **$600M in Gillette convertible preferred stock** — the largest of three preferred-stock investments that year (alongside [Salomon](../entities/salomon-inc.md) and USAir). The preferred converted to common equity in April 1991, and the position grew to approximately **$1.8B** in market value by 1994. Gillette merged into Procter & Gamble in Q4 2005.

Gillette illustrates two critical principles: (1) the razor/blade business model as the archetype of a consumer franchise with recurring revenue and pricing power; and (2) the honest comparison between preferred-stock investments (mediocre when right, disappointing when wrong) and direct equity purchases in businesses the investor truly understands.

## Buffett's Rationale

### Genuine Understanding vs. Agnosticism

**[1989 Letter]** Gillette was the only one of the three 1989 preferred investments where Buffett claimed genuine understanding: "Gillette's business is very much the kind we like. Charlie and I think we understand the company's economics and therefore believe we can make a reasonably intelligent guess about its future."

For USAir and Champion, he was "agnostic" about industry economics — relying on the preferred structure for downside protection. This distinction became prophetic: Gillette thrived; USAir proved a money-losing lesson; Champion was mediocre.

### The Razor/Blade Model

Gillette held **more than 60% of worldwide razor blade value sales** — a global dominance Buffett considered virtually unmatched: "Leaving aside chewing gum, I know of no other significant businesses in which the leading company has long enjoyed such global power."[^1]

The economics: razors sold at modest margins create a captive customer base that purchases blades at high margins, indefinitely. Minimal capital requirements relative to earnings. Universal demand. Brand loyalty reinforced by product quality.

### The Moat Castle Metaphor

**[1994 Letter]** Gillette and [Coca-Cola](../entities/coca-cola.md) together inspired Buffett's most memorable competitive-advantage metaphor: "The might of their brand names, the attributes of their products, and the strength of their distribution systems give them an enormous competitive advantage, setting up a protective moat around their economic castles."

## Price Discipline

- **Structure:** $600M convertible preferred; 8¾% dividend; mandatory redemption in 10 years; convertible at $50/share
- **Downside:** If Gillette disappointed, Berkshire would earn the preferred yield plus eventual redemption — "a mediocre return"
- **Upside:** If Gillette performed at American industry averages or better, conversion to common would produce "reasonably attractive results"
- **Honest risk:** The preferred yield meant surrendering flexibility — Berkshire would miss "significant opportunities that are bound to present themselves during the decade"

### The P&G Merger (2005)

**[2005 Letter]** Gillette merged into Procter & Gamble (closed Q4 2005). Jim Kilts's turnaround praised as exceptional — one of the few CEOs whose compensation was justified by performance. The transaction generated a **$5.0B paper gain** under GAAP, which Buffett dismissed as "meaningless" since Berkshire simply exchanged Gillette shares for P&G shares.

Buffett's verdict on Kilts-era Duracell: "From the day he arrived, Jim saw Duracell as a drag on the razor/blade business."

## Buffett's Retrospective

### Preferred vs. Equity — The Honest Assessment

**[1992 Letter]** Despite Gillette's success, Buffett concluded that Berkshire's overall record with negotiated preferred purchases was "inferior" to secondary-market common stock purchases. The market's clearing price mechanism periodically offers better prices than negotiated deals.

The lesson: if you truly understand the business (Gillette), buy the equity directly. Use preferreds only when you lack conviction about industry economics but trust management — and accept that the risk-adjusted returns will be mediocre.

### Colman Mockler

**[1990 Letter]** Mockler died in January 1991, just as the preferred was about to convert. Buffett's tribute: Mockler embodied "integrity, courage, and modesty." A Forbes cover story published days before his death attributed Gillette's dominance to its "devotion to quality" rather than marketing savvy[^2].

## Lessons

1. **The razor/blade model is the ideal business franchise.** Recurring revenue from a captive base, pricing power through product quality, minimal capital needs, global scalability. When you find one, hold forever.
2. **Use the right instrument for your conviction level.** Preferred stock when agnostic about industry; common equity when you understand the economics. The preferred structure is insurance against ignorance — but insurance has a cost.
3. **Negotiated deals underperform market purchases.** The market periodically offers franchise businesses cheaper than any negotiated price. Patience in public markets beats deal-making.
4. **Global dominance in essential products creates the widest moats.** 60%+ worldwide market share in razors, earned through product quality and maintained through distribution scale, is nearly impregnable.
5. **Management transitions matter.** Mockler built the franchise; Kilts rationalized it; P&G absorbed it. Each phase had different value drivers. The business quality outlived all three management regimes.
