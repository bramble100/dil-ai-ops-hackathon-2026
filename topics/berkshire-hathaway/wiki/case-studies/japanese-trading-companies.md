# Japanese Trading Companies — Berkshire's International Pivot

> Five trading houses. $15.4B invested. $35.4B in market value. $862M in annual dividends. Financed with yen debt at 1.2%. Berkshire's first major international equity portfolio — and possibly its most elegant capital allocation move since float-funded acquisitions.

## Overview

Between 2019 and 2020, Berkshire quietly accumulated ~9% positions in five Japanese _sogo shosha_ (general trading companies): **Itochu**, **Marubeni**, **Mitsubishi**, **Mitsui**, and **Sumitomo**. The investment was disclosed in 2023 and expanded through 2025. By yearend 2025, the combined portfolio stood at **$35.4B** on a **$15.4B** cost basis — a **130% unrealized gain** — while generating **$862M** in annual dividends[^1].

What makes this position distinctive is not just the returns but the _architecture_. Berkshire financed the purchases with ¥1.3 trillion in yen-denominated bonds at an average interest rate of just **1.2%**, creating a natural currency hedge: yen liabilities offset yen-denominated equity, while any yen depreciation against the dollar generates windfall gains. By 2024, cumulative after-tax currency gains had reached **$2.3B**[^2].

This is Berkshire's first significant international equity portfolio since the [ISCAR](../entities/iscar.md) acquisition in 2006 — and the largest public-equity position initiated in Buffett's final years as CEO.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[2023 Letter]** The five companies were selected for their **shareholder-friendly policies**: less aggressive executive compensation than Western peers and dividend payouts of roughly **one-third of earnings** — a retention-to-distribution ratio that mirrors Berkshire's own philosophy of retaining most earnings while returning enough to demonstrate discipline[^1].

**[2024 Letter]** Buffett expanded the ownership ceiling from 9% to **10%** per company, signaling increased conviction. The expected dividend yield was extraordinary: **$812M** in projected 2025 income against only **~$135M** in yen debt interest — a net yield of nearly **$680M** annually on the equity position[^2].

The rationale combined several Buffett principles simultaneously:

1. **Scale deployment.** At $561B+ in net worth, only a handful of investments can move the needle. The _sogo shosha_ are among the world's largest companies — big enough to absorb billions in capital. See [Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md).

2. **Business quality.** These are diversified industrial conglomerates with century-long histories, natural-resource exposure, and global logistics infrastructure — businesses Buffett understands from his experience with [BNSF](../entities/bnsf.md) and [BHE](../entities/midamerican-energy.md).

3. **Valuation.** Japanese trading companies have historically traded at significant discounts to book value, partly due to complex cross-shareholdings and conglomerate discounts — a gap Buffett recognized as unjustified.

4. **Currency architecture.** The yen-financed structure was not an afterthought. Borrowing in yen at 1.2% to buy yen-earning businesses created a natural hedge — dividends service the debt in the same currency. Any yen depreciation is pure profit on the dollar-denominated books.

## The Yen Financing Strategy

The financing structure deserves separate treatment because it represents a rare window into Buffett's thinking on currency and leverage:

| Metric                              | Value                                  |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Yen bonds issued                    | ¥1.3 trillion                          |
| Average interest rate               | 1.2%                                   |
| Weighted average life               | 5.75 years                             |
| 2024 yen currency gains (after-tax) | $2.3B cumulative ($850M in 2024 alone) |
| 2025 dividend income                | $862M                                  |
| Yen interest expense                | ~$135M                                 |

**[Analysis]** The structure effectively creates a **carry trade at institutional scale**: borrow cheap in yen, invest in yen-earning businesses yielding 5-6% on cost, and pocket the spread. But unlike a speculative carry trade, the currency risk is hedged — the assets and liabilities are in the same currency. The dollar-yen exchange rate only matters for Berkshire's consolidated reporting, not for the investment's internal economics[^2].

This is the same structural logic that makes [insurance float](../financials/float.md) so powerful: cheap funding deployed into productive assets. The difference is that float is denominated in dollars; here, Buffett replicated the model in yen.

## Outcome

**[2023 Letter]** At disclosure: ¥1.6T cost, ¥2.9T market value. Unrealized gain of **$8B+**. Yen bonds of ¥1.3T partially hedged the currency exposure[^1].

**[2024 Letter]** Cost basis grew to **$13.8B** as positions expanded. Market value reached **$23.5B**. The 10% ownership ceiling was relaxed — a signal that Buffett wanted to go deeper. Cumulative yen currency gains hit **$2.3B** after tax, with **$850M** realized in 2024 alone. Projected 2025 dividends: **$812M**[^2].

**[2025 Letter]** Combined market value: **$35.4B** on **$15.4B** cost. Dividends received: **$862M** (exceeding the $812M projection). The portfolio represented **12%** of Berkshire's total equity holdings and was the second-largest public equity position after Apple. Active rebalancing was visible: Mitsubishi and Itochu received disproportionate weightings ($9.2B and $8.9B respectively) while Marubeni ($4.5B) and Sumitomo ($4.0B) were trimmed — suggesting Buffett favored certain business mixes or valuations within the group[^3].

## Lessons

1. **Currency-matched financing is a structural edge, not speculation.** Borrowing in yen to buy yen-earning businesses eliminates currency risk at the operational level while creating optionality at the reporting level. This is the international equivalent of float-funded investing — cheap, naturally hedged capital deployed at scale. The $2.3B in currency gains was a bonus, not the thesis.

2. **Shareholder-friendly capital allocation translates across cultures.** Buffett selected these companies because they distribute ~1/3 of earnings as dividends and maintain conservative compensation — the same criteria he applies to American businesses. The lesson: good capital allocation is universal, and companies that exhibit it in any market deserve premium attention.

3. **Scale demands international thinking.** At $561B+ in net worth, Berkshire's investable universe is tiny. The _sogo shosha_ — each worth tens of billions — are among the few positions that can absorb meaningful capital. The move signals that post-Buffett Berkshire, under [Greg Abel](../entities/greg-abel.md), will likely maintain international equity positions as a permanent allocation category. See [Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md).

4. **Patient accumulation before disclosure maximizes value.** Berkshire spent years quietly building these positions before the 2023 disclosure. This is the same playbook used with [Coca-Cola](coca-cola-investment.md) (1988), [Wells Fargo](wells-fargo-investment.md) (1990), and [Apple](apple-investment.md) (2016) — accumulate at favorable prices, disclose only when required or when the position is built.

5. **Minority positions can work when the capital allocation culture is aligned.** Unlike most Berkshire investments — where Buffett seeks control or overwhelming influence — the Japanese positions cap at 9-10%. This works because the companies' own capital allocation (dividends, buybacks, conservative compensation) already mirrors Berkshire's philosophy. No operational control is needed when the management culture is already aligned.
