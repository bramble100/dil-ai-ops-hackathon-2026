# USAir — The Airline Mistake That Paid Off Anyway

> "Sloppy analysis." A $358M convertible preferred in a high-cost airline → dividend suspension → 75% writedown → an unexpected recovery to $73/share. The only case study in this wiki where a confessed mistake produced a profit — and the one that taught Buffett that bad industries defeat good investments.

## Overview

In 1989, Berkshire invested **$358M** in USAir convertible preferred stock (9¼% coupon, convertible at $60/share) alongside similar preferred investments in [Gillette](gillette-investment.md) ($600M) and Champion International ($300M). Gillette became a multi-bagger. Champion was sold at cost plus 19%. USAir nearly destroyed the capital — dividend suspended in September 1994, position written down to **$89.5M** (25 cents on the dollar) — before staging an improbable recovery that saw the common stock rise from **$4 to $73** by 1997[^1][^4][^6].

The case is unique in Buffett's portfolio: a self-confessed analytical failure in a structurally broken industry that, through a combination of industry restructuring and luck, ended up profitable. Buffett's lesson was not about recovery — it was about **why the investment should never have been made**.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[1989 Letter]** The three convertible preferreds (Gillette, USAir, Champion) were structured identically: high coupons with conversion options, providing a floor through the preferred dividend with upside participation through conversion to common[^1].

Buffett's stated expectation: "mediocre return if industry economics hinder, reasonably attractive if they earn average returns." This framing reveals the analytical gap — Buffett was betting on "average returns" in an industry where deregulation had permanently destroyed the economics of high-cost carriers.

The convertible preferred structure was meant to provide downside protection through the coupon. In practice, when USAir's business deteriorated enough to suspend dividends, the preferred's protection evaporated along with the common.

## The Descent

**[1990 Letter]** Just one year after purchase, Buffett acknowledged USAir was "worth substantially less" than cost. The airline industry's post-deregulation dynamics were accelerating: low-cost carriers like Southwest were permanently undercutting legacy airlines on price, and capital infusions only delayed the inevitable[^2].

**[1991 Letter]** Conditions worsened. Buffett described "USAir's deepening troubles amid airline industry kamikazes" — his term for competitors engaging in suicidal price wars that destroyed industry profitability for everyone[^3].

**[1994 Letter — The Confession]** The dividend was suspended in September 1994. Buffett wrote down the position to **$89.5M** — a 75% loss. The "Mistake Du Jour" section ranked USAir as the **gold-medal mistake** (ahead of the Cap Cities partial sale, which was merely "silver"):

- **"Sloppy analysis"** of a high-cost carrier in a deregulated commodity business
- **"When deregulation came, the capacity of low-cost carriers forced old-line high-cost airlines to cut fares — a day of reckoning only delayed by capital infusions"**
- Herb Kelleher's observation that **"bankruptcy has become a health spa for airlines"** — competitors could strip costs through bankruptcy without changing the structural dynamics
- **"You don't have to make it back the way you lost it"** — Buffett's signal that he expected permanent impairment[^4]

The diagnosis was devastating: the investment failed not because of execution but because **the industry's economics were fundamentally broken**. No amount of management quality or capital infusion could overcome the structural cost disadvantage of a legacy carrier competing against Southwest's inherently lower cost base.

## The Recovery

**[1995 Letter]** Despite the confession, the preferred continued paying — albeit at **penalty dividends of 5% over prime** (a distressed coupon reflecting the suspended regular dividend). The position was valued at roughly **60% of par**, a partial recovery from the $89.5M nadir but still well below cost. Buffett called it "persistent heartburn"[^5].

**[1996 Letter]** USAir's turnaround gained traction. The company paid **$47.9M in accumulated dividend arrears**, and the common stock — which had traded as low as $4 — recovered to **$40**. Yet Buffett "still expects to sell," maintaining his conviction that the industry was structurally flawed even as the stock recovered[^6].

**[1997 Letter]** In the retrospective of all four convertible preferreds, Buffett reported that **all four were ultimately profitable** — including USAir, whose common had risen to **$73**. His self-deprecating verdict: "Next time I make a big, dumb decision... Phone Mr. Wolf"[^7].

## Buffett's Retrospective

The 1994 confession is one of the most analytically transparent in the letters. Buffett didn't blame management, the economy, or bad luck. He blamed **his own analysis**:

1. He understood that airlines were commodity businesses but invested anyway, hoping "average returns" would suffice.
2. He underestimated how permanently deregulation would shift the cost structure — legacy carriers couldn't match low-cost competitors at any fare level.
3. The convertible preferred structure, designed for downside protection, failed when the business deteriorated enough to suspend dividends.

The recovery didn't change his verdict. Even in 1997, when USAir's common hit $73 and the investment was profitable, Buffett's tone was sheepish, not vindicated. The lesson was not "it worked out" — it was **"I got lucky in an investment that should have failed."**

Compare this with [Precision Castparts](precision-castparts-acquisition.md), where Buffett confessed "I paid too much" but maintained the business was excellent. With USAir, the confession went deeper: **the business itself was fatally flawed**, and no price would have made it a good investment for a long-term holder.

## Lessons

1. **Industry structure trumps everything.** USAir was not a bad company — it was a company in a bad industry. Deregulation created permanent overcapacity, low-cost competitors destroyed pricing power, and bankruptcy allowed rivals to reset costs without penalty. This is the airline version of the [textile operations](textile-operations.md) lesson: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact." See [Business Quality](../principles/business-quality.md).

2. **Structured downside protection can fail.** The convertible preferred was designed to provide a floor — the 9¼% coupon would protect capital even if the common went nowhere. But when the business deteriorated enough, the dividend was suspended, converting the preferred from a "mediocre" return to a near-total loss. The lesson: structural protections are only as strong as the business that backs them.

3. **Deregulation is a permanent economic event.** Buffett's "sloppy analysis" was in treating deregulation as a temporary disruption rather than a permanent restructuring. High-cost carriers could never recapture their pre-deregulation economics. The insight generalizes: when regulation changes the rules of an industry, the old players rarely adapt fast enough. See [Four Filters](../principles/four-filters.md) on durability.

4. **Lucky recoveries are not vindication.** USAir's rise from $4 to $73 was not a validation of Buffett's analysis — it was a cyclical recovery in an industry that remains structurally impaired. Buffett's refusal to take credit ("Phone Mr. Wolf") reflects his understanding that **process matters more than outcome**. A bad decision that produces a profit is still a bad decision.

5. **"You don't have to make it back the way you lost it."** This principle — stated during the 1994 writedown — reflects Buffett's sunk-cost discipline. Rather than doubling down or averaging into USAir, he accepted the loss and moved on. The capital freed from that mental commitment was available for better opportunities. See [Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md).
