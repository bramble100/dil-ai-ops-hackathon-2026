## Overview

The American Express investment spans Buffett's entire career — from a $1 research report in 1953 to a **$56.1B** position in 2025. Berkshire completed its purchases by 1998, acquiring **151,610,700 shares** at a total cost of **$1,287M**. Then it did nothing for 27 years. [AmEx](../entities/american-express.md)'s own share repurchases drove Berkshire's ownership from **5.5% to 22.1%** without a single additional purchase. The result: a **43.6x return** and **$479M in annual dividends** — a 37.2% yield on original cost.

This is Buffett's purest case study in what he calls "masterly inactivity."

## Buffett's Rationale

### 40 Years of Familiarity

**[1994 Letter]** Buffett's connection to AmEx spans four decades:

1. **1953:** Purchased IDS stock (later American Express Financial Advisors) at P/E of 3x — "a lot of low-hanging fruit in those days." Produced a research report sold for $1 via a Wall Street Journal ad.
2. **Mid-1960s:** Put ~40% of Buffett Partnership capital ($13M) into AmEx after the salad-oil scandal — the partnership's largest single investment. The key insight: the card business's franchise was intact despite the scandal. The liability was isolated, excisable.
3. **1993-1994:** Built new position as AmEx recovered — 27.76M shares at $723.9M cost, 5.5% ownership.

**[1980 Letter]** Buffett had earlier classified both GEICO and American Express as "not true turnarounds but extraordinary business franchises with a localized excisable cancer" — identifying the pattern that would define his most successful investments[^1].

### The Franchise Thesis

AmEx possessed qualities that made patience rational: a global payments network with switching costs, a premium brand commanding higher merchant fees, and a customer base selected for spending power. Founded in **1850**, it embodied what Buffett would later call "the need for unquestioned financial trust" — a timeless human requirement[^2].

> "A long-term familiarity with a company and its products is often helpful in evaluating it." — 1994 Letter

## Price Discipline

- **Cost:** $1,287M for 151,610,700 shares (completed by 1998)
- **1994 earnings:** $1.4B (up from $12.5M in 1964 — 112x growth in 30 years)
- **Approach:** Bought during recovery, not at peak crisis. Completed position over several years.
- **Last purchase:** 1998. "We haven't bought a share since 1998!" — 2020 Letter

## The Buyback Compounding Engine

The distinctive feature of the AmEx investment is how Berkshire's ownership grew **without action**. AmEx's own share repurchases reduced shares outstanding, automatically increasing Berkshire's percentage on unchanged share count:

**The math:** From 2015 to 2025, ownership grew from 15.6% to 22.1% — a **42% increase** in economic interest — without spending a dollar. Every AmEx repurchase below intrinsic value transferred wealth from departing shareholders to remaining ones, including Berkshire[^3].

### Dividend Growth: The Silent Compounding

| Year | Annual Dividends | Yield on Cost |
| ---- | ---------------- | ------------- |
| 2004 | ~$100M           | ~8%           |
| 2022 | $302M            | 23.5%         |
| 2023 | ~$340M           | ~26%          |
| 2025 | $479M            | 37.2%         |

**[2023 Letter]** Berkshire's share of AmEx's 2023 earnings "considerably exceeded the $1.3 billion cost of our long-ago purchase" — meaning AmEx earns Berkshire more in a single year than the entire original investment[^4].

## Buffett's Retrospective

### The "Secret Sauce" (2022)

**[2022 Letter]** AmEx and [Coca-Cola](coca-cola-investment.md) are presented as twin demonstrations of patience:

> "The weeds wither away in significance as the flowers bloom. Over time, it takes just a few winners to work wonders." — 2022 Letter

Both positions account for ~5% of Berkshire's net worth — the same weighting they had decades ago — because the rest of the portfolio grew at a similar pace. But the absolute dollar values transformed: $1.3B → $22B for AmEx (2022), with dividends growing from $41M to $302M. Buffett's verdict: **"one good decision every five years"** is all an investor needs[^5].

### The Rip Van Winkle Strategy (2023)

**[2023 Letter]** AmEx and Coke described as a 20+ year "Rip Van Winkle slumber." Berkshire "did not buy or sell a share of either" in 2023. Both increased earnings and dividends again. The lesson: when you find a truly wonderful business, the optimal strategy is to do nothing — literally nothing — for decades[^6].

### The Endgame (2024-2025)

**[2024 Letter]** AmEx named to the four-stock US core (with Apple, Coke, Moody's) — "household names" with "no intent to sell."

**[2025 Letter]** Abel's first CEO letter confirms: 9 holdings, $194B on $24.5B cost. AmEx at 22.1% ownership and $56.1B is the second-largest position. "The values of the businesses underlying these nine holdings far exceed their carrying costs"[^7].

## Lessons

1. **The best investment action is often inaction.** Berkshire's entire AmEx return — 43.6x over 27+ years — required exactly one decision: buying. Every year since 1998 was "do nothing."
2. **Investee buybacks compound ownership for free.** From 5.5% to 22.1% without purchasing a single share. This mechanism is invisible to income statements but transforms economics. See [Share Repurchase Economics](../financials/share-repurchase-economics.md).
3. **Familiarity reduces risk.** Buffett's 40 years of observing AmEx — from IDS at P/E 3x to the salad-oil crisis to the 1990s rebuilding — gave him conviction that mere financial analysis cannot provide.
4. **The "franchise with excisable cancer" pattern repeats.** First applied to AmEx (1960s) and [GEICO](geico-investment.md) (1970s), this mental model identifies businesses where temporary problems create permanent buying opportunities.
5. **Dividend growth proves franchise durability.** A 37.2% yield on cost is not a fluke — it's 27 years of compounding earnings applied to an unchanged share count on an unchanged position.
