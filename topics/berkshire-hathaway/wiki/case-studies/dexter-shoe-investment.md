## Overview

In November 1993, Berkshire acquired Dexter Shoe — a Maine-based manufacturer of popular-priced footwear — for **25,203 Berkshire shares**, worth approximately **$433M** at the time. Buffett declared it "one of the best-managed companies Charlie and I have seen in our business lifetimes." Harold Alfond had founded the company in 1956 with $10,000; Peter Lunder joined in 1958. Frank Rooney of [H.H. Brown](../entities/hh-brown.md) brokered the introduction.

By 2000, the business was essentially worthless as shoe manufacturing moved offshore. But those 25,203 Berkshire shares kept compounding — reaching **$3.5B** by 2007 and **$5.7B** by 2014. It became, in Buffett's words, a case "deserving a spot in the Guinness Book of World Records" for financial destruction.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[1993 Letter]** Three attractions: (1) exceptional management (Alfond and Lunder had built the business over 37 years); (2) dominant position in domestic shoe manufacturing (7.5M+ pairs annually, 77 retail outlets, major supplier to Nordstrom and J.C. Penney); (3) the stock-for-stock structure gave sellers "a smaller interest in a group of terrific businesses" with no immediate tax liability.

The deal was done without investment bankers, industry studies, or extended due diligence — consistent with Berkshire's speed advantage. But speed also meant Buffett skipped the structural analysis that would have revealed shoe manufacturing's vulnerability to offshore competition.

## Outcome

### Business Destruction

Global shoe manufacturing was inexorably moving to lower-cost countries. By 2000, Dexter's domestic manufacturing advantage had evaporated. Shoe operations lost **$46.2M pre-tax** in 2001. Restructuring under Frank Rooney and Jim Issler followed, but the business remained structurally challenged and was eventually folded into other Berkshire shoe operations.

### The Escalating Cost

The devastating math of paying with a compounding asset:

| Year | Value of 25,203 BRK Shares | Business Value | Real Cost |
| ---- | -------------------------- | -------------- | --------- |
| 1993 | $433M                      | $433M          | Even      |
| 2000 | ~$1.5B                     | ~$0            | $1.5B     |
| 2007 | $3.5B                      | $0             | $3.5B     |
| 2014 | $5.7B                      | $0             | $5.7B     |

Every year Berkshire stock compounds at ~20%, the effective cost of Dexter rises. A finite cash payment would have capped the loss at $433M. Stock payment made it infinite and escalating.

## Buffett's Retrospective

**Three confessions, deepening over time:**

**[2000 Letter]** "I clearly made a mistake in paying what I did for Dexter in 1993. Furthermore, I compounded that mistake in a huge way by using Berkshire shares in payment."

**[2001 Letter]** The most thorough admission: "I've made three decisions relating to Dexter that have hurt you in a major way: (1) buying it in the first place; (2) paying for it with stock; and (3) procrastinating when the need for changes in its operations was obvious. I would like to lay these mistakes on Charlie but they were mine."

**[2007 Letter]** Revised damage: $3.5B — "What I had assessed as durable competitive advantage vanished within a few years."

**[2014 Letter]** Final accounting: $5.7B — "As a financial disaster, this one deserves a spot in the Guinness Book of World Records."

The retrospectives reveal a critical insight: the _original_ error (buying a vulnerable business) was bad but finite. The _compounding_ error (paying with Berkshire stock) made it catastrophic. Had Buffett paid cash, Dexter would be a footnote alongside other small mistakes. Paying with stock made it the single most expensive acquisition error in Berkshire history.

## Lessons

1. **Payment method matters as much as business quality.** When you pay with an asset that compounds at 20%+ annually, the real cost escalates forever. Cash payments cap the downside; stock payments don't. See [Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md).
2. **Durable competitive advantage must survive globalization.** Domestic manufacturing cost advantages are inherently fragile when the product can be made elsewhere. Contrast with [See's Candies](sees-candies-acquisition.md) — a brand-driven franchise whose competitive advantage is location-specific and non-transferable.
3. **Speed of deal-making can skip critical analysis.** The same structural advantage (no bankers, no committees, three-week close) that won [Scott Fetzer](scott-fetzer-acquisition.md) cheaply led to insufficient analysis on Dexter. Speed is a tool, not a virtue.
4. **Admit mistakes early; delay compounds the cost.** Buffett's self-criticism of "procrastinating when the need for changes was obvious" applies to both operational restructuring and the broader decision to hold deteriorating businesses.
5. **Contrast with [General Re](general-re-turnaround.md).** Both were stock-deal acquisitions that proved costly. But General Re's franchise was structurally sound — fixable through operational discipline. Dexter's competitive position vanished permanently. The lesson: fix businesses with intact franchises; exit businesses whose moats have been breached.
