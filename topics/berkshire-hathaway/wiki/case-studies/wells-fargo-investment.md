## Overview

In 1989-1990, Berkshire built a **~10% stake** in [Wells Fargo](../entities/wells-fargo.md) for approximately **$290M** during a California real estate panic that drove the stock down ~50%. The investment demonstrated how to apply the [Mr. Market](../principles/mr-market.md) framework during a full-blown financial crisis — distinguishing solvency panic (buying opportunity) from genuine insolvency (destruction of capital).

The position grew to become one of Berkshire's "Big Four" equity holdings by 2005, peaked at ~$27B market value and 500M shares (2015), then was gradually unwound over 2016-2020 as the 30-year thesis played out.

## Buffett's Rationale

### The Worst-Case Analysis (1990 Letter)

Buffett's investment thesis was built on a deliberately pessimistic stress test:

Wells Fargo had $56B in assets, 20%+ ROE, 1.25% return on assets, and $1B+ in pre-tax earnings after $300M+ in loan-loss reserves. Even if **10% of all Wells Fargo loans** became problematic and produced **30% losses**, the bank would roughly break even for the year — painful but survivable, with long-term earnings power intact.

The market priced the stock as if this worst case was the _base case_ — offering $290M for 10% of a $56B bank earning 20%+ ROE.

### Management Conviction

**[1990 Letter]** Carl Reichardt and Paul Hazen were "the best managers in the business." Their partnership was "stronger than the sum of its parts" through mutual trust. Both attacked costs at peaks and troughs equally, paid well but abhorred headcount expansion, and stayed rigorously within their circle of competence.

**[1994 Letter]** When Reichardt retired: "Carl prepared his organization well for his departure, and the company is in outstanding hands."

### The Purchase Equivalence

$290M for 10% of Wells Fargo was equivalent to paying $2.9B for 100% of a $56B bank with 20%+ ROE. A negotiated acquisition of comparable quality would cost far more. Mr. Market was offering a franchise bank at panic-driven commodity-bank pricing.

**30-year arc:** $290M invested during bank panic → eventual multi-billion-dollar returns through dividends and appreciation → gradual exit as thesis completion and changing banking landscape warranted reallocation.

## Buffett's Retrospective

Buffett never published a detailed exit retrospective for Wells Fargo comparable to his Dexter Shoe or textile confessionals. The position's gradual unwinding (2016-2020) coincided with Wells Fargo's cross-selling scandal and regulatory consent orders — a management-quality deterioration that contrasted sharply with the Reichardt/Hazen era that originally attracted Buffett.

The 30-year holding period nonetheless validated the core thesis: buying a franchise bank at panic pricing, trusting capable management, and holding through multiple economic cycles produced outsized returns.

## Lessons

1. **Distinguish solvency panic from insolvency crisis.** When the market prices a solvent institution as if it's failing, the opportunity is enormous — but only if you can independently verify solvency. Buffett's worst-case analysis was the key analytical step. See [Mr. Market](../principles/mr-market.md).
2. **Management quality is the margin of safety in financial institutions.** Banks are leveraged businesses where management judgment determines survival. Reichardt and Hazen's cost discipline and conservative lending were the real collateral.
3. **Circle of competence determines position size.** Buffett committed $290M to a single bank stock — unusual concentration requiring genuine understanding of bank economics, not just pattern recognition.
4. **Buying at negotiated-acquisition prices in the open market.** Mr. Market periodically offers franchise businesses at prices below what a private buyer would pay. The public market investor's edge is patience, not access.
5. **Even great investments have endings.** The 30-year thesis played out; management and culture changed; the position was appropriately wound down. Permanent holding is a default, not a commandment.
