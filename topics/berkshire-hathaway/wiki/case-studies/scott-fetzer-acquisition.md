## Overview

Berkshire acquired [Scott & Fetzer](../entities/scott-fetzer.md) in early 1986 for approximately **$315M in cash** (plus ~$90M debt assumption). The company operated 17 businesses generating ~$700M in revenues, anchored by World Book (the #1 encyclopedia, 40% of sales) and Kirby vacuum cleaners. CEO **Ralph Schey** — whom Buffett called "a superb businessman and a straight shooter" — stayed for 14 years.

The acquisition unfolded in three weeks: Buffett wrote Schey on October 10, 1985; they dined October 22; the contract was signed the following week. No investment bankers, no industry studies, no due diligence committees. Scott Fetzer became Berkshire's canonical proof that [owner earnings](../principles/owner-earnings.md) — not GAAP earnings — measure a business's true value.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[1985 Letter]** Three forces created the opportunity: (1) an Ivan Boesky offer had failed; (2) an employee stock ownership plan was blocked in court; (3) the resulting uncertainty left few bidders. Berkshire's speed and certainty of close — cash, no conditions — won at a sensible price.

**Business quality:** World Book dominated direct-sales encyclopedias with customer loyalty, scale economies, and distribution advantages competitors couldn't replicate. Kirby, despite selling door-to-door vacuums, grew unit volumes consistently. Both required minimal capital to grow[^1].

**Management:** Schey's nine-year track record running 17 separately-managed companies demonstrated exceptional operational versatility. Buffett's model: "find such managers and leave them alone."

## Outcome: The Company O vs. Company N Teaching

### The 1986 Owner-Earnings Appendix

**[1986 Letter]** Buffett used Scott Fetzer as the centerpiece of his definitive owner-earnings argument. He created two fictional companies with identical operations:

- **Company O (old GAAP):** Pre-tax owner earnings of $40.2M
- **Company N (new GAAP, post-acquisition):** Reported earnings of only $28.6M — reduced by $11.6M in annual goodwill amortization on the $142.6M purchase premium

The $11.6M gap was purely an accounting artifact: "no cash, no economic cost." Yet it would persist for 38 more years under GAAP, systematically understating the business's earning power[^2].

### Nine Years of Compounding (1986-1994)

| Year | GAAP Earnings | Book Value | Distributions    | Notes                                |
| ---- | ------------- | ---------- | ---------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| 1986 | $28.6M        | $172.6M    | —                | First year; "Company O" earns $40.2M |
| 1987 | Growing       | Shrinking  | >Earnings        | Sainted Seven member; 57% ROE        |
| 1988 | Growing       | Shrinking  | >Earnings        | Sainted Seven Plus One; 67% ROE      |
| 1992 | $110M         | $116M      | >100% cumulative | 95% pre-tax ROE                      |
| 1994 | $79.3M        | $94M       | >100% cumulative | Would rank #1 Fortune 500 ROE        |

The critical feature: Scott Fetzer distributed **more than 100% of its earnings** to Berkshire while _growing_ earnings from $40M to $79M. The asset base _shrank_ from $172.6M to $94M. This is impossible under conventional accounting logic but perfectly explained by owner economics: capital-light franchises generate cash in excess of maintenance needs[^3].

### The 15-Year Result (1986-2000)

| Metric                                | Value            |
| ------------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Net purchase price                    | ~$230M           |
| Cumulative distributions to Berkshire | **$1.03B**       |
| Return on capital                     | 4.5x in 15 years |
| Peak after-tax ROE (1998)             | **86%**          |

**[1998 Letter]** Peak performance: $96.5M after-tax on $112M net worth — "one of the most undervalued companies in the country when we bought it in 1986."

**[2000 Letter]** Ralph Schey retired. Buffett: "As a manager, Ralph belongs in Berkshire's Hall of Fame." The $1.03B in distributions was reinvested into other Berkshire acquisitions, compounding further — the same "rabbits breeding" dynamic that See's Candies initiated[^4].

## Buffett's Retrospective

**[1994 Letter]** The richest single-company analysis in any Buffett letter. Scott Fetzer answered three questions:

1. **Can GAAP earnings mislead?** Yes. Reported earnings of $28.6M in 1986 were 71% of the true $40.2M owner earnings. The gap persisted for years.
2. **Can a well-managed business generate cash while growing?** Yes. >100% distribution while doubling earnings — because the business needed _less_ capital, not more, to grow.
3. **Does management matter — but only in a good business?** Absolutely. Schey's performance would have been impossible in a commodity business. In capital-light franchises, exceptional management compounds returns to extraordinary levels.

The compensation arrangement: "conceived in five minutes, never written up" — no lawyers, no compensation consultants. The model for rational incentive design.

## Lessons

1. **Owner earnings are the true measure of business value.** GAAP earnings systematically distorted Scott Fetzer's performance for 14 years. Without the owner-earnings lens, an investor would have undervalued the business by 30%+ annually. See [Owner Earnings](../financials/owner-earnings.md).
2. **Capital-light businesses generate distributable cash while growing.** The $1.03B distributed to Berkshire funded other acquisitions — the same compounding engine as [See's Candies](sees-candies-acquisition.md). Contrast with [BNSF](bnsf-acquisition.md), which requires $4B+ annually just to maintain position.
3. **Speed of acquisition reflects buyer advantage.** Three weeks from letter to contract — no bankers, no committees. Berkshire's clarity and certainty of close produced a lower price than competing bidders could offer.
4. **Management retention requires the right structure.** Schey stayed 14 years because Berkshire let him run his businesses autonomously with stable compensation. "Never a manager of a significant subsidiary voluntarily leaving" proved the model worked.
5. **Cash payment caps downside.** $315M in cash for Scott Fetzer produced $1.03B in distributions. Contrast with [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md): $433M in Berkshire stock for a business worth zero — with the stock escalating to $5.7B.
