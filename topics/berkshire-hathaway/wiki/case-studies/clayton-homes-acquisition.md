## Overview

In August 2003, Berkshire acquired Clayton Homes — America's preeminent builder and financer of manufactured homes — after Buffett read founder Jim Clayton's autobiography, evaluated his son Kevin, reviewed the public financials, and called with an offer. No investment bankers. No extended due diligence. The manufactured housing industry had been devastated by predatory securitization; Conseco (the largest player) was bankrupt. Clayton survived because it retained credit risk on its own loans — the opposite of the originate-and-sell model that would later cause the 2008 financial crisis.

Berkshire provided what no other parent could: funding at cost-plus-one-percentage-point. This single structural advantage destroyed every competitor's economics and built Clayton into **America's largest home builder** with **47% of manufactured housing** and a **$14B+ mortgage portfolio** — all while maintaining loss rates that vindicated blue-collar borrowers against the skeptics.

> "After 40 years, we've finally generated a little synergy at Berkshire." — 2004 Letter

## Buffett's Rationale

**[2003 Letter]** The acquisition story was pure Buffett: University of Tennessee business students gave him Jim Clayton's book. He read it, liked it, researched the family and the public financials, and picked up the phone. The thesis:

1. **Disciplined lending in a devastated industry.** Clayton retained 100% of the credit risk on its mortgages — "every tub on its own bottom." While competitors securitized loans and walked away from credit risk (producing catastrophic defaults), Clayton's skin-in-the-game model produced sound underwriting.
2. **Survivor advantage.** With Conseco bankrupt and other lenders fleeing, Clayton was one of the last operators standing. The industry was at 40-year lows — a classic Buffett setup of buying a quality business in a terrible industry environment.
3. **Management quality.** Kevin Clayton ran the business; Jim Clayton had built it from $10,000 in 1966. The culture of treating manufactured-home buyers with dignity — often lower-income families buying their first home — was both ethical and economically sound[^1].

## The Economics: Berkshire's Balance Sheet as Weapon

### The Financing Advantage

The manufactured housing industry collapsed because lenders who securitized loans had no incentive to maintain underwriting standards. When the securitization market froze, surviving builders couldn't finance their customers. Clayton could — because Berkshire lent to it at Berkshire's own borrowing cost plus one percentage point.

**[2004 Letter]** The Oakwood Homes acquisition (April 2004) made Clayton the industry's **largest producer and retailer**. Berkshire's financing: **$2B at start of 2004 → $7.35B** by January 2005. This wasn't a subsidy — it was a competitive weapon. Clayton earned satisfactory returns while offering better terms than any competitor could match.

> "Had Clayton remained independent, it would have had mediocre earnings as it struggled with financing." — 2004 Letter[^2]

## Outcome

### The Lending Record

**[2010 Letter]** The portfolio's performance silenced the skeptics: **200,804 mortgages** with an average FICO score of **648** and net loss rates of only **1.17-1.86%**. These borrowers — often unable to qualify for Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac mortgages — performed far better than many prime-quality mortgage pools during the 2008 crisis[^3].

**[2015 Letter]** Clayton retained **100% of credit risk** on originations. Average loan: **$59,942**; average monthly payment: **$522**. Regulatory scrutiny was intense: 65 examinations over two years by FTC, HUD, CFPB, and 25 state regulators. Total fines: **$38,200**. Customer refunds: **$704,678**. Foreclosure rate: 2.64%. The numbers demolished the narrative that manufactured-home lending was inherently predatory[^4].

### The Financing Discrimination Critique

**[2009 Letter]** Buffett devoted extensive space to a structural injustice: government-backed financing offered site-built homebuyers ~5¼% rates while manufactured-home buyers (often lower-income) paid ~9%. "This differential is unfair and borders on discriminatory." Clayton's own borrower performance — disciplined underwriting producing low defaults despite lower FICO scores — proved the rate gap was not justified by credit risk[^5].

### Crossing $1B Pre-Tax

**[2020 Letter]** Clayton crossed the **$1B pre-tax earnings** threshold. Jim Clayton's origin story retold: born in a house "literally without floors" on a Tennessee farm. The culture connection extended beyond Clayton itself — Kevin Clayton had referred the Haslam family to Berkshire, leading to the [Pilot Flying J](../entities/pilot-flying-j.md) acquisition[^6].

## Lessons

1. **The balance sheet can be a moat.** Clayton's competitive advantage isn't brand, technology, or patents — it's the ability to finance customers at rates competitors cannot match because they lack access to Berkshire's credit. This is the clearest case in the Berkshire portfolio of the parent company's financial strength directly creating subsidiary value. See [Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md).

2. **Skin in the game produces better outcomes.** Clayton's 100% risk retention — keeping every mortgage on its own books — created the incentive alignment that the securitization industry lacked. When you eat your own cooking, you cook more carefully. The 2008 financial crisis was fundamentally a failure of this principle at scale.

3. **Devastated industries produce the best acquisition opportunities.** Berkshire acquired Clayton when manufactured housing was at 40-year lows and the largest competitor was bankrupt. The same pattern appears in [Wells Fargo](wells-fargo-investment.md) (1990 bank panic), [General Re](general-re-turnaround.md) (post-crisis), and [BNSF](bnsf-acquisition.md) (recession pricing). The [Four Filters](../principles/four-filters.md) are easiest to satisfy when an excellent business is surrounded by industry despair.

4. **"Synergy" is real when the parent provides something irreplaceable.** Buffett's famous skepticism of synergy claims ("the acquiring company's stock often falls on the news") has one notable exception: when the parent provides funding that the subsidiary literally cannot obtain elsewhere. Clayton could not have built a $14B mortgage portfolio as an independent company. The Berkshire relationship is not a synergy claim — it's a structural fact.

5. **Blue-collar borrowers outperformed the narrative.** Clayton's data — 200,804 mortgages, FICO 648, loss rates of 1-2% — is an empirical rebuke to the assumption that low-income borrowers are inherently risky. The risk was never the borrowers; it was the lending model. When underwriting is disciplined and the lender retains risk, even sub-prime portfolios perform.
