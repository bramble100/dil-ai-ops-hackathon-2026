## Overview

In the summer of 1988, Berkshire began purchasing shares of The Coca-Cola Company. By yearend 1989, the position totaled **23,350,000 shares** at a cost of **$1,024M**. Eventually stabilizing at **400,000,000 shares** (post-splits) on **$1,299M** total cost, Coca-Cola became the longest-held core equity position and the single most powerful illustration of patient franchise investing in financial history.

By 2025: market value **$28.0B** on $1.3B cost (21.5x). Annual dividends: **$816M** — 63% of original cost basis, received every year and growing.

## Buffett's Rationale

### The 52-Year Confession

**[1989 Letter]** Buffett's most famous self-deprecating investment narrative:

> "Of a certainty, it was in 1936 that I started buying Cokes at the rate of six for 25 cents from Buffett & Son, the family grocery store, to sell around the neighborhood for 5 cents each. In this excursion into high-margin retailing, I duly observed the extraordinary consumer attractiveness and commercial possibilities of the product."

He watched these qualities for 52 years "as Coke blanketed the world" — while allocating capital to "street railway companies, windmill manufacturers, anthracite producers, textile businesses, trading-stamp issuers, and the like." Only in 1988 did his "brain finally establish contact with his eyes."

### Why 1988?

Two catalysts: (1) Roberto Goizueta's transformation since 1981 — refocusing the company and energizing international growth with a "truly rare blend of marketing and financial skills"; (2) the education from [See's Candies](sees-candies-acquisition.md) that taught Buffett to value consumer franchises over statistical bargains[^1].

**[2014 Letter]** "Through watching See's in action, I gained a business education about the value of powerful brands that opened my eyes to many other profitable investments — including Coca-Cola."

### The Franchise Thesis

Coke exemplifies every quality in the [business quality](../principles/business-quality.md) framework: global pricing power, minimal capital requirements relative to earnings, a self-reinforcing brand moat, and management that maximizes both product growth and shareholder returns. By 1994, Coke held approximately **44% of world soft drink consumption** — "leaving aside chewing gum, I know of no other significant businesses in which the leading company has long enjoyed such global power."[^2]

## Price Discipline

- **Cost:** $1,024M initially (1988-89), eventually $1,299M
- **Earning power at purchase:** Berkshire's share of Coke earnings was growing at double-digit rates
- **Permanent-hold conviction:** "When we own portions of outstanding businesses with outstanding managements, our favorite holding period is forever" — 1988 Letter

### Dividend Compounding: The Secret Sauce

**[2022 Letter]** The most powerful illustration of patience:

| Year | Annual Dividends | Yield on Cost |
| ---- | ---------------- | ------------- |
| 1994 | $75M             | 5.8%          |
| 2005 | $240M            | 18.5%         |
| 2010 | $376M            | 28.9%         |
| 2013 | $488M            | 37.6%         |
| 2022 | $704M            | 54.2%         |
| 2025 | $816M            | 62.8%         |

> "Growth occurred every year, just as certain as birthdays." — 2022 Letter

The counterfactual: $1.3B invested in a 30-year bond in 1994 would now represent **0.3% of net worth** delivering an unchanged $80M annually. Coke instead accounts for ~5% of net worth with growing dividends.

> "The weeds wither away in significance as the flowers bloom. Over time, it takes just a few winners to work wonders." — 2022 Letter

## Buffett's Retrospective

### The 1991 P/E Expansion Warning

**[1991 Letter]** Coke alone contributed ~$1B to Berkshire's $2.1B net worth increase — "a phenomenon not apt to be repeated." The gain came from P/E expansion, not earnings growth. By yearend, "our Coke stock alone is worth more than Berkshire's entire net worth was when we bought it." Buffett flagged this as unsustainable while holding the position forever.

### "The Inevitables" (1996)

**[1996 Letter]** Coke and Gillette formally classified as businesses whose "worldwide dominance is virtually certain to persist for the life of the investment." The 1896 evidence: 116,492 gallons of syrup in the first year; a century later, over 10 billion unit cases. Selling Coke to buy something else would be "like the Bulls trading Michael Jordan because he had a bad first quarter."

### The Rip Van Winkle Strategy (2023)

**[2023 Letter]** Coke and AmEx described as a 20+ year "Rip Van Winkle slumber" — Berkshire "did not buy or sell a share of either" in 2023. Both increased earnings and dividends again.

> "The lesson from Coke and AMEX? When you find a truly wonderful business, stick with it. Patience pays, and one wonderful business can offset the many mediocre decisions that are inevitable." — 2023 Letter

## Lessons

1. **The biggest mistakes are omissions, not commissions.** Buffett watched Coke for 52 years — the cost of analytical timidity dwarfs any active investment error. See [Four Filters](../principles/four-filters.md).
2. **Consumer franchises compound on their own.** Once identified, the investor's job is to do nothing. Activity destroys returns.
3. **Dividend growth is the ultimate proof of franchise value.** From $75M to $816M in annual dividends on an unchanged position — no trading, no rebalancing, no "optimization."
4. **P/E expansion is a bonus, not a strategy.** The 1991 lesson: even in a great business, market re-rating produces one-time gains that cannot recur. Business earnings growth is what endures.
5. **See's taught Buffett to buy Coke.** The intellectual pipeline from $25M See's Candies to $1.3B Coca-Cola is the most important cause-and-effect chain in Berkshire's history. See [See's Candies](sees-candies-acquisition.md).
