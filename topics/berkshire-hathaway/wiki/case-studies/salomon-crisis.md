## Overview

Berkshire invested **$700M in 9% convertible preferred stock** of [Salomon Inc](../entities/salomon-inc.md) in 1987 — drawn primarily by trust in CEO John Gutfreund. Four years later, a Treasury auction bidding scandal nearly destroyed the firm. Buffett served as **Interim Chairman for 10 months** (August 1991 – June 1992), navigating five simultaneous government investigations and rebuilding the firm's reputation from near-zero.

The financial outcome was modest: a small positive return on the $700M. The real significance lies elsewhere — in what the crisis taught about reputation, management integrity, and the hidden cost of distraction. The Salomon Congressional testimony became Berkshire's most enduring ethical touchstone, replayed at shareholder meetings for **25+ years**.

## Buffett's Rationale (1987)

### Trust in Gutfreund

**[1987 Letter]** The investment was unusual for Buffett — a $700M bet on an industry where he had "no special insights regarding direction or future profitability." The convertible preferred structure provided downside protection (9% coupon, ratable redemption beginning 1995), but the real thesis was trust in management:

> "Such service-above-self behavior is far from automatic in Wall Street." — 1987 Letter

Gutfreund's track record: (1) played a key role in [GEICO](../entities/geico.md)'s escape from near-bankruptcy in 1976, and (2) repeatedly steered clients away from unwise transactions even when it cost Salomon fees. Salomon also served as Berkshire's banker for a $250M debenture issuance[^1].

### The Preferred Structure

The 9% convertible preferred was designed for exactly this situation — industries with unpredictable economics. Conversion at $38/share after three years provided equity upside; if not converted, ratable redemption over five years beginning October 1995 returned principal. The coupon provided an acceptable floor. This became the template extended to Gillette, USAir, and Champion in 1989[^2].

## The Treasury Auction Scandal

### What Happened (1991)

Salomon traders submitted false bids in U.S. Treasury auctions to circumvent rules limiting any single bidder to 35% of an auction. Management learned of the violations in April 1991 but failed to report them promptly. When the scandal surfaced publicly in August, it triggered a cascade: regulatory investigations, client defections, and a near-collapse of the firm's funding.

**[1991 Letter]** On August 18, 1991, Buffett was elected Interim Chairman. The title deliberately included "Interim" — his "first love is Berkshire." He could serve only because Berkshire's operating managers were "so outstanding that I knew I could materially reduce the time I was spending at the company"[^3].

### The Rescue (1991-1992)

Buffett's approach combined transparency with operational pragmatism:

- **Immediate full cooperation** with all five government authorities: SEC, Federal Reserve, Treasury, U.S. Attorney, Justice Antitrust Division
- **Congressional testimony** establishing the ethical standard: "Lose money for the firm, and I will be understanding; lose a shred of reputation for the firm, and I will be ruthless"
- **Internal crisis team:** Deryck Maughan, Bob Denham, Don Howard, John Macfarlane — praised as "tireless, effective, supportive and selfless"
- **Legal resolution** by Ron Olson of Munger, Tolles & Olson, managing five simultaneous authorities to settlement

**[1992 Letter]** Buffett stepped down in June 1992 after 10 months. Results: Salomon ranked **#2 among 311 companies** in improving its reputation (Fortune survey). Salomon Brothers posted **record pre-tax earnings — 34% above previous high**. The survival and recovery were complete[^4].

## Financial Outcome

| Event                     | Date     | Amount                                      |
| ------------------------- | -------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Initial investment        | 1987     | $700M (9% convertible preferred)            |
| Yearend 1987 valuation    | Dec 1987 | $686M (98% of par)                          |
| Yearend 1992 valuation    | Dec 1992 | $756M                                       |
| First partial redemption  | 1995     | −$140M                                      |
| Travelers merger/exchange | 1996     | $500M exchangeable notes → Travelers common |
| Final outcome             | 1997     | Modest positive return                      |

The financial arithmetic was adequate but unimpressive — roughly break-even to a small gain after accounting for the 9% coupon income over the holding period.

## Buffett's Retrospective

### "Negative Overall" (1996)

**[1996 Letter]** Buffett's blunt assessment: **"My overall experience — negative overall."** The investment "consumed disproportionate Buffett attention relative to its economic contribution to Berkshire." The Travelers merger ended the direct exposure, but the opportunity cost — of management time, mental bandwidth, and reputational risk — far exceeded any financial return[^5].

**[1995 Letter]** More nuanced: "Considerably increased my appreciation for the difficulty of being both combative and fair."

### The Convertible Preferred Scorecard (1995-1997)

By 1997, all four convertible preferreds were profitable, but with wildly different outcomes:

| Investment                                         | Outcome                                                   |
| -------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| [Gillette](../case-studies/gillette-investment.md) | Best performer — preferred converted to equity, huge gain |
| Champion                                           | At cost + 19%                                             |
| Salomon                                            | Modest positive — redeemed/exchanged                      |
| USAir                                              | Recovered to par after writing down to 60%                |

The lesson: convertible preferreds protect capital, but they cannot overcome a weak or unpredictable business. [Filter (2)](../principles/four-filters.md) — favorable long-term prospects — cannot be bypassed through structural downside protection[^6].

### The Enduring Legacy (2025)

**[2025 Letter]** 25+ years later, the Salomon Congressional testimony is still played at every shareholder meeting. Abel's first CEO letter lists integrity as foundational value #2, explicitly requiring that it be "an active quality that must be earned, re-earned, and maintained daily." The Salomon experience transformed a crisis quote into an institutional operating principle[^7].

## Lessons

1. **Reputation is the ultimate asset — and the hardest to rebuild.** The Treasury scandal took Salomon from market leader to near-death in weeks. Recovery required the personal intervention of Warren Buffett and 10 months of crisis management.
2. **Management time has an opportunity cost.** The $700M produced a small positive return, but the 10 months Buffett spent at Salomon — time not spent on Berkshire — represents the real cost. This is why Buffett later emphasized decentralized management and autonomous operating subsidiaries.
3. **Structural protections don't fix bad businesses.** The convertible preferred provided downside protection, but it couldn't generate the returns that a true [franchise business](../principles/business-quality.md) produces. The instrument was sound; the investment thesis (trust in an unpredictable industry) was weak.
4. **Full transparency is the only crisis strategy.** Buffett's immediate cooperation with five government authorities — rather than legalistic delay — is what saved the firm. The 10-month turnaround (from scandal to record earnings) validates radical transparency.
5. **Character crises can generate unexpected deal flow.** Bob Mundheim, Salomon's general counsel during the crisis, later cold-called Buffett about the [Benjamin Moore](../entities/benjamin-moore.md) acquisition — relationships forged under pressure create lasting trust networks.
