## Overview

In 1999, Berkshire acquired MidAmerican Energy Holdings Company for approximately **$2B**, gaining ~76% economic ownership of a regulated Iowa utility. Due to the Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935 (PUHCA), Berkshire's voting power was constrained to less than 10%. Walter Scott Jr. introduced the deal; David Sokol became CEO; [Greg Abel](../entities/greg-abel.md) would later rise through MidAmerican to become Buffett's successor as Berkshire CEO.

Over 25 years, MidAmerican was renamed Berkshire Hathaway Energy (BHE) and grew from a regional utility into a national energy infrastructure company generating **$8.4B in operating cash flows** (2025), spanning regulated utilities in 11 states, ~8% of US natural gas transmission, the UK's third-largest electricity distributor, and America's leading renewable energy investor. It never paid a dividend — retaining **$28B+ in earnings** to fund expansion. By 2021, BHE earned a **record $4B** — up **30-fold** from $122M in 2000.

Then came the wildfire crisis.

## Buffett's Rationale

**[1999-2000 Letters]** Three attractions:

1. **Capital absorption.** Berkshire's growing cash pile needed large-scale deployment. Regulated utilities can absorb billions at predictable, if unspectacular, returns. "You can't expect to earn outsized profits in regulated utilities, but the industry offers owners the opportunity to deploy large sums at fair returns."
2. **Regulatory moat.** Competitors cannot enter without regulatory approval — an effective franchise. Rate-of-return regulation provides predictable economics even through recessions.
3. **Management pipeline.** David Sokol and Greg Abel were "huge assets for Berkshire." The deal also brought Walter Scott Jr. into Berkshire's inner circle[^1].

## The Growth Arc: From $2B to Four Giants

### Phase 1: Utility + Pipelines (1999-2004)

**[2002 Letter]** The pipeline acquisitions transformed MidAmerican from a regional utility into national infrastructure: **Kern River Gas Transmission** ($1.2B expansion) and **Northern Natural Gas** (16,600 miles, rescued from the Enron/Dynegy collapse for ~$1B). After expansion, MidAmerican would carry **~8% of all US natural gas**[^2].

**[2003 Letter]** Pre-tax earnings reached **$1.076B**. UK operations served 3.7M customers. The efficiency story was dramatic: since denationalization, UK workforce fell from 6,800 to 2,539 while delivering more electricity to the same customers[^3].

**[2004 Letter]** The zinc recovery write-off (**$579M**) taught a capital-allocation lesson: "If ten independent variables need to break favorably for a successful result, and each has a 90% probability of success, the likelihood of having a winner is only 35%." The corollary: "Stay with simple propositions"[^4].

### Phase 2: Expansion + Renewables (2005-2014)

**[2005 Letter]** Two transformative events: **PUHCA repealed** (Berkshire finally gained voting control, reaching 83.4%) and the **PacifiCorp acquisition** added 1.6M customers in six Western states. Buffett hinted at more: "Note the plural"[^5].

**[2013 Letter]** **NV Energy acquired for $5.6B** — serving 88% of Nevada's population. MidAmerican accounted for **7% of US wind generation** with a planned **$15B renewables portfolio**. A critical structural advantage crystallized: MidAmerican retained **all of its earnings** — "more dollars of earnings — by far — than any other American electric utility." The 100% retention policy funded expansion without Berkshire contributing additional capital or issuing debt[^6].

**[2014 Letter]** Renamed **Berkshire Hathaway Energy**. Operating earnings reached **$3.1B**. AltaLink (Alberta transmission, $3B Canadian) broadened geographic diversity. BHE now served customers in **eleven states** — "no utility company stretches further"[^7].

### Phase 3: Renewable Dominance (2015-2022)

**[2015 Letter]** Iowa rates remained unchanged for **16 years** at 6.8¢/KWH — while the competitor charged 9.5¢ and the national average stood at 10.4¢. Wind generation reached **47% of Iowa's total output**. BHE's formula was deceptively simple: retain all earnings → invest in renewables → lower rates → attract tech customers → earn regulatory goodwill[^8].

**[2017 Letter]** Buffett dubbed Iowa "the Saudi Arabia of wind energy." Rate freeze extended to **2029** — 30 years without an increase. Total renewable investment: **$16B**[^9].

**[2021 Letter]** BHE earned a **record $4B** — up 30-fold from 2000. Named one of the "Four Giants." "BHE has the management, the experience, the capital and the appetite for the huge power projects that our country needs"[^10].

### Phase 4: Wildfire Crisis (2023-2025)

**[2023 Letter]** The most serious operational crisis in BHE's history. Earnings dropped to **$2.3B** (from $4.3B). The regulatory compact — utilities invest in infrastructure in exchange for a fixed return — was "broken in a few states." Wildfire frequency and intensity were increasing, with potential liability extending years into the future.

Buffett's confession was unsparing: "I did not anticipate or even consider the adverse developments in regulatory returns and, along with Berkshire's two partners at BHE, I made a costly mistake in not doing so." The ominous conclusion: some utilities "might no longer attract the savings of American citizens and will be forced to adopt the public-power model"[^11].

**[2024 Letter]** BHE earnings recovered to **$3.7B**. Berkshire increased ownership to **100%** at ~$3.9B — a signal of confidence despite the ongoing wildfire resolution[^12].

**[2025 Letter]** Operating cash flows reached **$8.4B**. Customer rates averaged **24% below national levels**. Abel — now CEO of Berkshire — reported PacifiCorp had "taken a leadership role" in wildfire resolution while maintaining "principled opposition to unwarranted liability." AI/data center demand was creating new investment opportunities, but Abel insisted infrastructure "must be paid for by those customers and reflect the risks tied to step-changes in long-term demand"[^13].

## Lessons

1. **Retained earnings are the ultimate competitive weapon in regulated industries.** BHE's 100% retention policy — unique among major US utilities — funded $15B+ in renewables, pipeline expansion, and acquisitions without external capital. Competitors paying dividends couldn't match this reinvestment rate. The lesson applies beyond utilities: any business that can retain and redeploy all earnings at acceptable returns has a structural advantage. See [Capital Allocation](../principles/capital-allocation.md).

2. **Regulatory moats are real — until they're not.** For two decades, rate regulation provided predictable returns. The wildfire crisis showed that the "social compact" between utilities and regulators can break down when climate risks create open-ended liabilities. Buffett's confession — "I did not anticipate or even consider" — is a warning that even well-understood moats have failure modes.

3. **Capital-intensive businesses can compound when funded by float and retained earnings.** BHE and [BNSF](../entities/bnsf.md) together represent Berkshire's "capital-intensive pivot" — a departure from the capital-light franchise businesses (See's, Coca-Cola) that built early Berkshire. The economics work because Berkshire's insurance float and retained earnings provide effectively free capital. See [Insurance Float](../financials/float.md).

4. **Management pipelines are acquisitions' hidden dividend.** The MidAmerican deal brought Greg Abel into Berkshire's orbit. Twenty-five years later, he's CEO of the entire company. No financial model at the time of purchase could have valued this outcome — yet it may prove the most consequential result of the $2B investment.

5. **"Note the plural" is the mindset of a permanent capital allocator.** Buffett's hint in 2005 signaled that utilities were not a one-time deployment but a permanent capital-allocation category. PacifiCorp, NV Energy, AltaLink, and the renewable buildout followed. The insight: regulated infrastructure absorbs capital at scale in a way that acquisition-hungry conglomerates rarely can.
