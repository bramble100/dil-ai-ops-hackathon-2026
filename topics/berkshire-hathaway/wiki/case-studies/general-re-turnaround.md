## Overview

Berkshire's December 1998 acquisition of [General Re](../entities/general-re.md) for approximately **$22B in stock** — the largest acquisition in company history — proved an immediate disaster. Reserves were systematically inadequate; underwriting discipline had collapsed during the soft market. The 1999 underwriting loss reached **$1.4B**. Then 2001 exploded: **$4.3B loss** ($800M in unrecognized prior-year reserve deficiency plus $2B–$2.5B in 9/11 exposure).

Joe Brandon's appointment as CEO in September 2001, combined with Tad Montross's relentless enforcement of the four commandments of [underwriting discipline](../principles/underwriting-discipline.md), transformed the business. By 2003, General Re posted its first **$145M profit**. By 2013, Buffett declared it **"now a gem."**

## Buffett's Rationale

The structural case was sound: public reinsurers, pressured by Wall Street to smooth earnings, systematically avoid volatile policies — exactly the business Berkshire was positioned to accept. General Re was the established market leader with 82% ownership of Cologne Re (Germany's largest reinsurer), creating a global platform. The $22B all-stock payment reflected Berkshire's 48.3% book value gain in 1998 — an implicit admission that shares were overvalued, making them attractive acquisition currency[^1].

## Outcome: Crisis and Turnaround

### The Collapse (1999-2002)

| Year | Underwriting Result | Key Driver                                    |
| ---- | ------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| 1999 | −$1,400M            | Reserve inadequacy; soft-market pricing       |
| 2000 | −$1,400M            | Continued mispricing; Cologne Re difficulties |
| 2001 | −$4,300M            | 9/11 ($2–2.5B) + $800M prior-year development |
| 2002 | −$1,390M            | $1.31B additional reserve charge              |

**[1999 Letter]** Buffett's explicit apology: "I have not kept the promise I made at the time of the acquisition."

### The Fix (2001-2003)

Joe Brandon became CEO in September 2001. He and Tad Montross enforced the **four commandments** that became Berkshire's insurance doctrine:

1. **Understand all exposures** that might cause losses — including tail events and correlation risks
2. **Conservatively assess** loss probability using worst-case data, not averages
3. **Set premiums** that deliver profit after all costs
4. **Walk away** if appropriate premium cannot be obtained

> "Many insurers pass the first three tests and flunk the fourth." — 2004 Letter

The Gen Re Securities derivatives operation (losing $173M/year) was closed. Aggregation risks were eliminated. Pricing was rebuilt from the ground up. By 2003: **$145M underwriting profit** — a $5.5B swing from the 2001 trough[^2].

### The Gem (2006-2017)

**[2006 Letter]** $526M underwriting profit — best year under Berkshire ownership. Montross maintained four-commandment discipline through his 39-year career. When he retired in 2016, Kara Raiguel succeeded him.

**[2013 Letter]** Buffett's verdict: **"General Re is now a gem."** The float — initially a $23.7B cost center — had become essentially free, delivering consistent underwriting profit year after year.

## Buffett's Retrospective

The General Re turnaround proved that a structurally sound franchise can survive even catastrophic mismanagement — if the fix is operational discipline rather than financial engineering. The underlying business (global reinsurance with Cologne Re platform) was always valuable. The error was not the business but the pricing, reserving, and risk management.

**Contrast with [Dexter Shoe](dexter-shoe-investment.md):** Both were stock-deal acquisitions. But General Re's franchise was structurally sound — fixable through discipline. Dexter's competitive position vanished as manufacturing moved offshore — unfixable regardless of management.

**Contrast with [textile operations](textile-operations.md):** Textiles had no franchise to rescue. General Re had a global platform, brand, and distribution that simply needed proper underwriting. The lesson: distinguish "bad business" (exit) from "good business badly run" (fix).

## Lessons

1. **Even Buffett makes acquisition mistakes.** The $22B General Re purchase produced $8.5B in cumulative underwriting losses (1999-2002) before turning profitable. Size of check doesn't prevent error.
2. **In insurance, the fix is always the same: underwriting discipline.** The four commandments work at every scale — from [National Indemnity](../entities/national-indemnity.md)'s ebb-and-flow model to General Re's global reinsurance platform.
3. **Walk away from bad pricing.** Commandment #4 is the hardest and most important. Most insurers can analyze risk; few can refuse profitable-looking volume when competitors are writing it.
4. **Stock-deal acquisitions compound the damage of mistakes.** The $22B in Berkshire shares issued for General Re would be worth far more today. Unlike [See's Candies](sees-candies-acquisition.md) ($25M cash), the real cost of General Re escalates with every year of Berkshire's compounding.
5. **Management changes can transform outcomes.** Brandon and Montross turned a $4.3B annual loss into consistent profitability within two years — proof that operational talent, properly empowered, can rescue even catastrophic situations.
